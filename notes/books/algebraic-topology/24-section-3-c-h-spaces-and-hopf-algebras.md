# 3.C H-Spaces and Hopf Algebras

Of the three axioms for a group, it would seem that the least subtle is the existence of an identity element. However, we shall see in this section that when topology is added to the picture, the identity axiom becomes much more potent. To give a name to the objects we will be considering, define a space \( X \) to be an H-space,’H’ standing for ’Hopf’, if there is a continuous multiplication map \( \mu  : X \times  X \rightarrow  X \) and an ’identity’ element \( e \in  X \) such that the two maps \( X \rightarrow  X \) given by \( x \mapsto  \mu \left( {x, e}\right) \) and \( x \mapsto  \mu \left( {e, x}\right) \) are homotopic to the identity through maps \( \left( {X, e}\right)  \rightarrow  \left( {X, e}\right) \) . In particular, this implies that \( \mu \left( {e, e}\right)  = e \) .

In terms of generality, this definition represents something of a middle ground. One could weaken the definition by dropping the condition that the homotopies preserve the basepoint \( e \) , or one could strengthen it by requiring that \( e \) be a strict identity, without any homotopies. An exercise at the end of the section is to show the three possible definitions are equivalent if \( X \) is a CW complex. An advantage of allowing homotopies in the definition is that a space homotopy equivalent in the basepointed sense to an H-space is again an H-space. Imposing basepoint conditions is fairly standard in homotopy theory, and is usually not a serious restriction.

The most classical examples of H-spaces are topological groups, spaces \( X \) with a group structure such that both the multiplication map \( X \times  X \rightarrow  X \) and the inversion map \( X \rightarrow  X, x \mapsto  {x}^{-1} \) , are continuous. For example, the group \( G{L}_{n}\left( \mathbb{R}\right) \) of invertible \( n \times  n \) matrices with real entries is a topological group when topologized as a subspace of the \( {n}^{2} \) -dimensional vector space \( {M}_{n}\left( \mathbb{R}\right) \) of all \( n \times  n \) matrices over \( \mathbb{R} \) . It is an open subspace since the invertible matrices are those with nonzero determinant, and the determinant function \( {M}_{n}\left( \mathbb{R}\right)  \rightarrow  \mathbb{R} \) is continuous. Matrix multiplication is certainly continuous, being defined by simple algebraic formulas, and it is not hard to see that matrix inversion is also continuous if one thinks for example of the classical adjoint formula for the inverse matrix.

Likewise \( G{L}_{n}\left( \mathbb{C}\right) \) is a topological group, as is the quaternionic analog \( G{L}_{n}\left( \mathbb{H}\right) \) , though in the latter case one needs a somewhat different justification since determinants of quaternionic matrices do not have the good properties one would like. Since these groups \( G{L}_{n} \) over \( \mathbb{R},\mathbb{C} \) , and \( \mathbb{H} \) are open subsets of Euclidean spaces, they are examples of Lie groups, which can be defined as topological groups which are also manifolds. The \( G{L}_{n} \) groups are noncompact, being open subsets of Euclidean spaces, but they have the homotopy types of compact Lie groups \( O\left( n\right) , U\left( n\right) \) , and \( {Sp}\left( n\right) \) . This is explained in §3.D for \( G{L}_{n}\left( \mathbb{R}\right) \) , and the other two cases are similar.

Among the simplest H-spaces from a topological viewpoint are the unit spheres \( {S}^{1} \) in \( \mathbb{C},{S}^{3} \) in the quaternions \( \mathbb{H} \) , and \( {S}^{7} \) in the octonions \( \mathbb{O} \) . These are H-spaces since the multiplications in these division algebras are continuous, being defined by polynomial formulas, and are norm-preserving, \( \left| {ab}\right|  = \left| a\right| \left| b\right| \) , hence restrict to multiplications on the unit spheres, and the identity element of the division algebra lies in the unit sphere in each case. Both \( {S}^{1} \) and \( {S}^{3} \) are Lie groups since the multiplications in \( \mathbb{C} \) and \( \mathbb{H} \) are associative and inverses exist since \( a\bar{a} = {\left| a\right| }^{2} = 1 \) if \( \left| a\right|  = 1 \) . However, \( {S}^{7} \) is not a group since multiplication of octonions is not associative. Of course \( {S}^{0} = \{  \pm  1\} \) is also a topological group, trivially. A famous theorem of J. F. Adams asserts that \( {S}^{0},{S}^{1},{S}^{3} \) , and \( {S}^{7} \) are the only spheres that are H-spaces; see §4.B for a fuller discussion.

Let us describe now some associative H-spaces where inverses fail to exist. Multiplication of polynomials provides an \( \mathrm{H} \) -space structure on \( {\mathbb{{CP}}}^{\infty } \) in the following way. A nonzero polynomial \( {a}_{0} + {a}_{1}z + \cdots  + {a}_{n}{z}^{n} \) with coefficients \( {a}_{i} \in  \mathbb{C} \) corresponds to a point \( \left( {{a}_{0},\cdots ,{a}_{n},0,\cdots }\right)  \in  {\mathbb{C}}^{\infty } - \{ 0\} \) . Multiplication of two such polynomials determines a multiplication \( {\mathbb{C}}^{\infty } - \{ 0\}  \times  {\mathbb{C}}^{\infty } - \{ 0\}  \rightarrow  {\mathbb{C}}^{\infty } - \{ 0\} \) which is associative, commutative, and has an identity element \( \left( {1,0,\cdots }\right) \) . Since \( \mathbb{C} \) is commutative we can factor out by scalar multiplication by nonzero constants and get an induced product \( {\mathbb{{CP}}}^{\infty } \times  {\mathbb{{CP}}}^{\infty } \rightarrow  {\mathbb{{CP}}}^{\infty } \) with the same properties. Thus \( {\mathbb{{CP}}}^{\infty } \) is an associative, commutative H-space with a strict identity. Instead of factoring out by all nonzero scalars, we could factor out only by scalars of the form \( \rho {e}^{{2\pi ik}/q} \) with \( \rho \) an arbitrary positive real, \( k \) an arbitrary integer, and \( q \) a fixed positive integer. The quotient of \( {\mathbb{C}}^{\infty } - \{ 0\} \) under this identification, an infinite-dimensional lens space \( {L}^{\infty } \) with \( {\pi }_{1}\left( {L}^{\infty }\right)  \approx  {\mathbb{Z}}_{q} \) , is therefore also an associative, commutative H-space. This includes \( {\mathbb{{RP}}}^{\infty } \) in particular.

The spaces \( J\left( X\right) \) defined in \( \text{ § }{3.2} \) are also \( \mathrm{H} \) -spaces, with the multiplication given by \( \left( {{x}_{1},\cdots ,{x}_{m}}\right) \left( {{y}_{1},\cdots ,{y}_{n}}\right)  = \left( {{x}_{1},\cdots ,{x}_{m},{y}_{1},\cdots ,{y}_{n}}\right) \) , which is associative and has an identity element \( \left( e\right) \) where \( e \) is the basepoint of \( X \) . One could describe \( J\left( X\right) \) as the free associative H-space generated by \( X \) . There is also a commutative ana- \( \log \) of \( J\left( X\right) \) called the infinite symmetric product \( {SP}\left( X\right) \) defined in the following way. Let \( S{P}_{n}\left( X\right) \) be the quotient space of the \( n \) -fold product \( {X}^{n} \) obtained by identifying all \( n \) -tuples \( \left( {{x}_{1},\cdots ,{x}_{n}}\right) \) that differ only by a permutation of their coordinates. The inclusion \( {X}^{n} \hookrightarrow  {X}^{n + 1},\left( {{x}_{1},\cdots ,{x}_{n}}\right)  \mapsto  \left( {{x}_{1},\cdots ,{x}_{n}, e}\right) \) induces an inclusion \( S{P}_{n}\left( X\right)  \hookrightarrow  S{P}_{n + 1}\left( X\right) \) , and \( {SP}\left( X\right) \) is defined to be the union of this increasing sequence of \( S{P}_{n}\left( X\right) \) ’s, with the weak topology. Alternatively, \( {SP}\left( X\right) \) is the quotient of \( J\left( X\right) \) obtained by identifying points that differ only by permutation of coordinates. The H-space structure on \( J\left( X\right) \) induces an H-space structure on \( {SP}\left( X\right) \) which is commutative in addition to being associative and having a strict identity. The spaces \( {SP}\left( X\right) \) are studied in more detail in §4.K.

The goal of this section will be to describe the extra structure which the multiplication in an H-space gives to its homology and cohomology. This is of particular interest since many of the most important spaces in algebraic topology turn out to be H-spaces.

## Hopf Algebras

Let us look at cohomology first. Choosing a commutative ring \( R \) as coefficient ring, we can regard the cohomology ring \( {H}^{ * }\left( {X;R}\right) \) of a space \( X \) as an algebra over \( R \) rather than merely a ring. Suppose \( X \) is an H-space satisfying two conditions:

(1) \( X \) is path-connected, hence \( {H}^{0}\left( {X;R}\right)  \approx  R \) .

(2) \( {H}^{n}\left( {X;R}\right) \) is a finitely generated free \( R \) -module for each \( n \) , so the cross product \( {H}^{ * }\left( {X;R}\right) { \otimes  }_{R}{H}^{ * }\left( {X;R}\right)  \rightarrow  {H}^{ * }\left( {X \times  X;R}\right) \) is an isomorphism.

The multiplication \( \mu  : X \times  X \rightarrow  X \) induces a map \( {\mu }^{ * } : {H}^{ * }\left( {X;R}\right)  \rightarrow  {H}^{ * }\left( {X \times  X;R}\right) \) , and when we combine this with the cross product isomorphism in (2) we get a map

\[
{H}^{ * }\left( {X;R}\right) \overset{\Delta }{ \rightarrow  }{H}^{ * }\left( {X;R}\right) { \otimes  }_{R}{H}^{ * }\left( {X;R}\right)
\]

which is an algebra homomorphism since both \( {\mu }^{ * } \) and the cross product isomorphism are algebra homomorphisms. The key property of \( \Delta \) turns out to be that for any \( \alpha  \in  {H}^{n}\left( {X;R}\right) , n > 0 \) , we have

\[
\Delta \left( \alpha \right)  = \alpha  \otimes  1 + 1 \otimes  \alpha  + \mathop{\sum }\limits_{i}{\alpha }_{i}^{\prime } \otimes  {\alpha }_{i}^{\prime \prime }\;\text{ where }\left| {\alpha }_{i}^{\prime }\right|  > 0\text{ and }\left| {\alpha }_{i}^{\prime \prime }\right|  > 0
\]

To verify this, let \( i : X \rightarrow  X \times  X \) be the inclusion \( x \mapsto  \left( {x, e}\right) \) for \( e \) the identity element of \( X \) , and consider the commutative diagram

![bo_d7dtv0s91nqc73eq8nv0_291_334_1157_1131_191_0.jpg](images/bo_d7dtv0s91nqc73eq8nv0_291_334_1157_1131_191_0.jpg)

The map \( P \) is defined by commutativity, and by looking at the lower right triangle we see that \( P\left( {\alpha  \otimes  1}\right)  = \alpha \) and \( P\left( {\alpha  \otimes  \beta }\right)  = 0 \) if \( \left| \beta \right|  > 0 \) . The H-space property says that \( {\mu i} \simeq  \mathbb{1} \) , so \( {P\Delta } = \mathbb{1} \) . This implies that the component of \( \Delta \left( \alpha \right) \) in \( {H}^{n}\left( {X;R}\right) { \otimes  }_{R}{H}^{0}\left( {X;R}\right) \) is \( \alpha  \otimes  1 \) . A similar argument shows the component in \( {H}^{0}\left( {X;R}\right) { \otimes  }_{R}{H}^{n}\left( {X;R}\right) \) is \( 1 \otimes  \alpha \) .

We can summarize this situation by saying that \( {H}^{ * }\left( {X;R}\right) \) is a Hopf algebra, that is, a graded algebra \( A = {\bigoplus }_{n \geq  0}{A}^{n} \) over a commutative base ring \( R \) , satisfying the following two conditions:

(1) There is an identity element \( 1 \in  {A}^{0} \) such that the map \( R \rightarrow  {A}^{0}, r \mapsto  r \cdot  1 \) , is an isomorphism. In this case one says \( A \) is connected.

(2) There is a diagonal or coproduct \( \Delta  : A \rightarrow  A \otimes  A \) , a homomorphism of graded algebras satisfying \( \Delta \left( \alpha \right)  = \alpha  \otimes  1 + 1 \otimes  \alpha  + \mathop{\sum }\limits_{i}{\alpha }_{i}^{\prime } \otimes  {\alpha }_{i}^{\prime \prime } \) where \( \left| {\alpha }_{i}^{\prime }\right|  > 0 \) and \( \left| {\alpha }_{i}^{\prime \prime }\right|  > 0 \) , for all \( \alpha \) with \( \left| \alpha \right|  > 0 \) .

Here and in what follows we take \( \otimes \) to mean \( { \otimes  }_{R} \) . The multiplication in \( A \otimes  A \) is given by the standard formula \( \left( {\alpha  \otimes  \beta }\right) \left( {\gamma  \otimes  \delta }\right)  = {\left( -1\right) }^{\left| \beta \right| \left| \gamma \right| }\left( {{\alpha \gamma } \otimes  {\beta \delta }}\right) \) . For a general Hopf algebra the multiplication is not assumed to be either associative or commutative (in the graded sense), though in the example of \( {H}^{ * }\left( {X;R}\right) \) for \( X \) an H-space the algebra structure is of course associative and commutative.

Example 3C.1. One of the simplest Hopf algebras is a polynomial ring \( R\left\lbrack  \alpha \right\rbrack \) . The coproduct \( \Delta \left( \alpha \right) \) must equal \( \alpha  \otimes  1 + 1 \otimes  \alpha \) since the only elements of \( R\left\lbrack  \alpha \right\rbrack \) of lower dimension than \( \alpha \) are the elements of \( R \) in dimension zero, so the terms \( {\alpha }_{i}^{\prime } \) and \( {\alpha }_{i}^{\prime \prime } \) in the coproduct formula \( \Delta \left( \alpha \right)  = \alpha  \otimes  1 + 1 \otimes  \alpha  + \mathop{\sum }\limits_{i}{\alpha }_{i}^{\prime } \otimes  {\alpha }_{i}^{\prime \prime } \) must be zero. The requirement that \( \Delta \) be an algebra homomorphism then determines \( \Delta \) completely. To describe \( \Delta \) explicitly we distinguish two cases. If the dimension of \( \alpha \) is even or if \( 2 = 0 \) in \( R \) , then the multiplication in \( R\left\lbrack  \alpha \right\rbrack   \otimes  R\left\lbrack  \alpha \right\rbrack \) is strictly commutative and \( \Delta \left( {\alpha }^{n}\right)  = \; {\left( \alpha  \otimes  1 + 1 \otimes  \alpha \right) }^{n} = \mathop{\sum }\limits_{i}\left( \begin{matrix} n \\  i \end{matrix}\right) {\alpha }^{i} \otimes  {\alpha }^{n - i} \) . In the opposite case that \( \alpha \) is odd-dimensional, then \( \Delta \left( {\alpha }^{2}\right)  = {\left( \alpha  \otimes  1 + 1 \otimes  \alpha \right) }^{2} = {\alpha }^{2} \otimes  1 + 1 \otimes  {\alpha }^{2} \) since \( \left( {\alpha  \otimes  1}\right) \left( {1 \otimes  \alpha }\right)  = \alpha  \otimes  \alpha \) and \( \left( {1 \otimes  \alpha }\right) \left( {\alpha  \otimes  1}\right)  =  - \alpha  \otimes  \alpha \) if \( \alpha \) has odd dimension. Thus if we set \( \beta  = {\alpha }^{2} \) , then \( \beta \) is even-dimensional and we have \( \Delta \left( {\alpha }^{2n}\right)  = \Delta \left( {\beta }^{n}\right)  = {\left( \beta  \otimes  1 + 1 \otimes  \beta \right) }^{n} = \mathop{\sum }\limits_{i}\left( \begin{matrix} n \\  i \end{matrix}\right) {\beta }^{i} \otimes  {\beta }^{n - i} \) and \( \Delta \left( {\alpha }^{{2n} + 1}\right)  = \Delta \left( {\alpha {\beta }^{n}}\right)  = \Delta \left( \alpha \right) \Delta \left( {\beta }^{n}\right)  = \mathop{\sum }\limits_{i}\left( \begin{matrix} n \\  i \end{matrix}\right) \alpha {\beta }^{i} \otimes  {\beta }^{n - i} + \mathop{\sum }\limits_{i}\left( \begin{matrix} n \\  i \end{matrix}\right) {\beta }^{i} \otimes  \alpha {\beta }^{n - i} \) .

Example 3C.2. The exterior algebra \( {\Lambda }_{R}\left\lbrack  \alpha \right\rbrack \) on an odd-dimensional generator \( \alpha \) is a Hopf algebra, with \( \Delta \left( \alpha \right)  = \alpha  \otimes  1 + 1 \otimes  \alpha \) . To verify that \( \Delta \) is an algebra homomorphism we must check that \( \Delta \left( {\alpha }^{2}\right)  = \Delta {\left( \alpha \right) }^{2} \) , or in other words, since \( {\alpha }^{2} = 0 \) , we need to see that \( \Delta {\left( \alpha \right) }^{2} = 0 \) . As in the preceding example we have \( \Delta {\left( \alpha \right) }^{2} = {\left( \alpha  \otimes  1 + 1 \otimes  \alpha \right) }^{2} = \; {\alpha }^{2} \otimes  1 + 1 \otimes  {\alpha }^{2} \) , so \( \Delta {\left( \alpha \right) }^{2} \) is indeed 0 . Note that if \( \alpha \) were even-dimensional we would instead have \( \Delta {\left( \alpha \right) }^{2} = {\alpha }^{2} \otimes  1 + {2\alpha } \otimes  \alpha  + 1 \otimes  {\alpha }^{2} \) , which would be 0 in \( {\Lambda }_{R}\left\lbrack  \alpha \right\rbrack   \otimes  {\Lambda }_{R}\left\lbrack  \alpha \right\rbrack \) only if \( 2 = 0 \) in \( R \) .

An element \( \alpha \) of a Hopf algebra is called primitive if \( \Delta \left( \alpha \right)  = \alpha  \otimes  1 + 1 \otimes  \alpha \) . As the preceding examples illustrate, if a Hopf algebra is generated as an algebra by primitive elements, then the coproduct \( \Delta \) is uniquely determined by the product. This happens in a number of interesting special cases, but certainly not in general, as we shall see.

The existence of the coproduct in a Hopf algebra turns out to restrict the multiplicative structure considerably. Here is an important example illustrating this:

Example 3C.3. Suppose that the truncated polynomial algebra \( F\left\lbrack  \alpha \right\rbrack  /\left( {\alpha }^{n}\right) \) over a field \( F \) is a Hopf algebra. Then \( \alpha \) is primitive, just as it is in \( F\left\lbrack  \alpha \right\rbrack \) , so if we assume either that \( \alpha \) is even-dimensional or that \( F \) has characteristic 2, then the relation \( {\alpha }^{n} = 0 \) yields an equation

\[
0 = \Delta \left( {\alpha }^{n}\right)  = {\alpha }^{n} \otimes  1 + 1 \otimes  {\alpha }^{n} + \mathop{\sum }\limits_{{0 < i < n}}\left( \begin{matrix} n \\  i \end{matrix}\right) {\alpha }^{i} \otimes  {\alpha }^{n - i} = \mathop{\sum }\limits_{{0 < i < n}}\left( \begin{matrix} n \\  i \end{matrix}\right) {\alpha }^{i} \otimes  {\alpha }^{n - i}
\]

which implies that \( \left( \begin{matrix} n \\  i \end{matrix}\right)  = 0 \) in \( F \) for each \( i \) in the range \( 0 < i < n \) . This is impossible if \( F \) has characteristic 0, and if the characteristic of \( F \) is \( p > 0 \) then it happens only when \( n \) is a power of \( p \) . For \( p = 2 \) this was shown in the proof of Theorem 3.21, and the argument given there works just as well for odd primes. Conversely, it is easy to check that if \( F \) has characteristic \( p \) then \( F\left\lbrack  \alpha \right\rbrack  /\left( {\alpha }^{{p}^{i}}\right) \) is a Hopf algebra, assuming still that \( \alpha \) is even-dimensional if \( p \) is odd.

The characteristic 0 case of this result implies that \( {\mathbb{{CP}}}^{n} \) is not an H-space for finite \( n \) , in contrast with \( {\mathbb{{CP}}}^{\infty } \) which is an H-space as we saw earlier. Similarly, taking \( F = {\mathbb{Z}}_{2} \) , we deduce that \( {\mathbb{{RP}}}^{n} \) can be an H-space only if \( n + 1 \) is a power of 2 . Indeed, \( {\mathbb{{RP}}}^{1} = {S}^{1}/ \pm  1,{\mathbb{{RP}}}^{3} = {S}^{3}/ \pm  1 \) , and \( {\mathbb{{RP}}}^{7} = {S}^{7}/ \pm  1 \) have quotient H-space structures from \( {S}^{1},{S}^{3} \) and \( {S}^{7} \) since -1 commutes with all elements of \( {S}^{1},{S}^{3} \) , or \( {S}^{7} \) . However, these are the only cases when \( {\mathbb{{RP}}}^{n} \) is an H-space since, by an exercise at the end of this section, the universal cover of an H-space is an H-space, and \( {S}^{1},{S}^{3} \) , and \( {S}^{7} \) are the only spheres that are H-spaces, by the theorem of Adams mentioned earlier.

The tensor product \( A \otimes  B \) of Hopf algebras \( A \) and \( B \) is again a Hopf algebra, with coproduct the composition \( A \otimes  B\xrightarrow[]{\Delta  \otimes  \Delta }\left( {A \otimes  A}\right)  \otimes  \left( {B \otimes  B}\right)  \rightarrow  \left( {A \otimes  B}\right)  \otimes  \left( {A \otimes  B}\right) \) where the second map interchanging the middle two factors includes the usual sign in graded commutativity. Thus the preceding examples yield many other Hopf algebras, tensor products of polynomial, truncated polynomial, and exterior algebras on any number of generators. The following theorem of Hopf is a partial converse:

Theorem 3C.4. If \( A \) is a commutative, associative Hopf algebra over a field \( F \) of characteristic 0, and \( {A}^{n} \) is finite-dimensional over \( F \) for each \( n \) , then \( A \) is isomorphic as an algebra to the tensor product of an exterior algebra on odd-dimensional generators and a polynomial algebra on even-dimensional generators.

There is an analogous theorem of Borel when \( F \) is a finite field of characteristic \( p \) . In this case \( A \) is again isomorphic to a tensor product of single-generator Hopf algebras, of one of the following types:

- \( F\left\lbrack  \alpha \right\rbrack \) , with \( \alpha \) even-dimensional if \( p \neq  2 \) .

- \( {\Lambda }_{F}\left\lbrack  \alpha \right\rbrack \) with \( \alpha \) odd-dimensional.

- \( F\left\lbrack  \alpha \right\rbrack  /\left( {\alpha }^{{p}^{i}}\right) \) , with \( \alpha \) even-dimensional if \( p \neq  2 \) .

For a proof see [Borel 1953] or [Kane 1988].

Proof of 3C.4: Since \( {A}^{n} \) is finitely generated over \( F \) for each \( n \) , we may choose algebra generators \( {x}_{1},{x}_{2},\cdots \) for \( A \) with \( \left| {x}_{i}\right|  \leq  \left| {x}_{i + 1}\right| \) for all \( i \) . Let \( {A}_{n} \) be the subalgebra generated by \( {x}_{1},\cdots ,{x}_{n} \) . This is a Hopf subalgebra of \( A \) , that is, \( \Delta \left( {A}_{n}\right)  \subset  {A}_{n} \otimes  {A}_{n} \) , since \( \Delta \left( {x}_{i}\right) \) involves only \( {x}_{i} \) and terms of smaller dimension. We may assume \( {x}_{n} \) does not lie in \( {A}_{n - 1} \) . Since \( A \) is associative and commutative, there is a natural surjection \( {A}_{n - 1} \otimes  F\left\lbrack  {x}_{n}\right\rbrack   \rightarrow  {A}_{n} \) if \( \left| {x}_{n}\right| \) is even, or \( {A}_{n - 1} \otimes  {\Lambda }_{F}\left\lbrack  {x}_{n}\right\rbrack   \rightarrow  {A}_{n} \) if \( \left| {x}_{n}\right| \) is odd. By induction on \( n \) it will suffice to prove these surjections are injective. Thus in the two cases we must rule out nontrivial relations \( \mathop{\sum }\limits_{i}{\alpha }_{i}{x}_{n}^{i} = 0 \) and \( {\alpha }_{0} + {\alpha }_{1}{x}_{n} = 0 \) , respectively, with coefficients \( {\alpha }_{i} \in  {A}_{n - 1} \) .

Let \( I \) be the ideal in \( {A}_{n} \) generated by \( {x}_{n}^{2} \) and the positive-dimensional elements of \( {A}_{n - 1} \) , so \( I \) consists of the polynomials \( \mathop{\sum }\limits_{i}{\alpha }_{i}{x}_{n}^{i} \) with coefficients \( {\alpha }_{i} \in  {A}_{n - 1} \) , the first two coefficients \( {\alpha }_{0} \) and \( {\alpha }_{1} \) having trivial components in \( {A}^{0} \) . Note that \( {x}_{n} \notin  I \) since elements of \( I \) having dimension \( \left| {x}_{n}\right| \) must lie in \( {A}_{n - 1} \) . Consider the composition

\[
{A}_{n}\overset{\Delta }{ \rightarrow  }{A}_{n} \otimes  {A}_{n}\overset{q}{ \rightarrow  }{A}_{n} \otimes  \left( {{A}_{n}/I}\right)
\]

with \( q \) the natural quotient map. By the definition of \( I \) , this composition \( {q\Delta } \) sends \( \alpha  \in  {A}_{n - 1} \) to \( \alpha  \otimes  1 \) and \( {x}_{n} \) to \( {x}_{n} \otimes  1 + 1 \otimes  {\bar{x}}_{n} \) where \( {\bar{x}}_{n} \) is the image of \( {x}_{n} \) in \( {A}_{n}/I \) . In case \( \left| {x}_{n}\right| \) is even, applying \( {q\Delta } \) to a nontrivial relation \( \mathop{\sum }\limits_{i}{\alpha }_{i}{x}_{n}^{i} = 0 \) gives

\[
0 = \mathop{\sum }\limits_{i}\left( {{\alpha }_{i} \otimes  1}\right) {\left( {x}_{n} \otimes  1 + 1 \otimes  {\bar{x}}_{n}\right) }^{i} = \left( {\mathop{\sum }\limits_{i}{\alpha }_{i}{x}_{n}^{i}}\right)  \otimes  1 + \mathop{\sum }\limits_{i}i{\alpha }_{i}{x}_{n}^{i - 1} \otimes  {\bar{x}}_{n}
\]

Since \( \mathop{\sum }\limits_{i}{\alpha }_{i}{x}_{n}^{i} = 0 \) , this implies that \( \mathop{\sum }\limits_{i}i{\alpha }_{i}{x}_{n}^{i - 1} \otimes  {\bar{x}}_{n} \) is zero in the tensor product \( {A}_{n} \otimes  \left( {{A}_{n}/I}\right) \) , hence \( \mathop{\sum }\limits_{i}i{\alpha }_{i}{x}_{n}^{i - 1} = 0 \) since \( {x}_{n} \notin  I \) implies \( {\bar{x}}_{n} \neq  0 \) . The relation \( \mathop{\sum }\limits_{i}i{\alpha }_{i}{x}_{n}^{i - 1} = 0 \) has lower degree than the original relation, and is not the trivial relation since \( F \) has characteristic \( 0,{\alpha }_{i} \neq  0 \) implying \( i{\alpha }_{i} \neq  0 \) if \( i > 0 \) . Since we could assume the original relation had minimum degree, we have reached a contradiction.

The case \( \left| {x}_{n}\right| \) odd is similar. Applying \( {q\Delta } \) to a relation \( {\alpha }_{0} + {\alpha }_{1}{x}_{n} = 0 \) gives \( 0 = {\alpha }_{0} \otimes  1 + \left( {{\alpha }_{1} \otimes  1}\right) \left( {{x}_{n} \otimes  1 + 1 \otimes  {\bar{x}}_{n}}\right)  = \left( {{\alpha }_{0} + {\alpha }_{1}{x}_{n}}\right)  \otimes  1 + {\alpha }_{1} \otimes  {\bar{x}}_{n} \) . Since \( {\alpha }_{0} + {\alpha }_{1}{x}_{n} = 0 \) , we get \( {\alpha }_{1} \otimes  {\bar{x}}_{n} = 0 \) , which implies \( {\alpha }_{1} = 0 \) and hence \( {\alpha }_{0} = 0 \) .

The structure of Hopf algebras over \( \mathbb{Z} \) is much more complicated than over a field. Here is an example that is still fairly simple.

Example 3C.5: Divided Polynomial Algebras. We showed in Proposition 3.22 that the H-space \( J\left( {S}^{n}\right) \) for \( n \) even has \( {H}^{ * }\left( {J\left( {S}^{n}\right) ;\mathbb{Z}}\right) \) a divided polynomial algebra, the algebra \( {\Gamma }_{\mathbb{Z}}\left\lbrack  \alpha \right\rbrack \) with additive generators \( {\alpha }_{i} \) in dimension \( {ni} \) and multiplication given by \( {\alpha }_{1}^{k} = \; k!{\alpha }_{k} \) , hence \( {\alpha }_{i}{\alpha }_{j} = \left( \begin{matrix} i + j \\  i \end{matrix}\right) {\alpha }_{i + j} \) . The coproduct in \( {\Gamma }_{\mathbb{Z}}\left\lbrack  \alpha \right\rbrack \) is uniquely determined by the multiplicative structure since \( \Delta \left( {\alpha }_{1}^{k}\right)  = {\left( {\alpha }_{1} \otimes  1 + 1 \otimes  {\alpha }_{1}\right) }^{k} = \mathop{\sum }\limits_{i}\left( \begin{matrix} k \\  i \end{matrix}\right) {\alpha }_{1}^{i} \otimes  {\alpha }_{1}^{k - i} \) , which implies that \( \Delta \left( {{\alpha }_{1}^{k}/k!}\right)  = \mathop{\sum }\limits_{i}\left( {{\alpha }_{1}^{i}/i!}\right)  \otimes  \left( {{\alpha }_{1}^{k - i}/\left( {k - i}\right) !}\right) \) , that is, \( \Delta \left( {\alpha }_{k}\right)  = \mathop{\sum }\limits_{i}{\alpha }_{i} \otimes  {\alpha }_{k - i} \) . So in this case the coproduct has a simpler description than the product.

It is interesting to see what happens to the divided polynomial algebra \( {\Gamma }_{\mathbb{Z}}\left\lbrack  \alpha \right\rbrack \) when we change to field coefficients. Clearly \( {\Gamma }_{\mathbb{Q}}\left\lbrack  \alpha \right\rbrack \) is the same as \( \mathbb{Q}\left\lbrack  \alpha \right\rbrack \) . In contrast with this, \( {\Gamma }_{{\mathbb{Z}}_{p}}\left\lbrack  \alpha \right\rbrack \) , with multiplication defined by \( {\alpha }_{i}{\alpha }_{j} = \left( \begin{matrix} i + j \\  i \end{matrix}\right) {\alpha }_{i + j} \) , happens to be isomorphic as an algebra to the infinite tensor product \( {\bigotimes }_{i \geq  0}{\mathbb{Z}}_{p}\left\lbrack  {\alpha }_{{p}^{i}}\right\rbrack  /\left( {\alpha }_{{p}^{i}}^{p}\right) \) , as we will show in a moment. However, as Hopf algebras these two objects are different since \( {\alpha }_{{p}^{i}} \) is primitive in \( {\bigotimes }_{i \geq  0}{\mathbb{Z}}_{p}\left\lbrack  {\alpha }_{{p}^{i}}\right\rbrack  /\left( {\alpha }_{{p}^{i}}^{p}\right) \) but not in \( {\Gamma }_{{\mathbb{Z}}_{p}}\left\lbrack  \alpha \right\rbrack \) when \( i > 0 \) , since the coproduct in \( {\Gamma }_{{\mathbb{Z}}_{p}}\left\lbrack  \alpha \right\rbrack \) is given by \( \Delta \left( {\alpha }_{k}\right)  = \mathop{\sum }\limits_{i}{\alpha }_{i} \otimes  {\alpha }_{k - i} \) .

Now let us show that there is an algebra isomorphism

\[
{\Gamma }_{{\mathbb{Z}}_{p}}\left\lbrack  \alpha \right\rbrack   \approx  {\bigotimes }_{i \geq  0}{\mathbb{Z}}_{p}\left\lbrack  {\alpha }_{{p}^{i}}\right\rbrack  /\left( {\alpha }_{{p}^{i}}^{p}\right)
\]

Since \( {\Gamma }_{{\mathbb{Z}}_{p}}\left\lbrack  \alpha \right\rbrack   = {\Gamma }_{\mathbb{Z}}\left\lbrack  \alpha \right\rbrack   \otimes  {\mathbb{Z}}_{p} \) , this is equivalent to:

(*) The element \( {\alpha }_{1}^{{n}_{0}}{\alpha }_{p}^{{n}_{1}}\cdots {\alpha }_{{p}^{k}}^{{n}_{k}} \) in \( {\Gamma }_{\mathbb{Z}}\left\lbrack  \alpha \right\rbrack \) is divisible by \( p \) iff \( {n}_{i} \geq  p \) for some \( i \) .

The product \( {\alpha }_{1}^{{n}_{0}}{\alpha }_{p}^{{n}_{1}}\cdots {\alpha }_{{p}^{k}}^{{n}_{k}} \) equals \( m{\alpha }_{n} \) for \( n = {n}_{0} + {n}_{1}p + \cdots  + {n}_{k}{p}^{k} \) and some integer \( m \) . The question is whether \( p \) divides \( m \) . We will show:

\( \left( {* * }\right) \;{\alpha }_{n}{\alpha }_{{p}^{k}} \) is divisible by \( p \) iff \( {n}_{k} = p - 1 \) , assuming that \( {n}_{i} < p \) for each \( i \) . This implies \( \left( *\right) \) by an inductive argument in which we build up the product in \( \left( *\right) \) by repeated multiplication on the right by terms \( {\alpha }_{{p}^{i}} \) .

To prove \( \left( {* * }\right) \) we recall that \( {\alpha }_{n}{\alpha }_{{p}^{k}} = \left( \begin{matrix} n + {p}^{k} \\  n \end{matrix}\right) {\alpha }_{n + {p}^{k}} \) . The mod \( p \) value of this binomial coefficient can be computed using Lemma 3C.6 below. Assuming that \( {n}_{i} < p \) for each \( i \) and that \( {n}_{k} + 1 < p \) , the \( p \) -adic representations of \( n + {p}^{k} \) and \( n \) differ only in the coefficient of \( {p}^{k} \) , so mod \( p \) we have \( \left( \begin{matrix} n + {p}^{k} \\  n \end{matrix}\right)  = \left( \begin{matrix} {n}_{k} + 1 \\  {n}_{k} \end{matrix}\right)  = {n}_{k} + 1 \) . This conclusion also holds if \( {n}_{k} + 1 = p \) , when the \( p \) -adic representations of \( n + {p}^{k} \) and \( n \) differ also in the coefficient of \( {p}^{k + 1} \) . The statement \( \left( {* * }\right) \) then follows.

Lemma 3C.6. If \( p \) is a prime, then \( \left( \begin{array}{l} n \\  k \end{array}\right)  \equiv  \mathop{\prod }\limits_{i}\left( \begin{array}{l} {n}_{i} \\  {k}_{i} \end{array}\right) {\;\operatorname{mod}\;p} \) where \( n = \mathop{\sum }\limits_{i}{n}_{i}{p}^{i} \) and \( k = \mathop{\sum }\limits_{i}{k}_{i}{p}^{i} \) with \( 0 \leq  {n}_{i} < p \) and \( 0 \leq  {k}_{i} < p \) are the \( p \) -adic representations of \( n \) and \( k \) .

Here the convention is that \( \left( \begin{array}{l} n \\  k \end{array}\right)  = 0 \) if \( n < k \) , and \( \left( \begin{array}{l} n \\  0 \end{array}\right)  = 1 \) for all \( n \geq  0 \) .

Proof: In \( {\mathbb{Z}}_{p}\left\lbrack  x\right\rbrack \) there is an identity \( {\left( 1 + x\right) }^{p} = 1 + {x}^{p} \) since \( p \) clearly divides \( \left( \begin{array}{l} p \\  k \end{array}\right)  = \; p!/k!\left( {p - k}\right) ! \) for \( 0 < k < p \) . By induction it follows that \( {\left( 1 + x\right) }^{{p}^{i}} = 1 + {x}^{{p}^{i}} \) . Hence if \( n = \mathop{\sum }\limits_{i}{n}_{i}{p}^{i} \) is the \( p \) -adic representation of \( n \) then:

\[
{\left( 1 + x\right) }^{n} = {\left( 1 + x\right) }^{{n}_{0}}{\left( 1 + {x}^{p}\right) }^{{n}_{1}}{\left( 1 + {x}^{{p}^{2}}\right) }^{{n}_{2}}\cdots
\]

\[
= \left\lbrack  {1 + \left( \begin{matrix} {n}_{0} \\  1 \end{matrix}\right) \chi  + \left( \begin{matrix} {n}_{0} \\  2 \end{matrix}\right) {x}^{2} + \cdots  + \left( \begin{matrix} {n}_{0} \\  p - 1 \end{matrix}\right) {\chi }^{p - 1}}\right\rbrack
\]

\[
\times  \left\lbrack  {1 + \left( \begin{matrix} {n}_{1} \\  1 \end{matrix}\right) {x}^{p} + \left( \begin{matrix} {n}_{1} \\  2 \end{matrix}\right) {x}^{2p} + \cdots  + \left( \begin{matrix} {n}_{1} \\  p - 1 \end{matrix}\right) {x}^{\left( {p - 1}\right) p}}\right\rbrack
\]

\[
\times  \left\lbrack  {1 + \left( \begin{matrix} {n}_{2} \\  1 \end{matrix}\right) {x}^{{p}^{2}} + \left( \begin{matrix} {n}_{2} \\  2 \end{matrix}\right) {x}^{2{p}^{2}} + \cdots  + \left( \begin{matrix} {n}_{2} \\  p - 1 \end{matrix}\right) {x}^{\left( {p - 1}\right) {p}^{2}}}\right\rbrack   \times  \cdots
\]

When this is multiplied out, one sees that no terms combine, and the coefficient of \( {x}^{k} \) is just \( \mathop{\prod }\limits_{i}\left( \begin{array}{l} {n}_{i} \\  {k}_{i} \end{array}\right) \) where \( k = \mathop{\sum }\limits_{i}{k}_{i}{p}^{i} \) is the \( p \) -adic representation of \( k \) .

## Pontryagin Product

Another special feature of H-spaces is that their homology groups have a product operation, called the Pontryagin product. For an H-space \( X \) with multiplication \( \mu  : X \times  X \rightarrow  X \) , this is the composition

\[
{H}_{ * }\left( {X;R}\right)  \otimes  {H}_{ * }\left( {X;R}\right) \overset{ \times  }{ \rightarrow  }{H}_{ * }\left( {X \times  X;R}\right) \overset{{\mu }_{ * }}{ \rightarrow  }{H}_{ * }\left( {X;R}\right)
\]

where the first map is the cross product defined in §3.B. Thus the Pontryagin product consists of bilinear maps \( {H}_{i}\left( {X;R}\right)  \times  {H}_{j}\left( {X;R}\right)  \rightarrow  {H}_{i + j}\left( {X;R}\right) \) . Unlike cup product, the Pontryagin product is not in general associative unless the multiplication \( \mu \) is associative or at least associative up to homotopy, in the sense that the maps \( X \times  X \times  X \rightarrow  X \) , \( \left( {x, y, z}\right)  \mapsto  \mu \left( {x,\mu \left( {y, z}\right) }\right) \) and \( \left( {x, y, z}\right)  \mapsto  \mu \left( {\mu \left( {x, y}\right) , z}\right) \) are homotopic. Fortunately most H-spaces one meets in practice satisfy this associativity property. Nor is the Pontryagin product generally commutative, even in the graded sense, unless \( \mu \) is commutative or homotopy-commutative, which is relatively rare for H-spaces. We will give examples shortly where the Pontryagin product is not commutative.

In case \( X \) is a CW complex and \( \mu \) is a cellular map the Pontryagin product can be computed using cellular homology via the cellular chain map

\[
{C}_{i}\left( {X;R}\right)  \times  {C}_{j}\left( {X;R}\right) \overset{ \times  }{ \rightarrow  }{C}_{i + j}\left( {X \times  X;R}\right) \overset{{\mu }_{ * }}{ \rightarrow  }{C}_{i + j}\left( {X;R}\right)
\]

where the cross product map sends generators corresponding to cells \( {e}^{i} \) and \( {e}^{j} \) to the generator corresponding to the product cell \( {e}^{i} \times  {e}^{j} \) , and then \( {\mu }_{ * } \) is applied to this product cell.

Example 3C.7. Let us compute the Pontryagin product for \( J\left( {S}^{n}\right) \) . Here there is one cell \( {e}^{in} \) for each \( i \geq  0 \) , and \( \mu \) takes the product cell \( {e}^{in} \times  {e}^{jn} \) homeomorphically onto the cell \( {e}^{\left( {i + j}\right) n} \) . This means that \( {H}_{ * }\left( {J\left( {S}^{n}\right) ;\mathbb{Z}}\right) \) is simply the polynomial ring \( \mathbb{Z}\left\lbrack  x\right\rbrack \) on an \( n \) -dimensional generator \( x \) . This holds for \( n \) odd as well as for \( n \) even, so the Pontryagin product need not satisfy the same general commutativity relation as cup product. In this example the Pontryagin product structure is simpler than the cup product structure, though for some H-spaces it is the other way round. In applications it is often convenient to have the choice of which product structure to use.

This calculation immediately generalizes to \( J\left( X\right) \) where \( X \) is any connected CW complex whose cellular boundary maps are all trivial. The cellular boundary maps in the product \( {X}^{m} \) of \( m \) copies of \( X \) are then trivial by induction on \( m \) using Proposition 3B.1, and therefore the cellular boundary maps in \( J\left( X\right) \) are all trivial since the quotient map \( {X}^{m} \rightarrow  {J}_{m}\left( X\right) \) is cellular and each cell of \( {J}_{m}\left( X\right) \) is the homeomorphic image of a cell of \( {X}^{m} \) . Thus \( {H}_{ * }\left( {J\left( X\right) ;\mathbb{Z}}\right) \) is free with additive basis the products \( {e}^{{n}_{1}} \times  \cdots  \times  {e}^{{n}_{k}} \) of positive-dimensional cells of \( X \) , and the multiplicative structure is that of polynomials in noncommuting variables corresponding to the positive-dimensional cells of \( X \) .

Another way to describe \( {H}_{ * }\left( {J\left( X\right) ;\mathbb{Z}}\right) \) in this example is as the tensor algebra \( T{\widetilde{H}}_{ * }\left( {X;\mathbb{Z}}\right) \) , where for a graded \( R \) -module \( M \) that is trivial in dimension zero, like the reduced homology of a path-connected space, the tensor algebra \( {TM} \) is the direct sum of the \( n \) -fold tensor products of \( M \) with itself for all \( n \geq  1 \) , together with a copy of \( R \) in dimension zero, with the obvious multiplication coming from tensor product and scalar multiplication.

Generalizing the preceding example, we have:

Proposition 3C.8. If \( X \) is a connected CW complex with \( {H}_{ * }\left( {X;R}\right) \) a free \( R \) -module, then \( {H}_{ * }\left( {J\left( X\right) ;R}\right) \) is isomorphic to the tensor algebra \( T{\widetilde{H}}_{ * }\left( {X;R}\right) \) .

This can be paraphrased as saying that the homology of the free H-space generated by a space with free homology is the free algebra generated by the homology of the space.

Proof: With coefficients in \( R \) , let \( \varphi  : T{\widetilde{H}}_{ * }\left( X\right)  \rightarrow  {H}_{ * }\left( {J\left( X\right) }\right) \) be the homomorphism whose restriction to the \( n \) -fold tensor product \( {\widetilde{H}}_{ * }{\left( X\right) }^{\otimes n} \) is the composition

\[
{\widetilde{H}}_{ * }{\left( X\right) }^{\otimes n} \hookrightarrow  {H}_{ * }{\left( X\right) }^{\otimes n}\overset{ \times  }{ \rightarrow  }{H}_{ * }\left( {X}^{n}\right)  \rightarrow  {H}_{ * }\left( {{J}_{n}\left( X\right) }\right)  \rightarrow  {H}_{ * }\left( {J\left( X\right) }\right)
\]

where the next-to-last map is induced by the quotient map \( {X}^{n} \rightarrow  {J}_{n}\left( X\right) \) . It is clear that \( \varphi \) is a ring homomorphism since the product in \( J\left( X\right) \) is induced from the natural map \( {X}^{m} \times  {X}^{n} \rightarrow  {X}^{m + n} \) . To show that \( \varphi \) is an isomorphism, consider the following commutative diagram of short exact sequences:

\[
0 \rightarrow  {T}_{n - 1}{\widetilde{H}}_{ * }\left( X\right)  \rightarrow  {T}_{n}{\widetilde{H}}_{ * }\left( X\right)  \rightarrow  {\widetilde{H}}_{ * }{\left( X\right) }^{\otimes n} \rightarrow  0
\]

\[
\varphi  \downarrow  \;\varphi  \downarrow  \; \times   \downarrow   \approx
\]

\[
0 \rightarrow  {H}_{ * }\left( {{J}_{n - 1}\left( X\right) }\right)  \rightarrow  {H}_{ * }\left( {{J}_{n}\left( X\right) }\right)  \rightarrow  {\widetilde{H}}_{ * }\left( {X}^{\land n}\right)  \rightarrow  0
\]

In the upper row, \( {T}_{m}{\widetilde{H}}_{ * }\left( X\right) \) denotes the direct sum of the products \( {\widetilde{H}}_{ * }{\left( X\right) }^{\otimes k} \) for \( k \leq  m \) , so this row is exact. The second row is the homology exact sequence for the pair \( \left( {{J}_{n}\left( X\right) ,{J}_{n - 1}\left( X\right) }\right) \) , with quotient \( {J}_{n}\left( X\right) /{J}_{n - 1}\left( X\right) \) the \( n \) -fold smash product \( {X}^{\land n} \) . This long exact sequence breaks up into short exact sequences as indicated, by commutativity of the right-hand square and the fact that the right-hand vertical map is an isomorphism by the Künneth formula, using the hypothesis that \( {H}_{ * }\left( X\right) \) is free over the given coefficient ring. By induction on \( n \) and the five-lemma we deduce from the diagram that \( \varphi  : {T}_{n}{\widetilde{H}}_{ * }\left( X\right)  \rightarrow  {H}_{ * }\left( {{J}_{n}\left( X\right) }\right) \) is an isomorphism for all \( n \) . Letting \( n \) go to \( \infty \) , this implies that \( \varphi  : T{\widetilde{H}}_{ * }\left( X\right)  \rightarrow  {H}_{ * }\left( {J\left( X\right) }\right) \) is an isomorphism since in any given dimension \( {T}_{n}{\widetilde{H}}_{ * }\left( X\right) \) is independent of \( n \) when \( n \) is sufficiently large, and the same is true of \( {H}_{ * }\left( {{J}_{n}\left( X\right) }\right) \) by the second row of the diagram.

## Dual Hopf Algebras

There is a close connection between the Pontryagin product in homology and the Hopf algebra structure on cohomology. Suppose that \( X \) is an H-space such that, with coefficients in a field \( R \) , the vector spaces \( {H}_{n}\left( {X;R}\right) \) are finite-dimensional for all \( n \) . Alternatively, we could take \( R = \mathbb{Z} \) and assume \( {H}_{n}\left( {X;\mathbb{Z}}\right) \) is finitely generated and free for all \( n \) . In either case we have \( {H}^{n}\left( {X;R}\right)  = {\operatorname{Hom}}_{R}\left( {{H}_{n}\left( {X;R}\right) , R}\right) \) , and as a consequence the Pontryagin product \( {H}_{ * }\left( {X;R}\right)  \otimes  {H}_{ * }\left( {X;R}\right)  \rightarrow  {H}_{ * }\left( {X;R}\right) \) and the coproduct \( \Delta  : {H}^{ * }\left( {X;R}\right)  \rightarrow  {H}^{ * }\left( {X;R}\right)  \otimes  {H}^{ * }\left( {X;R}\right) \) are dual to each other, both being induced by the H-space product \( \mu  : X \times  X \rightarrow  X \) . Therefore the coproduct in cohomology determines the Pontryagin product in homology, and vice versa. Specifically, the component \( {\Delta }_{ij} : {H}^{i + j}\left( {X;R}\right)  \rightarrow  {H}^{i}\left( {X;R}\right)  \otimes  {H}^{j}\left( {X;R}\right) \) of \( \Delta \) is dual to the product \( {H}_{i}\left( {X;R}\right)  \otimes  {H}_{j}\left( {X;R}\right)  \rightarrow  {H}_{i + j}\left( {X;R}\right) . \)

Example 3C.9. Consider \( J\left( {S}^{n}\right) \) with \( n \) even, so \( {H}^{ * }\left( {J\left( {S}^{n}\right) ;\mathbb{Z}}\right) \) is the divided polynomial algebra \( {\Gamma }_{\mathbb{Z}}\left\lbrack  \alpha \right\rbrack \) . In Example 3C.5 we derived the coproduct formula \( \Delta \left( {\alpha }_{k}\right)  = \; \mathop{\sum }\limits_{i}{\alpha }_{i} \otimes  {\alpha }_{k - i} \) . Thus \( {\Delta }_{ij} \) takes \( {\alpha }_{i + j} \) to \( {\alpha }_{i} \otimes  {\alpha }_{j} \) , so if \( {x}_{i} \) is the generator of \( {H}_{in}\left( {J\left( {S}^{n}\right) ;\mathbb{Z}}\right) \) dual to \( {\alpha }_{i} \) , then \( {x}_{i}{x}_{j} = {x}_{i + j} \) . This says that \( {H}_{ * }\left( {J\left( {S}^{n}\right) ;\mathbb{Z}}\right) \) is the polynomial ring \( \mathbb{Z}\left\lbrack  x\right\rbrack \) . We showed this in Example 3C.7 using the cell structure of \( J\left( {S}^{n}\right) \) , but the present proof deduces it purely algebraically from the cup product structure.

Now we wish to show that the relation between \( {H}^{ * }\left( {X;R}\right) \) and \( {H}_{ * }\left( {X;R}\right) \) is perfectly symmetric: They are dual Hopf algebras. This is a purely algebraic fact:

Proposition 3C.10. Let \( A \) be a Hopf algebra over \( R \) that is a finitely generated free \( R \) -module in each dimension. Then the product \( \pi  : A \otimes  A \rightarrow  A \) and coproduct \( \Delta  : A \rightarrow  A \otimes  A \) have duals \( {\pi }^{ * } : {A}^{ * } \rightarrow  {A}^{ * } \otimes  {A}^{ * } \) and \( {\Delta }^{ * } : {A}^{ * } \otimes  {A}^{ * } \rightarrow  {A}^{ * } \) that give \( {A}^{ * } \) the structure of a Hopf algebra.

Proof: This will be apparent if we reinterpret the Hopf algebra structure on \( A \) formally as a pair of graded \( R \) -module homomorphisms \( \pi  : A \otimes  A \rightarrow  A \) and \( \Delta  : A \rightarrow  A \otimes  A \) together with an element \( 1 \in  {A}^{0} \) satisfying:

(1) The two compositions \( A\overset{{i}_{\ell }}{ \rightarrow  }A \otimes  A\overset{\pi }{ \rightarrow  }A \) and \( A\overset{{i}_{r}}{ \rightarrow  }A \otimes  A\overset{\pi }{ \rightarrow  }A \) are the identity, where \( {i}_{\ell }\left( a\right)  = a \otimes  1 \) and \( {i}_{r}\left( a\right)  = 1 \otimes  a \) . This says that 1 is a two-sided identity for the multiplication in \( A \) .

(2) The two compositions \( A\overset{\Delta }{ \rightarrow  }A \otimes  A\overset{{p}_{\ell }}{ \rightarrow  }A \) and \( A\overset{\Delta }{ \rightarrow  }A \otimes  A\overset{{p}_{r}}{ \rightarrow  }A \) are the identity, where \( {p}_{\ell }\left( {a \otimes  1}\right)  = a = {p}_{r}\left( {1 \otimes  a}\right) ,{p}_{\ell }\left( {a \otimes  b}\right)  = 0 \) if \( \left| b\right|  > 0 \) , and \( {p}_{r}\left( {a \otimes  b}\right)  = 0 \) if \( \left| a\right|  > 0 \) . This is just the coproduct formula \( \Delta \left( a\right)  = a \otimes  1 + 1 \otimes  a + \mathop{\sum }\limits_{i}{a}_{i}^{\prime } \otimes  {a}_{i}^{\prime \prime } \) .

(3) The diagram at the right commutes, with \( \tau \left( {a \otimes  b \otimes  c \otimes  d}\right)  = {\left( -1\right) }^{\left| b\right| \left| c\right| }a \otimes  c \otimes  b \otimes  d. \) This is the condition that \( \Delta \) is an algebra homomorphism since if we follow an element \( a \otimes  b \) across the top of the diagram we get \( \Delta \left( {ab}\right) \) , while the lower route gives first \( \Delta \left( a\right)  \otimes  \Delta \left( b\right)  = \left( {\mathop{\sum }\limits_{i}{a}_{i}^{\prime } \otimes  {a}_{i}^{\prime \prime }}\right)  \otimes  \left( {\mathop{\sum }\limits_{j}{b}_{j}^{\prime } \otimes  {b}_{j}^{\prime \prime }}\right) \) , then after applying \( \tau \) and \( \pi  \otimes  \pi \) this becomes \( \mathop{\sum }\limits_{{i, j}}{\left( -1\right) }^{\left| {a}_{i}^{\prime \prime }\right| \left| {b}_{j}^{\prime }\right| }{a}_{i}^{\prime }{b}_{j}^{\prime } \otimes  {a}_{i}^{\prime \prime }{b}_{j}^{\prime \prime } = \left( {\mathop{\sum }\limits_{i}{a}_{i}^{\prime } \otimes  {a}_{i}^{\prime \prime }}\right) \left( {\mathop{\sum }\limits_{j}{b}_{j}^{\prime } \otimes  {b}_{j}^{\prime \prime }}\right) \) , which is \( \Delta \left( a\right) \Delta \left( b\right) \) .

Condition (1) for \( A \) dualizes to (2) for \( {A}^{ * } \) , and similarly (2) for \( A \) dualizes to (1) for \( {A}^{ * } \) . Condition (3) for \( A \) dualizes to (3) for \( {A}^{ * } \) .

Example 3C.11. Let us compute the dual of a polynomial algebra \( R\left\lbrack  x\right\rbrack \) . Suppose first that \( x \) has even dimension. Then \( \Delta \left( {x}^{n}\right)  = {\left( x \otimes  1 + 1 \otimes  x\right) }^{n} = \mathop{\sum }\limits_{i}\left( \begin{array}{l} n \\  i \end{array}\right) {x}^{i} \otimes  {x}^{n - i} \) , so if \( {\alpha }_{i} \) is dual to \( {x}^{i} \) , the term \( \left( \begin{matrix} n \\  i \end{matrix}\right) {x}^{i} \otimes  {x}^{n - i} \) in \( \Delta \left( {x}^{n}\right) \) gives the product relation \( {\alpha }_{i}{\alpha }_{n - i} = \left( \begin{matrix} n \\  i \end{matrix}\right) {\alpha }_{n} \) . This is the rule for multiplication in a divided polynomial algebra, so the dual of \( R\left\lbrack  x\right\rbrack \) is \( {\Gamma }_{R}\left\lbrack  \alpha \right\rbrack \) if the dimension of \( x \) is even. This also holds if \( 2 = 0 \) in \( R \) , since the even-dimensionality of \( x \) was used only to deduce that \( R\left\lbrack  x\right\rbrack   \otimes  R\left\lbrack  x\right\rbrack \) is strictly commutative.

In case \( x \) is odd-dimensional, then as we saw in Example 3C.1, if we set \( y = {x}^{2} \) , we have \( \Delta \left( {y}^{n}\right)  = {\left( y \otimes  1 + 1 \otimes  y\right) }^{n} = \mathop{\sum }\limits_{i}\left( \begin{matrix} n \\  i \end{matrix}\right) {y}^{i} \otimes  {y}^{n - i} \) and \( \Delta \left( {x{y}^{n}}\right)  = \Delta \left( x\right) \Delta \left( {y}^{n}\right)  = \; \mathop{\sum }\limits_{i}\left( \begin{matrix} n \\  i \end{matrix}\right) x{y}^{i} \otimes  {y}^{n - i} + \mathop{\sum }\limits_{i}\left( \begin{matrix} n \\  i \end{matrix}\right) {y}^{i} \otimes  x{y}^{n - i} \) . These formulas for \( \Delta \) say that the dual of \( R\left\lbrack  x\right\rbrack \) is \( {\Lambda }_{R}\left\lbrack  \alpha \right\rbrack   \otimes  {\Gamma }_{R}\left\lbrack  \beta \right\rbrack \) where \( \alpha \) is dual to \( x \) and \( \beta \) is dual to \( y \) .

This algebra allows us to deduce the cup product structure on \( {H}^{ * }\left( {J\left( {S}^{n}\right) ;R}\right) \) from the geometric calculation \( {H}_{ * }\left( {J\left( {S}^{n}\right) ;R}\right)  \approx  R\left\lbrack  x\right\rbrack \) in Example 3C.7. As another application, recall from earlier in this section that \( {\mathbb{{RP}}}^{\infty } \) and \( {\mathbb{{CP}}}^{\infty } \) are H-spaces, so from their cup product structures we can conclude that the Pontryagin rings \( {H}_{ * }\left( {{\mathbb{{RP}}}^{\infty };{\mathbb{Z}}_{2}}\right) \) and \( {H}_{ * }\left( {{\mathbb{{CP}}}^{\infty };\mathbb{Z}}\right) \) are divided polynomial algebras.

In these examples the Hopf algebra is generated as an algebra by primitive elements, so the product determines the coproduct and hence the dual algebra. This is not true in general, however. For example, we have seen that the Hopf algebra \( {\Gamma }_{{\mathbb{Z}}_{v}}\left\lbrack  \alpha \right\rbrack \) is isomorphic as an algebra to \( {\bigotimes }_{i \geq  0}{\mathbb{Z}}_{p}\left\lbrack  {\alpha }_{{p}^{i}}\right\rbrack  /\left( {\alpha }_{{p}^{i}}^{p}\right) \) , but if we regard the latter tensor product as the tensor product of the Hopf algebras \( {\mathbb{Z}}_{p}\left\lbrack  {\alpha }_{{p}^{i}}\right\rbrack  /\left( {\alpha }_{{p}^{i}}^{p}\right) \) then the elements \( {\alpha }_{{p}^{i}} \) are primitive, though they are not primitive in \( {\Gamma }_{{\mathbb{Z}}_{p}}\left\lbrack  \alpha \right\rbrack \) for \( i > 0 \) . In fact, the Hopf algebra \( {\bigotimes }_{i \geq  0}{\mathbb{Z}}_{p}\left\lbrack  {\alpha }_{{p}^{i}}\right\rbrack  /\left( {\alpha }_{{p}^{i}}^{p}\right) \) is its own dual, according to one of the exercises below, but the dual of \( {\Gamma }_{{\mathbb{Z}}_{p}}\left\lbrack  \alpha \right\rbrack \) is \( {\mathbb{Z}}_{p}\left\lbrack  \alpha \right\rbrack \) .

## Exercises

1. Suppose that \( X \) is a CW complex with basepoint \( e \in  X \) a 0-cell. Show that \( X \) is an H-space if there is a map \( \mu  : X \times  X \rightarrow  X \) such that the maps \( X \rightarrow  X, x \mapsto  \mu \left( {x, e}\right) \) and \( x \mapsto  \mu \left( {e, x}\right) \) , are homotopic to the identity. [Sometimes this is taken as the definition of an H-space, rather than the more restrictive condition in the definition we have given.] With the same hypotheses, show also that \( \mu \) can be homotoped so that \( e \) is a strict two-sided identity.

2. Show that a retract of an H-space is an H-space if it contains the identity element.

3. Show that in a homotopy-associative H-space whose set of path-components is a group with respect to the multiplication induced by the H-space structure, all the path-components must be homotopy equivalent. [Homotopy-associative means associative up to homotopy.]

4. Show that an H-space or topological group structure on a path-connected, locally path-connected space can be lifted to such a structure on its universal cover. [For the group \( {SO}\left( n\right) \) considered in the next section, the universal cover for \( n > 2 \) is a 2-sheeted cover, a group called Spin(n).]

5. Show that if \( \left( {X, e}\right) \) is an \( \mathrm{H} \) -space then \( {\pi }_{1}\left( {X, e}\right) \) is abelian. [Compare the usual composition \( f \cdot  g \) of loops with the product \( \mu \left( {f\left( t\right) , g\left( t\right) }\right) \) coming from the H-space multiplication \( \mu \) .]

6. Show that \( {S}^{n} \) is an H-space iff the attaching map of the \( {2n} \) -cell of \( {J}_{2}\left( {S}^{n}\right) \) is homotopically trivial.

7. What are the primitive elements of the Hopf algebra \( {\mathbb{Z}}_{p}\left\lbrack  x\right\rbrack \) for \( p \) prime?

8. Show that the tensor product of two Hopf algebras is a Hopf algebra.

9. Apply the theorems of Hopf and Borel to show that for an H-space \( X \) that is a connected finite CW complex with \( {\widetilde{H}}_{ * }\left( {X;\mathbb{Z}}\right)  \neq  0 \) , the Euler characteristic \( \chi \left( X\right) \) is 0 .

10. Let \( X \) be a path-connected H-space with \( {H}^{ * }\left( {X;R}\right) \) free and finitely generated in each dimension. For maps \( f, g : X \rightarrow  X \) , the product \( {fg} : X \rightarrow  X \) is defined by \( \left( {fg}\right) \left( x\right)  = f\left( x\right) g\left( x\right) \) , using the H-space product.

(a) Show that \( {\left( fg\right) }^{ * }\left( \alpha \right)  = {f}^{ * }\left( \alpha \right)  + {g}^{ * }\left( \alpha \right) \) for primitive elements \( \alpha  \in  {H}^{ * }\left( {X;R}\right) \) .

(b) Deduce that the \( {k}^{th} \) -power map \( x \mapsto  {x}^{k} \) induces the map \( \alpha  \mapsto  {k\alpha } \) on primitive elements \( \alpha \) . In particular the quaternionic \( {k}^{th} \) -power map \( {S}^{3} \rightarrow  {S}^{3} \) has degree \( k \) .

(c) Show that every polynomial \( {a}_{n}{x}^{n}{b}_{n} + \cdots  + {a}_{1}x{b}_{1} + {a}_{0} \) of nonzero degree with coefficients in \( \mathbb{H} \) has a root in \( \mathbb{H} \) . [See Theorem 1.8.]

11. If \( {T}^{n} \) is the \( n \) -dimensional torus, the product of \( n \) circles, show that the Pontrya-gin ring \( {H}_{ * }\left( {{T}^{n};\mathbb{Z}}\right) \) is the exterior algebra \( {\Lambda }_{\mathbb{Z}}\left\lbrack  {{x}_{1},\cdots ,{x}_{n}}\right\rbrack \) with \( \left| {x}_{i}\right|  = 1 \) .

12. Compute the Pontryagin product structure in \( {H}_{ * }\left( {L;{\mathbb{Z}}_{p}}\right) \) where \( L \) is an infinite-dimensional lens space \( {S}^{\infty }/{\mathbb{Z}}_{p} \) , for \( p \) an odd prime, using the coproduct in \( {H}^{ * }\left( {L;{\mathbb{Z}}_{p}}\right) \) .

13. Verify that the Hopf algebras \( {\Lambda }_{R}\left\lbrack  \alpha \right\rbrack \) and \( {\mathbb{Z}}_{p}\left\lbrack  \alpha \right\rbrack  /\left( {\alpha }^{p}\right) \) are self-dual.

14. Show that the coproduct in the Hopf algebra \( {H}_{ * }\left( {X;R}\right) \) dual to \( {H}^{ * }\left( {X;R}\right) \) is induced by the diagonal map \( X \rightarrow  X \times  X, x \mapsto  \left( {x, x}\right) \) .

15. Suppose that \( X \) is a path-connected \( \mathrm{H} \) -space such that \( {H}^{ * }\left( {X;\mathbb{Z}}\right) \) is free and finitely generated in each dimension, and \( {H}^{ * }\left( {X;\mathbb{Q}}\right) \) is a polynomial ring \( \mathbb{Q}\left\lbrack  \alpha \right\rbrack \) . Show that the Pontryagin ring \( {H}_{ * }\left( {X;\mathbb{Z}}\right) \) is commutative and associative, with a structure uniquely determined by the ring \( {H}^{ * }\left( {X;\mathbb{Z}}\right) \) .

16. Classify algebraically the Hopf algebras \( A \) over \( \mathbb{Z} \) such that \( {A}^{n} \) is free for each \( n \) and \( A \otimes  \mathbb{Q} \approx  \mathbb{Q}\left\lbrack  \alpha \right\rbrack \) . In particular, determine which Hopf algebras \( A \otimes  {\mathbb{Z}}_{p} \) arise from such \( A \) ’s.
