# 4.K The Dold-Thom Theorem

In the preceding section we studied the free monoid \( J\left( X\right) \) generated by a space \( X \) , and in this section we take up its commutative analog, the free abelian monoid generated by \( X \) . This is the infinite symmetric product \( {SP}\left( X\right) \) introduced briefly in §3.C. The main result will be a theorem of [Dold & Thom 1958] asserting that \( {\pi }_{ * }{SP}\left( X\right)  \approx  {\widetilde{H}}_{ * }\left( {X;\mathbb{Z}}\right) \) for all connected CW complexes \( X \) . In particular this yields the surprising fact that \( {SP}\left( {S}^{n}\right) \) is a \( K\left( {\mathbb{Z}, n}\right) \) , and more generally that the functor \( {SP} \) takes Moore spaces \( M\left( {G, n}\right) \) to Eilenberg-MacLane spaces \( K\left( {G, n}\right) \) . This leads to the general result that for all connected CW complexes \( X,{SP}\left( X\right) \) has the homotopy type of a product of Eilenberg-MacLane spaces. In other words, the \( k \) -invariants of \( {SP}\left( X\right) \) are all trivial.

The main step in the proof of the Dold-Thom theorem will be to show that the homotopy groups \( {\pi }_{ * }{SP}\left( X\right) \) define a homology theory. An easy computation of the coefficient groups \( {\pi }_{ * }{SP}\left( {S}^{n}\right) \) will then show that this must be ordinary homology with \( \mathbb{Z} \) coefficients. A new idea needed for the proof of the main step is the notion of a quasifibration, generalizing fibrations and fiber bundles. In order to establish a few basic facts about quasifibrations we first make a small detour to prove an essentially elementary fact about relative homotopy groups.

## A Mayer-Vietoris Property of Homotopy Groups

In this subsection we will be concerned largely with relative homotopy groups, and it will be impossible to avoid the awkward fact that there is no really good way to define the relative \( {\pi }_{0} \) . What we will do as a compromise is to take \( {\pi }_{0}\left( {X, A,{x}_{0}}\right) \) to be the quotient set \( {\pi }_{0}\left( {X,{x}_{0}}\right) /{\pi }_{0}\left( {A,{x}_{0}}\right) \) . This at least allows the long exact sequence of homotopy groups for \( \left( {X, A}\right) \) to end with the terms

\[
{\pi }_{0}\left( {A,{x}_{0}}\right)  \rightarrow  {\pi }_{0}\left( {X,{x}_{0}}\right)  \rightarrow  {\pi }_{0}\left( {X, A,{x}_{0}}\right)  \rightarrow  0
\]

An exercise for \( \text{ § }{4.1} \) shows that the five-lemma can be applied to the map of long exact sequences induced by a map \( \left( {X, A}\right)  \rightarrow  \left( {Y, B}\right) \) , provided the basepoint is allowed to vary. However, the long exact sequence of a triple cannot be extended through the \( {\pi }_{0} \) terms with this definition, so one must proceed with some caution.

The excision theorem for homology involves a space \( X \) with subspaces \( A \) and \( B \) such that \( X \) is the union of the interiors of \( A \) and \( B \) . In this situation we call \( \left( {X;A, B}\right) \) an excisive triad. By a map \( f : \left( {X;A, B}\right)  \rightarrow  \left( {Y;C, D}\right) \) we mean \( f : X \rightarrow  Y \) with \( f\left( A\right)  \subset  C \) and \( f\left( B\right)  \subset  D \) .

Proposition 4K.1. Let \( f : \left( {X;A, B}\right)  \rightarrow  \left( {Y;C, D}\right) \) be a map of excisive triads. If the induced maps \( {\pi }_{i}\left( {A, A \cap  B}\right)  \rightarrow  {\pi }_{i}\left( {C, C \cap  D}\right) \) and \( {\pi }_{i}\left( {B, A \cap  B}\right)  \rightarrow  {\pi }_{i}\left( {D, C \cap  D}\right) \) are bijections for \( i < n \) and surjections for \( i = n \) , for all choices of basepoints, then the same is true of the induced maps \( {\pi }_{i}\left( {X, A}\right)  \rightarrow  {\pi }_{i}\left( {Y, C}\right) \) . By symmetry the conclusion holds also for the maps \( {\pi }_{i}\left( {X, B}\right)  \rightarrow  {\pi }_{i}\left( {Y, D}\right) \) .

The corresponding statement for homology is a trivial consequence of excision which says that \( {H}_{i}\left( {X, A}\right)  \approx  {H}_{i}\left( {B, A \cap  B}\right) \) and \( {H}_{i}\left( {Y, C}\right)  \approx  {H}_{i}\left( {D, C \cap  D}\right) \) , so it is not necessary to assume anything about the map \( {H}_{i}\left( {A, A \cap  B}\right)  \rightarrow  {H}_{i}\left( {C, C \cap  D}\right) \) . With the failure of excision for homotopy groups, however, it is not surprising that the assumption on \( {\pi }_{i}\left( {A, A \cap  B}\right)  \rightarrow  {\pi }_{i}\left( {C, C \cap  D}\right) \) cannot be dropped. An example is provided by the quotient map \( f : {D}^{2} \rightarrow  {S}^{2} \) collapsing \( \partial {D}^{2} \) to the north pole of \( {S}^{2} \) , with \( C \) and \( D \) the northern and southern hemispheres of \( {S}^{2} \) , and \( A \) and \( B \) their preimages under \( f \) .

Proof: First we will establish a general fact about relative homotopy groups. Consider an inclusion \( \left( {X, A}\right)  \hookrightarrow  \left( {Y, C}\right) \) . We will show the following three conditions are equivalent for each \( n \geq  1 \) :

(i) For all choices of basepoints the map \( {\pi }_{i}\left( {X, A}\right)  \rightarrow  {\pi }_{i}\left( {Y, C}\right) \) induced by inclusion is surjective for \( i = n \) and has trivial kernel for \( i = n - 1 \) .

(ii) Let \( \partial {D}^{n} \) be written as the union of hemispheres \( {\partial }_{ + }{D}^{n} \) and \( {\partial }_{ - }{D}^{n} \) intersecting in \( {S}^{n - 2} \) . Then every map

\[
\left( {{D}^{n}\times \{ 0\}  \cup  {\partial }_{ + }{D}^{n} \times  I,{\partial }_{ - }{D}^{n}\times \{ 0\}  \cup  {S}^{n - 2} \times  I}\right)  \rightarrow  \left( {Y, C}\right)
\]

taking \( \left( {{\partial }_{ + }{D}^{n}\times \{ 1\} ,{S}^{n - 2}\times \{ 1\} }\right) \) to \( \left( {X, A}\right) \) extends to a map \( \left( {{D}^{n} \times  I,{\partial }_{ - }{D}^{n} \times  I}\right)  \rightarrow  \left( {Y, C}\right) \) taking \( \left( {{D}^{n}\times \{ 1\} ,{\partial }_{ - }{D}^{n}\times \{ 1\} }\right) \) to \( \left( {X, A}\right) \) .

(iii) Condition (ii) with the added hypothesis that the restriction of the given map to \( {\partial }_{ + }{D}^{n} \times  I \) is independent of the \( I \) coordinate.

It is obvious that (ii) and (iii) are equivalent since the stronger hypothesis in (iii) can always be achieved by composing with a homotopy of \( {D}^{n} \times  I \) that shrinks \( {\partial }_{ + }{D}^{n} \times  I \) to \( {\partial }_{ + }{D}^{n} \times  \{ 1\} \) .

To see that (iii) implies (i), let \( f : \left( {{\partial }_{ + }{D}^{n}\times \{ 1\} ,{S}^{n - 2}\times \{ 1\} }\right)  \rightarrow  \left( {X, A}\right) \) represent an element of \( {\pi }_{n - 1}\left( {X, A}\right) \) . If this is in the kernel of the map to \( {\pi }_{n - 1}\left( {Y, C}\right) \) , then we get an extension of \( f \) over \( {D}^{n} \times  \{ 0\}  \cup  {\partial }_{ + }{D}^{n} \times  I \) , with the constant homotopy on \( {\partial }_{ + }{D}^{n} \times  I \) and \( \left( {{D}^{n}\times \{ 0\} ,{\partial }_{ - }{D}^{n}\times \{ 0\} }\right) \) mapping to \( \left( {Y, C}\right) \) . Condition (iii) then gives an extension over \( {D}^{n} \times  I \) , whose restriction to \( {D}^{n} \times  \{ 1\} \) shows that \( f \) is zero in \( {\pi }_{n - 1}\left( {X, A}\right) \) , so the kernel of \( {\pi }_{n - 1}\left( {X, A}\right)  \rightarrow  {\pi }_{n - 1}\left( {Y, C}\right) \) is trivial. To check surjectivity of the map \( {\pi }_{n}\left( {X, A}\right)  \rightarrow  {\pi }_{n}\left( {Y, C}\right) \) , represent an element of \( {\pi }_{n}\left( {Y, C}\right) \) by a map \( f : {D}^{n} \times  \{ 0\}  \rightarrow  Y \) taking \( {\partial }_{ - }{D}^{n} \times  \{ 0\} \) to \( C \) and \( {\partial }_{ + }{D}^{n} \times  \{ 0\} \) to a chosen basepoint. Extend \( f \) over \( {\partial }_{ + }{D}^{n} \times  I \) via the constant homotopy, then extend over \( {D}^{n} \times  I \) by applying (iii). The result is a homotopy of the given \( f \) to a map representing an element of the image of \( {\pi }_{n}\left( {X, A}\right)  \rightarrow  {\pi }_{n}\left( {Y, C}\right) . \)

Now we show that (i) implies (ii). Given a map \( f \) as in the hypothesis of (ii), the injectivity part of (i) gives an extension of \( f \) over \( {D}^{n} \times  \{ 1\} \) . Choose a small disk \( {E}^{n} \subset  {\partial }_{ - }{D}^{n} \times  I \) , shown shaded in the figure, intersecting \( {\partial }_{ - }{D}^{n} \times  \{ 1\} \) in a hemisphere \( {\partial }_{ + }{E}^{n} \) of its boundary. We may assume the extended \( f \) has a constant value \( {x}_{0} \in  A \) on \( {\partial }_{ + }{E}^{n} \) . Viewing the extended \( f \) as representing an element of \( {\pi }_{n}\left( {Y, C,{x}_{0}}\right) \) , the surjectivity part of (i) then gives an extension of \( f \) over \( {D}^{n} \times  I \) taking \( \left( {{E}^{n},{\partial }_{ - }{E}^{n}}\right) \) to \( \left( {X, A}\right) \) and the rest of \( {\partial }_{ - }{D}^{n} \times  I \) to \( C \) . The argument is finished by composing this extended \( f \) with a deformation of \( {D}^{n} \times  I \) pushing \( {E}^{n} \) into \( {D}^{n} \times  \{ 1\} \) .

Having shown the equivalence of (i)-(iii), let us prove the proposition. We may reduce to the case that the given \( f : \left( {X;A, B}\right)  \rightarrow  \left( {Y;C, D}\right) \) is an inclusion by using mapping cylinders. One’s first guess would be to replace \( \left( {Y;C, D}\right) \) by the triad of mapping cylinders \( \left( {{M}_{f};{M}_{f \mid  A},{M}_{f \mid  B}}\right) \) , where we view \( f \mid  A \) as a map \( A \rightarrow  C \) and \( f \mid  B \) as a map \( B \rightarrow  D \) . However, the triad \( \left( {{M}_{f};{M}_{f \mid  A},{M}_{f \mid  B}}\right) \) need not be excisive, for example if \( X \) consists of two points \( A \) and \( B \) and \( Y \) is a single point. To remedy this problem, replace \( {M}_{f \mid  A} \) by its union with \( {f}^{-1}\left( C\right)  \times  \left( {{}^{1}/{}_{2},1}\right) \) in \( {M}_{f} \) , and enlarge \( {M}_{f \mid  B} \) similarly.

Now we prove the proposition for an inclusion \( \left( {X;A, B}\right)  \hookrightarrow  \left( {Y;C, D}\right) \) . The case \( n = 0 \) is trivial from the definitions, so let us assume \( n \geq  1 \) . In view of the equivalence of condition (i) with (ii) and (iii), it suffices to show that condition (ii) for the inclusions \( \left( {A, A \cap  B}\right)  \hookrightarrow  \left( {C, C \cap  D}\right) \) and \( \left( {B, A \cap  B}\right)  \hookrightarrow  \left( {D, C \cap  D}\right) \) implies (iii) for the inclusion \( \left( {X, A}\right)  \hookrightarrow  \left( {Y, C}\right) \) . Let a map \( f : {D}^{n} \times  \{ 0\}  \cup  {\partial }_{ + }{D}^{n} \times  I \rightarrow  Y \) as in the hypothesis of (iii) be given. The argument will involve subdivision of \( {D}^{n} \) into smaller disks, and for this it is more convenient to use the cube \( {I}^{n} \) instead of \( {D}^{n} \) , so let us identify \( {I}^{n} \) with \( {D}^{n} \) in such a way that \( {\partial }_{ - }{D}^{n} \) corresponds to the face \( {I}^{n - 1} \times  \{ 1\} \) , which we denote by \( {\partial }_{ - }{I}^{n} \) , and \( {\partial }_{ + }{D}^{n} \) corresponds to the remaining faces of \( {I}^{n} \) , which we denote by \( {\partial }_{ + }{I}^{n} \) . Thus we are given \( f \) on \( {I}^{n} \times  \{ 0\} \) taking \( {\partial }_{ + }{I}^{n} \times  \{ 0\} \) to \( X \) and \( {\partial }_{ - }{I}^{n} \times  \{ 0\} \) to \( C \) , and on \( {\partial }_{ + }{I}^{n} \times  I \) we have the constant homotopy.

Since \( \left( {Y;C, D}\right) \) is an excisive triad, we can subdivide each of the \( I \) factors of \( {I}^{n} \times  \{ 0\} \) into subintervals so that \( f \) takes each of the resulting \( n \) -dimensional sub-cubes of \( {I}^{n} \times  \{ 0\} \) into either \( C \) or \( D \) . The extension of \( f \) we construct will have the following key property:

If \( K \) is a one of the subcubes of \( {I}^{n} \times  \{ 0\} \) , or a lower-dimensional face of such a (*) cube, then the extension of \( f \) takes \( \left( {K \times  I, K\times \{ 1\} }\right) \) to \( \left( {C, A}\right) \) or \( \left( {D, B}\right) \) whenever \( f \) takes \( K \) to \( C \) or \( D \) , respectively.

Initially we have \( f \) defined on \( {\partial }_{ + }{I}^{n} \times  I \) with image in \( X \) , independent of the \( I \) coordinate, and we may assume the condition (*) holds here since we may assume that \( A = X \cap  C \) and \( B = X \cap  D \) , these conditions holding for the mapping cylinder construction described above.

Consider the problem of extending \( f \) over \( K \times  I \) for \( K \) one of the subcubes. We may assume that \( f \) has already been extended to \( {\partial }_{ + }K \times  I \) so that \( \left( *\right) \) is satisfied, by induction on \( n \) and on the sequence of subintervals of the last coordinate of \( {I}^{n} \times  \{ 0\} \) . To extend \( f \) over \( K \times  I \) , let us first deal with the cases that the given \( f \) takes \( \left( {K,{\partial }_{ - }K}\right) \) to \( \left( {C, C \cap  D}\right) \) or \( \left( {D, C \cap  D}\right) \) . Then by (ii) for the inclusion \( \left( {A, A \cap  B}\right)  \hookrightarrow  \left( {C, C \cap  D}\right) \) or \( \left( {B, A \cap  B}\right)  \hookrightarrow  \left( {D, C \cap  D}\right) \) we may extend \( f \) over \( K \times  I \) so that \( \left( *\right) \) is still satisfied. If neither of these two cases applies, then the given \( f \) takes \( \left( {K,{\partial }_{ - }K}\right) \) just to \( \left( {C, C}\right) \) or \( \left( {D, D}\right) \) , and we can apply (ii) trivially to construct the desired extension of \( f \) over \( K \times  I \) .

Corollary 4K.2. Given a map \( f : X \rightarrow  Y \) and open covers \( \left\{  {U}_{i}\right\} \) of \( X \) and \( \left\{  {V}_{i}\right\} \) of \( Y \) with \( f\left( {U}_{i}\right)  \subset  {V}_{i} \) for all \( i \) , then if each restriction \( f : {U}_{i} \rightarrow  {V}_{i} \) and more generally each \( \parallel f : {U}_{{i}_{1}} \cap  \cdots  \cap  {U}_{{i}_{n}} \rightarrow  {V}_{{i}_{1}} \cap  \cdots  \cap  {V}_{{i}_{n}} \) is a weak homotopy equivalence, so is \( f : X \rightarrow  Y \) .

Proof: First let us do the case of covers by two sets. By the five-lemma, the hypotheses imply that \( {\pi }_{n}\left( {{U}_{i},{U}_{1} \cap  {U}_{2}}\right)  \rightarrow  {\pi }_{n}\left( {{V}_{i},{V}_{1} \cap  {V}_{2}}\right) \) is bijective for \( i = 1,2, n \geq  0 \) , and all choices of basepoints. The preceding proposition then implies that the maps \( {\pi }_{n}\left( {X,{U}_{1}}\right)  \rightarrow  {\pi }_{n}\left( {Y,{V}_{1}}\right) \) are isomorphisms. Hence by the five-lemma again, so are the maps \( {\pi }_{n}\left( X\right)  \rightarrow  {\pi }_{n}\left( Y\right) \) .

By induction, the case of finite covers by \( k > 2 \) sets reduces to the case of covers by two sets, by letting one of the two sets be the union of the first \( k - 1 \) of the given sets and the other be the \( {k}^{th} \) set. The case of infinite covers reduces to the finite case since for surjectivity of \( {\pi }_{n}\left( X\right)  \rightarrow  {\pi }_{n}\left( Y\right) \) , a map \( {S}^{n} \rightarrow  Y \) has compact image covered by finitely many \( {V}_{i} \) ’s, and similarly for injectivity.

## Quasifibrations

A map \( p : E \rightarrow  B \) with \( B \) path-connected is a quasifibration if the induced map \( {p}_{ * } : {\pi }_{i}\left( {E,{p}^{-1}\left( b\right) ,{x}_{0}}\right)  \rightarrow  {\pi }_{i}\left( {B, b}\right) \) is an isomorphism for all \( b \in  B,{x}_{0} \in  {p}^{-1}\left( b\right) \) , and \( i \geq  0 \) . We have shown in Theorem 4.41 that fiber bundles and fibrations have this property for \( i > 0 \) , as a consequence of the homotopy lifting property, and the same reasoning applies for \( i = 0 \) since we assume \( B \) is path-connected.

For example, consider the natural projection \( {M}_{f} \rightarrow  I \) of the mapping cylinder of a map \( f : X \rightarrow  Y \) . This projection will be a quasifibration iff \( f \) is a weak homotopy equivalence, since the latter condition is equivalent to having \( {\pi }_{i}\left( {{M}_{f},{p}^{-1}\left( b\right) }\right)  = 0 = {\pi }_{i}\left( {I, b}\right) \) for all \( i \) and all \( b \in  I \) . Note that if \( f \) is not surjective, there are paths in \( I \) that do not lift to paths in \( {M}_{f} \) with a prescribed starting point, so \( p \) will not be a fibration in such cases.

An alternative condition for a map \( p : E \rightarrow  B \) to be a quasifibration is that the inclusion of each fiber \( {p}^{-1}\left( b\right) \) into the homotopy fiber \( {F}_{b} \) of \( p \) over \( b \) is a weak homotopy equivalence. Recall that \( {F}_{b} \) is the space of all pairs \( \left( {x, y}\right) \) with \( x \in  E \) and \( \gamma \) a path in \( B \) from \( p\left( x\right) \) to \( b \) . The actual fiber \( {p}^{-1}\left( b\right) \) is included in \( {F}_{b} \) as the pairs with \( x \in  {p}^{-1}\left( b\right) \) and \( \gamma \) the constant path at \( x \) . To see the equivalence of the two definitions, consider the commutative triangle at the right, where \( {F}_{b} \rightarrow  {E}_{p} \rightarrow  B \) is the usual path-fibration construction applied to \( p \) . The right-hand map in the diagram is an isomorphism for all \( i \geq  0 \) , and the upper map will be an isomorphism for all \( i \geq  0 \) iff the inclusion \( {p}^{-1}\left( b\right)  \hookrightarrow  {F}_{b} \) is a weak equivalence since \( E \simeq  {E}_{p} \) . Hence the two definitions are equivalent.

Recall from Proposition 4.61 that all fibers of a fibration over a path-connected base are homotopy equivalent. Since we are only considering quasifibrations over path-connected base spaces, this implies that all the fibers of a quasifibration have the same weak homotopy type. Quasifibrations over a base that is not path-connected are considered in the exercises, but we will not need this generality in what follows.

The following technical lemma gives various conditions for recognizing that a map is a quasifibration, which will be needed in the proof of the Dold-Thom theorem.

Lemma 4K.3. A map \( p : E \rightarrow  B \) is a quasifibration if any one of the following conditions is satisfied:

(a) \( B \) can be decomposed as the union of open sets \( {V}_{1} \) and \( {V}_{2} \) such that each of the restrictions \( {p}^{-1}\left( {V}_{1}\right)  \rightarrow  {V}_{1},{p}^{-1}\left( {V}_{2}\right)  \rightarrow  {V}_{2} \) , and \( {p}^{-1}\left( {{V}_{1} \cap  {V}_{2}}\right)  \rightarrow  {V}_{1} \cap  {V}_{2} \) is a quasifibration.

(b) \( B \) is the union of an increasing sequence of subspaces \( {B}_{1} \subset  {B}_{2} \subset  \cdots \) with the property that each compact set in \( B \) lies in some \( {B}_{n} \) , and such that each restriction \( {p}^{-1}\left( {B}_{n}\right)  \rightarrow  {B}_{n} \) is a quasifibration.

(c) There is a deformation \( {F}_{t} \) of \( E \) into a subspace \( {E}^{\prime } \) , covering a deformation \( {\bar{F}}_{t} \) of \( B \) into a subspace \( {B}^{\prime } \) , such that the restriction \( {E}^{\prime } \rightarrow  {B}^{\prime } \) is a quasifibration and \( {F}_{1} : {p}^{-1}\left( b\right)  \rightarrow  {p}^{-1}\left( {{\bar{F}}_{1}\left( b\right) }\right) \) is a weak homotopy equivalence for each \( b \in  B \) .

By a 'deformation' in (c) we mean a deformation retraction in the weak sense as defined in the exercises for Chapter 0, where the homotopy is not required to be the identity on the subspace.

Proof: (a) To avoid some tedious details we will consider only the case that the fibers of \( p \) are path-connected, which will suffice for our present purposes, leaving the general case as an exercise for the reader. This hypothesis on fibers guarantees that all \( {\pi }_{0} \) ’s arising in the proof are trivial. In particular, by an exercise for \( \text{ § }{4.1} \) this allows us to terminate long exact sequences of homotopy groups of triples with zeros in the \( {\pi }_{0} \) positions.

Let \( {U}_{1} = {p}^{-1}\left( {V}_{1}\right) \) and \( {U}_{2} = {p}^{-1}\left( {V}_{2}\right) \) . The five-lemma for the long exact sequences of homotopy groups of the triples \( \left( {{U}_{k},{U}_{1} \cap  {U}_{2},{p}^{-1}\left( b\right) }\right) \) and \( \left( {{V}_{k},{V}_{1} \cap  {V}_{2}, b}\right) \) implies that the maps \( {\pi }_{i}\left( {{U}_{k},{U}_{1} \cap  {U}_{2}}\right)  \rightarrow  {\pi }_{i}\left( {{V}_{k},{V}_{1} \cap  {V}_{2}}\right) \) are isomorphisms for \( k = 1,2 \) and all \( i \) . Then Proposition 4K. 1 implies that the maps \( {\pi }_{i}\left( {E,{U}_{k}}\right)  \rightarrow  {\pi }_{i}\left( {B,{V}_{k}}\right) \) are isomorphisms for all choices of basepoints. The maps \( {\pi }_{i}\left( {{U}_{k},{p}^{-1}\left( b\right) }\right)  \rightarrow  {\pi }_{i}\left( {{V}_{k}, b}\right) \) are isomorphisms by hypothesis, so from the five-lemma we can then deduce that the maps \( {\pi }_{i}\left( {E,{p}^{-1}\left( b\right) }\right)  \rightarrow  {\pi }_{i}\left( {B, b}\right) \) are isomorphisms for all \( b \in  {V}_{k} \) , hence for all \( b \in  B \) . (b) Since each compact set in \( B \) lies in some \( {B}_{n} \) , each compact set in \( E \) lies in some subspace \( {E}_{n} = {p}^{-1}\left( {B}_{n}\right) \) , so \( {\pi }_{i}\left( {E,{p}^{-1}\left( b\right) }\right) \) is the direct limit \( \underline{\lim }{\pi }_{i}\left( {{E}_{n},{p}^{-1}\left( b\right) }\right) \) just as \( {\pi }_{i}\left( {B, b}\right)  = \underline{\lim }{\pi }_{i}\left( {{B}_{n}, b}\right) \) . It follows that the map \( {\pi }_{i}\left( {E,{p}^{-1}\left( b\right) }\right)  \rightarrow  {\pi }_{i}\left( {B, b}\right) \) is an isomorphism since each of the maps \( {\pi }_{i}\left( {{E}_{n},{p}^{-1}\left( b\right) }\right)  \rightarrow  {\pi }_{i}\left( {{B}_{n}, b}\right) \) is an isomorphism by assumption. We can take the point \( b \) to be an arbitrary point in \( B \) and then discard any initial spaces \( {B}_{n} \) in the sequence that do not contain \( b \) , so we can assume \( b \) lies in \( {B}_{n} \) for all \( n \) .

(c) Consider the commutative diagram where \( b \) is an arbitrary point in \( B \) . The upper map in the diagram is an isomorphism by the five-lemma since the hypotheses imply that \( {F}_{1} \) induces isomorphisms \( {\pi }_{i}\left( E\right)  \rightarrow  {\pi }_{i}\left( {E}^{\prime }\right) \) and \( {\pi }_{i}\left( {{p}^{-1}\left( b\right) }\right)  \rightarrow  {\pi }_{i}\left( {{p}^{-1}\left( {{\bar{F}}_{1}\left( b\right) }\right) }\right) \) for all \( i \) . The hypotheses also imply that the lower map and the right-hand map are isomorphisms. Hence the left-hand map is an isomorphism.

## Symmetric Products

Let us recall the definition from \( \text{ § }3.\mathrm{C} \) . For a space \( X \) the \( n \) -fold symmetric product \( S{P}_{n}\left( X\right) \) is the quotient space of the product of \( n \) copies of \( X \) obtained by factoring out the action of the symmetric group permuting the factors. A choice of basepoint \( e \in  X \) gives inclusions \( S{P}_{n}\left( X\right)  \hookrightarrow  S{P}_{n + 1}\left( X\right) \) induced by \( \left( {{x}_{1},\cdots ,{x}_{n}}\right)  \mapsto  \left( {{x}_{1},\cdots ,{x}_{n}, e}\right) \) , and \( {SP}\left( X\right) \) is defined to be the union of this increasing sequence of spaces, with the direct limit topology. Note that \( S{P}_{n} \) is a homotopy functor: A map \( f : X \rightarrow  Y \) induces \( {f}_{ * } : S{P}_{n}\left( X\right)  \rightarrow  S{P}_{n}\left( Y\right) \) , and \( f \simeq  g \) implies \( {f}_{ * } \simeq  {g}_{ * } \) . Hence \( X \simeq  Y \) implies \( S{P}_{n}\left( X\right)  \simeq  S{P}_{n}\left( Y\right) \) . In similar fashion \( {SP} \) is a homotopy functor on the category of basepointed spaces and basepoint-preserving homotopy classes of maps. It follows that \( X \simeq  Y \) implies \( {SP}\left( X\right)  \simeq  {SP}\left( Y\right) \) for connected CW complexes \( X \) and \( Y \) since in this case requiring maps and homotopies to preserve basepoints does not affect the relation of homotopy equivalence.

Example 4K.4. An interesting special case is when \( X = {S}^{2} \) because in this case \( {SP}\left( {S}^{2}\right) \) can be identified with \( {\mathbb{{CP}}}^{\infty } \) in the following way. We first identify \( {\mathbb{{CP}}}^{n} \) with the nonzero polynomials of degree at most \( n \) with coefficients in \( \mathbb{C} \) , modulo scalar multiplication, by letting \( {a}_{0} + \cdots  + {a}_{n}{z}^{n} \) correspond to the line containing \( \left( {{a}_{0},\cdots ,{a}_{n}}\right) \) . The sphere \( {S}^{2} \) we view as \( \mathbb{C} \cup  \{ \infty \} \) , and then we define \( f : {\left( {S}^{2}\right) }^{n} \rightarrow  {\mathbb{{CP}}}^{n} \) by setting \( f\left( {{a}_{1},\cdots ,{a}_{n}}\right)  = \left( {z + {a}_{1}}\right) \cdots \left( {z + {a}_{n}}\right) \) with factors \( z + \infty \) omitted, so in particular \( f\left( {\infty ,\cdots ,\infty }\right)  = 1 \) . To check that \( f \) is continuous, suppose some \( {a}_{i} \) approaches \( \infty \) , say \( {a}_{n} \) , and all the other \( {a}_{j} \) ’s are finite. Then if we write

\[
\left( {z + {a}_{1}}\right) \cdots \left( {z + {a}_{n}}\right)  =
\]

\[
{z}^{n} + \left( {{a}_{1} + \cdots  + {a}_{n}}\right) {z}^{n - 1} + \cdots  + \mathop{\sum }\limits_{{{i}_{1} < \cdots  < {i}_{k}}}{a}_{{i}_{1}}\cdots {a}_{{i}_{k}}{z}^{n - k} + \cdots  + {a}_{1}\cdots {a}_{n}
\]

we see that, dividing through by \( {a}_{n} \) and letting \( {a}_{n} \) approach \( \infty \) , this polynomial approaches \( {z}^{n - 1} + \left( {{a}_{1} + \cdots  + {a}_{n - 1}}\right) {z}^{n - 2} + \cdots  + {a}_{1}\cdots {a}_{n - 1} = \left( {z + {a}_{1}}\right) \cdots \left( {z + {a}_{n - 1}}\right) \) . The same argument would apply if several \( {a}_{i} \) ’s approach \( \infty \) simultaneously.

The value \( f\left( {{a}_{1},\cdots ,{a}_{n}}\right) \) is unchanged under permutation of the \( {a}_{i} \) ’s, so there is an induced map \( S{P}_{n}\left( {S}^{2}\right)  \rightarrow  {\mathbb{{CP}}}^{n} \) which is a continuous bijection, hence a homeomorphism since both spaces are compact Hausdorff. Letting \( n \) go to \( \infty \) , we then get a homeomorphism \( {SP}\left( {S}^{2}\right)  \approx  {\mathbb{{CP}}}^{\infty } \) .

The same argument can be used to show that \( S{P}_{n}\left( {S}^{1}\right)  \simeq  {S}^{1} \) for all \( n \) , including \( n = \infty \) . Namely, the argument shows that \( S{P}_{n}\left( {\mathbb{C}-\{ 0\} }\right) \) can be identified with the polynomials \( {z}^{n} + {a}_{n - 1}{z}^{n - 1} + \cdots  + {a}_{0} \) with \( {a}_{0} \neq  0 \) , or in other words, the \( n \) -tuples \( \left( {{a}_{0},\cdots ,{a}_{n - 1}}\right)  \in  {\mathbb{C}}^{n} \) with \( {a}_{0} \neq  0 \) , and this subspace of \( {\mathbb{C}}^{n} \) deformation retracts onto a circle.

The symmetric products of higher-dimensional spheres are more complicated, though things are not so bad for the 2-fold symmetric product:

Example 4K.5. Let us show that \( S{P}_{2}\left( {S}^{n}\right) \) is homeomorphic to the mapping cone of a map \( {S}^{n}{\mathbb{{RP}}}^{n - 1} \rightarrow  {S}^{n} \) where \( {S}^{n}{\mathbb{{RP}}}^{n - 1} \) is the \( n \) -fold unreduced suspension of \( {\mathbb{{RP}}}^{n - 1} \) . Hence \( {H}_{ * }\left( {S{P}_{2}\left( {S}^{n}\right) }\right)  \approx  {H}_{ * }\left( {S}^{n}\right)  \oplus  {\widetilde{H}}_{ * }\left( {{S}^{n + 1}{\mathbb{{RP}}}^{n - 1}}\right) \) from the long exact sequence of homology groups for the pair \( \left( {S{P}_{2}\left( {S}^{n}\right) ,{S}^{n}}\right) \) , since \( S{P}_{2}\left( {S}^{n}\right) /{S}^{n} \) is \( {S}^{n + 1}{\mathbb{{RP}}}^{n - 1} \) with no reduced homology below dimension \( n + 2 \) .

If we view \( {S}^{n} \) as \( {D}^{n}/\partial {D}^{n} \) , then \( S{P}_{2}\left( {S}^{n}\right) \) becomes a certain quotient of \( {D}^{n} \times  {D}^{n} \) . Viewing \( {D}^{n} \times  {D}^{n} \) as the cone on its boundary \( {D}^{n} \times  \partial {D}^{n} \cup  \partial {D}^{n} \times  {D}^{n} \) , the identifications that produce \( S{P}_{2}\left( {S}^{n}\right) \) respect the various concentric copies of this boundary which fill up the interior of \( {D}^{n} \times  {D}^{n} \) , so it suffices to analyze the identifications in all these copies of the boundary. The identifications on the boundary of \( {D}^{n} \times  {D}^{n} \) itself yield \( {S}^{n} \) . This is clear since the identification \( \left( {x, y}\right)  \sim  \left( {y, x}\right) \) converts \( {D}^{n} \times  \partial {D}^{n} \cup  \partial {D}^{n} \times  {D}^{n} \) to \( {D}^{n} \times  \partial {D}^{n} \) , and all points of \( \partial {D}^{n} \) are identified in \( {S}^{n} \) .

It remains to see that the identifications \( \left( {x, y}\right)  \sim  \left( {y, x}\right) \) on each concentric copy of the boundary in the interior of \( {D}^{n} \times  {D}^{n} \) produce \( {S}^{n}{\mathbb{{RP}}}^{n - 1} \) . Denote by \( Z \) the quotient of \( {D}^{n} \times  \partial {D}^{n} \cup  \partial {D}^{n} \times  {D}^{n} \) under these identifications. This is the same as the quotient of \( {D}^{n} \times  \partial {D}^{n} \) under the identifications \( \left( {x, y}\right)  \sim  \left( {y, x}\right) \) for \( \left( {x, y}\right)  \in  \partial {D}^{n} \times  \partial {D}^{n} \) .

Define \( f : {D}^{n} \times  {\mathbb{{RP}}}^{n - 1} \rightarrow  Z \) by \( f\left( {x, L}\right)  = \left( {w, z}\right) \) where \( x \) is equidistant from \( z \in  \partial {D}^{n} \) and \( w \in  {D}^{n} \) along the line through \( x \) parallel to \( L \) , as in the figure. If \( x \) is the midpoint of the segment \( z{z}^{\prime } \) then \( w = {z}^{\prime } \) and there is no way to distinguish between \( w \) and \( z \) , but since \( f \) takes values in the quotient space \( Z \) , this is not a problem. If \( x \in  \partial {D}^{n} \) then \( w = z = x \) , independent of \( L \) . If \( x \in  {D}^{n} - \partial {D}^{n} \) then \( w \neq  z \) , and conversely, given \( \left( {w, z}\right)  \in  {D}^{n} \times  \partial {D}^{n} \) with \( w \neq  z \) there is a unique \( \left( {x, L}\right) \) with \( f\left( {x, L}\right)  = \left( {w, z}\right) \) , namely \( x \) is the midpoint of the segment \( {wz} \) and \( L \) is the line parallel to this segment. In view of these remarks, we see that \( Z \) is the quotient space of \( {D}^{n} \times  {\mathbb{{RP}}}^{n - 1} \) under the identifications \( \left( {x, L}\right)  \sim  \left( {x,{L}^{\prime }}\right) \) if \( x \in  \partial {D}^{n} \) . This quotient is precisely \( {S}^{n}{\mathbb{{RP}}}^{n - 1} \) .

This example illustrates that passing from a CW structure on \( X \) to a CW structure on \( S{P}_{n}\left( X\right) \) or \( {SP}\left( X\right) \) is not at all straightforward. However, if \( X \) is a simplicial complex, there is a natural way of putting \( \Delta \) -complex structures on \( S{P}_{n}\left( X\right) \) and \( {SP}\left( X\right) \) , as follows. A simplicial complex structure on \( X \) gives a CW structure on the product of \( n \) copies of \( X \) , with cells \( n \) -fold products of simplices. Such a product has a canonical barycentric subdivision as a simplicial complex, with vertices the points whose coordinates are barycenters of simplices of \( X \) . By induction over skeleta, this just amounts to coning off a simplicial structure on the boundary of each product cell. This simplicial structure on the product of \( n \) copies of \( X \) is in fact a \( \Delta \) -complex structure since the vertices of each of its simplices have a natural ordering given by the dimensions of the cells of which they are barycenters. The action of the symmetric group permuting coordinates respects this \( \Delta \) -complex structure, taking simplices homeomorphically to simplices, preserving vertex-orderings, so there is an induced \( \Delta \) -complex structure on the quotient \( S{P}_{n}\left( X\right) \) . With the basepoint of \( X \) chosen to be a vertex, \( S{P}_{n}\left( X\right) \) is a subcomplex of \( S{P}_{n + 1}\left( X\right) \) so there is a natural \( \Delta \) -complex structure on the infinite symmetric product \( {SP}\left( X\right) \) as well.

As usual with products, the CW topology on \( S{P}_{n}\left( X\right) \) and \( {SP}\left( X\right) \) is in general different from the topology arising from the original definition in terms of product topologies, but one can check that the two topologies have the same compact sets, so the distinction will not matter for our present purposes. For definiteness, we will use the CW topology in what follows, which means restricting \( X \) to be a simplicial complex. Since every CW complex is homotopy equivalent to a simplicial complex by Theorem 2C.5, and \( S{P}_{n} \) and \( {SP} \) are homotopy functors, there is no essential loss of generality in restricting from CW complexes to simplicial complexes.

Here is the main result of this section, the Dold-Thom theorem:

Theorem 4K.6. The functor \( X \mapsto  {\pi }_{i}{SP}\left( X\right) \) for \( i \geq  1 \) coincides with the functor \( X \mapsto  {H}_{i}\left( {X;\mathbb{Z}}\right) \) on the category of basepointed connected CW complexes.

In particular this says that \( {SP}\left( {S}^{n}\right) \) is a \( K\left( {\mathbb{Z}, n}\right) \) , and more generally that for a Moore space \( M\left( {G, n}\right) ,{SP}\left( {M\left( {G, n}\right) }\right) \) is a \( K\left( {G, n}\right) \) .

The fact that \( {SP}\left( X\right) \) is a commutative, associative H-space with a strict identity element limits its weak homotopy type considerably:

Corollary 4K.7. A path-connected, commutative, associative H-space with a strict identity element has the weak homotopy type of a product of Eilenberg-MacLane spaces.

In particular, if \( X \) is a connected CW complex then \( {SP}\left( X\right) \) is path-connected and has the weak homotopy type of \( \mathop{\prod }\limits_{n}K\left( {{H}_{n}\left( X\right) , n}\right) \) . Thus the functor \( {SP} \) essentially reduces to Eilenberg-MacLane spaces.

Proof: Let \( X \) be a path-connected, commutative, associative H-space with a strict identity element, and let \( {G}_{n} = {\pi }_{n}\left( X\right) \) . By Lemma 4.31 there is a map \( M\left( {{G}_{n}, n}\right)  \rightarrow  X \) inducing an isomorphism on \( {\pi }_{n} \) when \( n > 1 \) and an isomorphism on \( {H}_{1} \) when \( n = 1 \) . We can take these maps to be basepoint-preserving, and then they combine to give a map \( \mathop{\bigvee }\limits_{n}M\left( {{G}_{n}, n}\right)  \rightarrow  X \) . The very special H-space structure on \( X \) allows us to extend this to a homomorphism \( f : {SP}\left( {\mathop{\bigvee }\limits_{n}M\left( {{G}_{n}, n}\right) }\right)  \rightarrow  X \) . In general, \( {SP}\left( {\mathop{\bigvee }\limits_{\alpha }{X}_{\alpha }}\right) \) can be identified with \( \mathop{\prod }\limits_{\alpha }{SP}\left( {X}_{\alpha }\right) \) where this is the ’weak’ infinite product, the union of the finite products. This, together with the general fact that the map \( {\pi }_{i}\left( X\right)  \rightarrow  {\pi }_{i}{SP}\left( X\right)  = {H}_{i}\left( {X;\mathbb{Z}}\right) \) induced by the inclusion \( X = S{P}_{1}\left( X\right)  \hookrightarrow  {SP}\left( X\right) \) is the Hurewicz homomorphism, as we will see at the end of the proof of the Dold-Thom theorem, implies that the map \( f \) induces an isomorphism on all homotopy groups. Thus we have a weak homotopy equivalence \( \mathop{\prod }\limits_{n}{SP}\left( {M\left( {{G}_{n}, n}\right) }\right)  \rightarrow  X \) , and as we noted above, \( {SP}\left( {M\left( {{G}_{n}, n}\right) }\right) \) is a \( K\left( {{G}_{n}, n}\right) \) . Finally, since each factor \( {SP}\left( {M\left( {{G}_{n}, n}\right) }\right) \) has only one nontrivial homotopy group, the weak infinite product has the same weak homotopy type as the ordinary infinite product.

The main step in the proof of the theorem will be to show that for a simplicial pair \( \left( {X, A}\right) \) with both \( X \) and \( A \) connected, there is a long exact sequence

\[
\cdots  \rightarrow  {\pi }_{i}{SP}\left( A\right)  \rightarrow  {\pi }_{i}{SP}\left( X\right)  \rightarrow  {\pi }_{i}{SP}\left( {X/A}\right)  \rightarrow  {\pi }_{i - 1}{SP}\left( A\right)  \rightarrow  \cdots
\]

This would follow if the maps \( {SP}\left( A\right)  \rightarrow  {SP}\left( X\right)  \rightarrow  {SP}\left( {X/A}\right) \) formed a fiber bundle or fibration. There is some reason to think this might be true, because all the fibers of the projection \( {SP}\left( X\right)  \rightarrow  {SP}\left( {X/A}\right) \) are homeomorphic to \( {SP}\left( A\right) \) . In fact, in terms of the H-space structure on \( {SP}\left( X\right) \) as the free abelian monoid generated by \( X \) , the fibers are exactly the cosets of the submonoid \( {SP}\left( A\right) \) . The projection \( {SP}\left( X\right)  \rightarrow  {SP}\left( {X/A}\right) \) , however, fails to have the homotopy lifting property, even the special case of lifting paths. For if \( {x}_{t}, t \in  \lbrack 0,1) \) , is a path in \( X - A \) approaching a point \( {x}_{1} = a \in  A \) other than the basepoint, then regarding \( {x}_{t} \) as a path in \( {SP}\left( {X/A}\right) \) , any lift to \( {SP}\left( X\right) \) would have the form \( {x}_{t}{\alpha }_{t},{\alpha }_{t} \in  {SP}\left( A\right) \) , ending at \( {x}_{1}{\alpha }_{1} = a{\alpha }_{1} \) , a point of \( {SP}\left( A\right) \) which is a multiple of \( a \) , so in particular there would be no lift ending at the basepoint of \( {SP}\left( X\right) \) .

What we will show is that the projection \( {SP}\left( X\right)  \rightarrow  {SP}\left( {X/A}\right) \) has instead the weaker structure of a quasifibration, which is still good enough to deduce a long exact sequence of homotopy groups.

Proof of 4K.6: As we have said, the main step will be to associate a long exact sequence of homotopy groups to each simplicial pair \( \left( {X, A}\right) \) with \( X \) and \( A \) connected. This will be the long exact sequence of homotopy groups coming from the quasifibration \( {SP}\left( A\right)  \rightarrow  {SP}\left( X\right)  \rightarrow  {SP}\left( {X/A}\right) \) , so the major work will be in verifying the quasifibration property. Since \( {SP} \) is a homotopy functor, we are free to replace \( \left( {X, A}\right) \) by a homotopy equivalent pair, so let us replace \( \left( {X, A}\right) \) by \( \left( {M, A}\right) \) where \( M \) is the mapping cylinder of the inclusion \( A \hookrightarrow  X \) . This new pair, which we still call \( \left( {X, A}\right) \) , has some slight technical advantages, as we will see later in the proof.

To begin the proof that the projection \( p : {SP}\left( X\right)  \rightarrow  {SP}\left( {X/A}\right) \) is a quasifibration, let \( {B}_{n} = S{P}_{n}\left( {X/A}\right) \) and \( {E}_{n} = {p}^{-1}\left( {B}_{n}\right) \) . Thus \( {E}_{n} \) consists of those points in \( {SP}\left( X\right) \) having at most \( n \) coordinates in \( X - A \) . By Lemma 4K.3(b) it suffices to show that \( p : {E}_{n} \rightarrow  {B}_{n} \) is a quasifibration. The proof of the latter fact will be by induction on \( n \) , starting with the trivial case \( n = 0 \) when \( {B}_{0} \) is a point. The induction step will consist of showing that \( p \) is a quasifibration over a neighborhood of \( {B}_{n - 1} \) and over \( {B}_{n} - {B}_{n - 1} \) , then applying Lemma 4K.3(a). We first tackle the problem of showing the quasifibration property over a neighborhood of \( {B}_{n - 1} \) .

Let \( {f}_{t} : X \rightarrow  X \) be a homotopy of the identity map deformation retracting a neighborhood \( N \) of \( A \) onto \( A \) . Since we have replaced the original \( X \) by the mapping cylinder of the inclusion \( A \hookrightarrow  X \) , we can take \( {f}_{t} \) simply to slide points along the segments \( \{ a\}  \times  I \) in the mapping cylinder, with \( N = A \times  \left\lbrack  {0,1/2}\right) \) . Let \( U \subset  {E}_{n} \) consist of those points having at least one coordinate in \( N \) , or in other words, products with at least one factor in \( N \) . Thus \( U \) is a neighborhood of \( {E}_{n - 1} \) in \( {E}_{n} \) and \( p\left( U\right) \) is a neighborhood of \( {B}_{n - 1} \) in \( {B}_{n} \) .

The homotopy \( {f}_{t} \) induces a homotopy \( {F}_{t} : {E}_{n} \rightarrow  {E}_{n} \) whose restriction to \( U \) is a deformation of \( U \) into \( {E}_{n - 1} \) , where by ’deformation’ we mean deformation retraction in the weak sense. Since \( {f}_{t} \) is the identity on \( A,{F}_{t} \) is the lift of a homotopy \( {\bar{F}}_{t} : {B}_{n} \rightarrow  {B}_{n} \) which restricts to a deformation of \( \bar{U} = p\left( U\right) \) into \( {B}_{n - 1} \) . We will deduce that the projection \( U \rightarrow  \bar{U} \) is a quasifibration by using Lemma 4K.3(c). To apply this to the case at hand we need to verify that \( {F}_{1} : {p}^{-1}\left( b\right)  \rightarrow  {p}^{-1}\left( {{\bar{F}}_{1}\left( b\right) }\right) \) is a weak equivalence for all \( b \) . Each point \( w \in  {p}^{-1}\left( b\right) \) is a commuting product of points in \( X \) . Let \( \widehat{w} \) be the subproduct whose factors are points in \( X - A \) , so we have \( w = \widehat{w}v \) for \( v \) a product of points in \( A \) . Since \( {f}_{1} \) is the identity on \( A \) and \( {F}_{1} \) is a homomorphism, we have \( {F}_{1}\left( w\right)  = {F}_{1}\left( \widehat{w}\right) v \) , which can be written \( \widehat{{F}_{1}\left( \widehat{w}\right) }{v}^{\prime }v \) with \( {v}^{\prime } \) also a product of points in \( A \) . If we fix \( \widehat{w} \) and let \( v \) vary over \( {SP}\left( A\right) \) , we get all points of \( {p}^{-1}\left( b\right) \) exactly once, or in other words, we have \( {p}^{-1}\left( b\right) \) expressed as the coset \( \widehat{w}{SP}\left( A\right) \) . The map \( {F}_{1},\widehat{w}v \mapsto  {F}_{1}\left( \widehat{w}\right) {v}^{\prime }v \) , takes this coset to the coset \( {F}_{1}\left( \widehat{w}\right) {SP}\left( A\right) \) by a map that would be a homeomorphism if the factor \( {v}^{\prime } \) were not present. Since \( A \) is connected, there is a path \( {v}_{t}^{\prime } \) from \( {v}^{\prime } \) to the basepoint, and so by replacing \( {v}^{\prime } \) with \( {v}_{t}^{\prime } \) in the product \( {F}_{1}\left( \widehat{w}\right) {v}^{\prime }v \) we obtain a homotopy from \( {F}_{1} : {p}^{-1}\left( b\right)  \rightarrow  {p}^{-1}\left( {{\bar{F}}_{1}\left( b\right) }\right) \) to a homeomorphism, so this map is a homotopy equivalence, as desired.

It remains to see that \( p \) is a quasifibration over \( {B}_{n} - {B}_{n - 1} \) and over the intersection of this set with \( \bar{U} \) . The argument will be the same in both cases.

Identifying \( {B}_{n} - {B}_{n - 1} \) with \( S{P}_{n}\left( {X - A}\right) \) , the projection \( p : {E}_{n} - {E}_{n - 1} \rightarrow  {B}_{n} - {B}_{n - 1} \) is the same as the operator \( w \mapsto  \widehat{w} \) . The inclusion \( S{P}_{n}\left( {X - A}\right)  \hookrightarrow  {E}_{n} - {E}_{n - 1} \) gives a section for \( p : {E}_{n} - {E}_{n - 1} \rightarrow  {B}_{n} - {B}_{n - 1} \) , so \( {p}_{ * } : {\pi }_{i}\left( {{E}_{n} - {E}_{n - 1},{p}^{-1}\left( b\right) }\right)  \rightarrow  {\pi }_{i}\left( {{B}_{n} - {B}_{n - 1}, b}\right) \) is surjective. To see that it is also injective, represent an element of its kernel by a map \( g : \left( {{D}^{i},\partial {D}^{i}}\right)  \rightarrow  \left( {{E}_{n} - {E}_{n - 1},{p}^{-1}\left( b\right) }\right) \) . A nullhomotopy of \( p\mathcal{g} \) gives a homotopy of \( g \) changing only its coordinates in \( X - A \) . This homotopy is through maps \( \left( {{D}^{i},\partial {D}^{i}}\right)  \rightarrow  \left( {{E}_{n} - {E}_{n - 1},{p}^{-1}\left( b\right) }\right) \) , and ends with a map to \( {p}^{-1}\left( b\right) \) , so the kernel of \( {p}_{ * } \) is trivial. Thus the projection \( {E}_{n} - {E}_{n - 1} \rightarrow  {B}_{n} - {B}_{n - 1} \) is a quasifibration, at least if \( {B}_{n} - {B}_{n - 1} \) is path-connected. But by replacing the original \( X \) with the mapping cylinder of the inclusion \( A \hookrightarrow  X \) , we guarantee that \( X - A \) is path-connected since it deformation retracts onto \( X \) . Hence the space \( {B}_{n} - {B}_{n - 1} = S{P}_{n}\left( {X - A}\right) \) is also path-connected.

This argument works equally well over any open subset of \( {B}_{n} - {B}_{n - 1} \) that is path-connected, in particular over \( U \cap  \left( {{B}_{n} - {B}_{n - 1}}\right) \) , so via Lemma 4K.3(a) this finishes the proof that \( {SP}\left( A\right)  \rightarrow  {SP}\left( X\right)  \rightarrow  {SP}\left( {X/A}\right) \) is a quasifibration.

Since the homotopy axiom is obvious, this gives us the first two of the three axioms needed for the groups \( {h}_{i}\left( X\right)  = {\pi }_{i}{SP}\left( X\right) \) to define a reduced homology theory. There remains only the wedge sum axiom, \( {h}_{i}\left( {\mathop{\bigvee }\limits_{\alpha }{X}_{\alpha }}\right)  \approx  {\bigoplus }_{\alpha }{h}_{i}\left( {X}_{\alpha }\right) \) , but this is immediate from the evident fact that \( {SP}\left( {\mathop{\bigvee }\limits_{\alpha }{X}_{\alpha }}\right)  = \mathop{\prod }\limits_{\alpha }{SP}\left( {X}_{\alpha }\right) \) , where this is the ’weak’ product, the union of the products of finitely many factors.

The homology theory \( {h}_{ * }\left( X\right) \) is defined on the category of connected, basepointed simplicial complexes, with basepoint-preserving maps. The coefficients of this homology theory, the groups \( {h}_{i}\left( {S}^{n}\right) \) , are the same as for ordinary homology with \( \mathbb{Z} \) coefficients since we know this is true for \( n = 2 \) by the homeomorphism \( {SP}\left( {S}^{2}\right)  \approx  {\mathbb{{CP}}}^{\infty } \) , and there are isomorphisms \( {h}_{i}\left( X\right)  \approx  {h}_{i + 1}\left( {\sum X}\right) \) in any reduced homology theory. If the homology theory \( {h}_{ * }\left( X\right) \) were defined on the category of all simplicial complexes, without basepoints, then Theorem 4.59 would give natural isomorphisms \( {h}_{i}\left( X\right)  \approx  {H}_{i}\left( {X;\mathbb{Z}}\right) \) for all \( X \) , and the proof would be complete. However, it is easy to achieve this by defining a new homology theory \( {h}_{i}^{\prime }\left( X\right)  = {h}_{i + 1}\left( {\sum X}\right) \) , since the suspension of an arbitrary complex is connected and the suspension of an arbitrary map is basepoint-preserving, taking the basepoint to be one of the suspension points. Since \( {h}_{i}^{\prime }\left( X\right) \) is naturally isomorphic to \( {h}_{i}\left( X\right) \) if \( X \) is connected, we are done.

It is worth noting that the map \( {\pi }_{i}\left( X\right)  \rightarrow  {\pi }_{i}{SP}\left( X\right)  = {H}_{i}\left( {X;\mathbb{Z}}\right) \) induced by the inclusion \( X = S{P}_{1}\left( X\right)  \hookrightarrow  {SP}\left( X\right) \) is the Hurewicz homomorphism. For by definition of the Hurewicz homomorphism and naturality this reduces to the case \( X = {S}^{i} \) , where the map \( S{P}_{1}\left( {S}^{i}\right)  \hookrightarrow  {SP}\left( {S}^{i}\right) \) induces on \( {\pi }_{i} \) a homomorphism \( \mathbb{Z} \rightarrow  \mathbb{Z} \) , which one just has to check is an isomorphism, the Hurewicz homomorphism being determined only up to sign. The suspension isomorphism gives a further reduction to the case \( i = 1 \) , where the inclusion \( S{P}_{1}\left( {S}^{1}\right)  \hookrightarrow  {SP}\left( {S}^{1}\right) \) is a homotopy equivalence, hence induces an isomorphism on \( {\pi }_{1} \) .
