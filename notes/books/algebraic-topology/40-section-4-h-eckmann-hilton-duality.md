# 4.H Eckmann-Hilton Duality

There is a very nice duality principle in homotopy theory, called Eckmann-Hilton duality in its more refined and systematic aspects, but which in its most basic form involves the simple idea of reversing the direction of all arrows in a given construction. For example, if in the definition of a fibration as a map satisfying the homotopy lifting property we reverse the direction of all the arrows, we obtain the dual notion of a cofibration. This is a map \( i : A \rightarrow  B \) satisfying the following property: Given \( {\widetilde{g}}_{0} : B \rightarrow  X \) and a homotopy \( {g}_{t} : A \rightarrow  X \) such that \( {\widetilde{g}}_{0}i = {g}_{0} \) , there exists a homotopy \( {\widetilde{g}}_{t} : B \rightarrow  X \) such that \( {\widetilde{g}}_{t}i = {g}_{t} \) . In the special case that \( i \) is the inclusion of a subspace, this is the homotopy extension property, and the next proposition says that this is indeed the general case. So a cofibration is the same as an inclusion satisfying the homotopy extension property.

![bo_d7dtv0s91nqc73eq8nv0_468_1359_1830_221_157_0.jpg](images/bo_d7dtv0s91nqc73eq8nv0_468_1359_1830_221_157_0.jpg)

Proposition 4H.1. If \( i : A \rightarrow  B \) is a cofibration, then \( i \) is injective, and in fact a homeomorphism onto its image.

Proof: Consider the mapping cylinder \( {M}_{i} \) , the quotient of \( A \times  I \coprod  B \) in which \( \left( {a,1}\right) \) is identified with \( i\left( a\right) \) . Let \( {g}_{t} : A \rightarrow  {M}_{i} \) be the homotopy mapping \( a \in  A \) to the image of \( \left( {a,1 - t}\right)  \in  A \times  I \) in \( {M}_{i} \) , and let \( {\widetilde{g}}_{0} \) be the inclusion \( B \hookrightarrow  {M}_{i} \) . The cofibration property gives \( {\widetilde{g}}_{t} : B \rightarrow  {M}_{i} \) with \( {\widetilde{g}}_{t}i = {g}_{t} \) . Restricting to a fixed \( t > 0 \) , this implies \( i \) is injective since \( {g}_{t} \) is. Furthermore, since \( {g}_{t} \) is a homeomorphism onto its image \( A \times  \{ 1 - t\} \) , the relation \( {\widetilde{g}}_{t}i = {g}_{t} \) implies that the map \( {g}_{t}^{-1}{\widetilde{g}}_{t} : i\left( A\right)  \rightarrow  A \) is a continuous inverse of \( i : A \rightarrow  i\left( A\right) \) .

Many constructions for fibrations have analogs for cofibrations, and vice versa. For example, for an arbitrary map \( f : A \rightarrow  B \) the inclusion \( A \hookrightarrow  {M}_{f} \) is readily seen to be a cofibration, so the analog of the factorization \( A \hookrightarrow  {E}_{f} \rightarrow  B \) of \( f \) into a homotopy equivalence followed by a fibration is the factorization \( A \hookrightarrow  {M}_{f} \rightarrow  B \) into a cofibration followed by a homotopy equivalence. Even the definition of \( {M}_{f} \) is in some way dual to the definition of \( {E}_{f} \) , since \( {E}_{f} \) can be defined as a pullback and \( {M}_{f} \) can be defined as a dual pushout. In general, the pushout of maps \( f : Z \rightarrow  X \) and \( g : Z \rightarrow  Y \) is defined as the quotient of \( X \coprod  Y \) under the identifications \( f\left( z\right)  \sim  g\left( z\right) \) . Thus the pushout is a quotient of \( X \coprod  Y \) , while the pullback of maps \( X \rightarrow  Z \) and \( Y \rightarrow  Z \) is a subobject of \( X \times  Y \) , so we see here two instances of duality: a duality between disjoint union and product, and a duality between subobjects and quotients. The first of these is easily explained, since a collection of maps \( {X}_{\alpha } \rightarrow  X \) is equivalent to a map \( \mathop{\coprod }\limits_{\alpha }{X}_{\alpha } \rightarrow  X \) , while a collection of maps \( X \rightarrow  {X}_{\alpha } \) is equivalent to a map \( X \rightarrow  \mathop{\prod }\limits_{\alpha }{X}_{\alpha } \) . The notation II for the 'coproduct' was chosen to indicate that it is dual to \( \Pi \) . If we were dealing with basepointed spaces and maps, the coproduct would be wedge sum. In the category of abelian groups the coproduct is direct sum.

![bo_d7dtv0s91nqc73eq8nv0_469_1045_901_541_155_0.jpg](images/bo_d7dtv0s91nqc73eq8nv0_469_1045_901_541_155_0.jpg)

The duality between subobjects and quotient objects is clear for abelian groups, where subobjects are kernels and quotient objects cokernels. The strict topological analog of a kernel is a fiber of a fibration. Dually, the topological analog of a cokernel is the cofiber \( B/A \) of a cofibration \( A \hookrightarrow  B \) . If we make an arbitrary map \( f : A \rightarrow  B \) into a cofibration \( A \hookrightarrow  {M}_{f} \) , the cofiber is the mapping cone \( {C}_{f} = {M}_{f}/\left( {A\times \{ 0\} }\right) \) .

In the diagram showing \( {E}_{f} \) and \( {M}_{f} \) as pullback and pushout, there also appears to be some sort of duality involving the terms \( A \times  I \) and \( {B}^{I} \) . This leads us to ask whether \( X \times  I \) and \( {X}^{I} \) are in some way dual. Indeed, if we ignore topology and just think set-theoretically, this is an instance of the familiar product-coproduct duality since the product of copies of \( X \) indexed by \( I \) is \( {X}^{I} \) , all functions \( I \rightarrow  X \) , while the coproduct of copies of \( X \) indexed by \( I \) is \( X \times  I \) , the disjoint union of the sets \( X \times  \{ t\} \) for \( t \in  I \) . Switching back from the category of sets to the topological category, we can view \( {X}^{I} \) as a ’continuous product’ of copies of \( X \) and \( X \times  I \) as a ’continuous coproduct’.

On a less abstract level, the fact that maps \( A \times  I \rightarrow  B \) are the same as maps \( A \rightarrow  {B}^{I} \) indicates a certain duality between \( A \times  I \) and \( {B}^{I} \) . This leads to a duality between suspension and loopspace, since \( {\sum A} \) is a quotient of \( A \times  I \) and \( {\Omega B} \) is a subspace of \( {B}^{I} \) . This duality is expressed in the adjoint relation \( \langle {\sum X}, Y\rangle  = \langle X,{\Omega Y}\rangle \) from \( \text{ § }{4.3} \) . Combining this duality between \( \sum \) and \( \Omega \) with the duality between fibers and cofibers, we see a duality relationship between the fibration and cofibration sequences of §4.3:

\[
\cdots  \rightarrow  {\Omega F} \rightarrow  {\Omega E} \rightarrow  {\Omega B} \rightarrow  F \rightarrow  E \rightarrow  B
\]

\[
A \rightarrow  X \rightarrow  X/A \rightarrow  {\sum A} \rightarrow  {\sum X} \rightarrow  \sum \left( {X/A}\right)  \rightarrow  \cdots
\]

Pushout and pullback constructions can be generalized to arbitrary diagrams. In the case of pushouts, this was done in §4.G where we associated a space \( \square X \) to a diagram of spaces \( X \) . This was the quotient of the coproduct \( \mathop{\coprod }\limits_{v}{X}_{v} \) , with \( v \) ranging over vertices of the diagram, under the identifications \( x \sim  {f}_{e}\left( x\right) \) for all maps \( {f}_{e} \) associated to edges \( e \) of the diagram. The dual construction \( {\Pi X} \) would be the subspace of the product \( \mathop{\prod }\limits_{v}{X}_{v} \) consisting of tuples \( \left( {x}_{v}\right) \) with \( {f}_{e}\left( {x}_{v}\right)  = {x}_{w} \) for all maps \( {f}_{e} : {X}_{v} \rightarrow  {X}_{w} \) in the diagram. Perhaps more useful in algebraic topology is the homotopy variant of this notion obtained by dualizing the definition of \( {\Delta X} \) in the previous section. This is the space \( \nabla X \) consisting of all choices of a point \( {x}_{v} \) in each \( {X}_{v} \) and a path \( {\gamma }_{e} \) in the target space of each edge map \( {f}_{e} : {X}_{v} \rightarrow  {X}_{w} \) , with \( {\gamma }_{e}\left( 0\right)  = f\left( {x}_{v}\right) \) and \( {\gamma }_{e}\left( 1\right)  = {x}_{w} \) . The subspace with all paths constant is \( {\Pi X} \) . In the case of a diagram \( \cdots  \rightarrow  {X}_{2} \rightarrow  {X}_{1} \) such as a Postnikov tower this construction gives something slightly different from simply turning each successive map into a fibration via the usual pathspace construction, starting with \( {X}_{2} \rightarrow  {X}_{1} \) and proceeding up the tower, as we did in §4.3. The latter construction is rather the dual of an iterated mapping cylinder, involving spaces of maps \( {\Delta }^{n} \rightarrow  {X}_{v} \) instead of simply pathspaces. One could use such mapping spaces to generalize the definition of \( \nabla X \) from diagrams of spaces to complexes of spaces.

As special cases of the constructions \( \sqcup  X \) and \( \sqcap  X \) we have direct limits and inverse limits for diagrams \( {X}_{0} \rightarrow  {X}_{1} \rightarrow  \cdots \) and \( \cdots  \rightarrow  {X}_{1} \rightarrow  {X}_{0} \) , respectively. Since inverse limit is related to product and direct limit to coproduct, it is common practice in some circles to use reverse logic and call inverse limit simply 'limit' and direct limit ’colimit’. The homotopy versions are then called ’holim’ for \( \nabla X \) and ’hocolim’ for \( {\Delta X} \) . This terminology is frequently used for more general diagrams as well.

## Homotopy Groups with Coefficients

There is a somewhat deeper duality between homotopy groups and cohomology, which one can see in the fact that cohomology groups are homotopy classes of maps into a space with a single nonzero homotopy group, while homotopy groups are homotopy classes of maps from a space with a single nonzero cohomology group. This duality is in one respect incomplete, however, in that the cohomology statement holds for an arbitrary coefficient group, but we have not yet defined homotopy groups with coefficients. In view of the duality, one would be tempted to define \( {\pi }_{n}\left( {X;G}\right) \) to be the set of basepoint-preserving homotopy classes of maps from the cohomology analog of a Moore space \( M\left( {G, n}\right) \) to \( X \) . The cohomology analog of \( M\left( {G, n}\right) \) would be a space

\( Y \) whose only nonzero cohomology group \( {\widetilde{H}}^{i}\left( {Y;\mathbb{Z}}\right) \) is \( G \) for \( i = n \) . Unfortunately, such a space does not exist for arbitrary \( G \) , for example for \( G = \mathbb{Q} \) , since we showed in Proposition 3F.12 that if the cohomology groups of a space are all countable, then they are all finitely generated.

As a first approximation to \( {\pi }_{n}\left( {X;G}\right) \) let us consider \( \langle M\left( {G, n}\right) , X\rangle \) , the set of basepoint-preserving homotopy classes of maps \( M\left( {G, n}\right)  \rightarrow  X \) . To give this set a more suggestive name, let us call it \( {\mu }_{n}\left( {X;G}\right) \) . We should assume \( n > 1 \) to guarantee that the homotopy type of \( M\left( {G, n}\right) \) is well-defined, as shown in Example 4.34. For \( n > 1,{\mu }_{n}\left( {X;G}\right) \) is a group since we can choose \( M\left( {G, n}\right) \) to be the suspension of an \( M\left( {G, n - 1}\right) \) . And if \( n > 2 \) then \( {\mu }_{n}\left( {X;G}\right) \) is abelian since we can choose \( M\left( {G, n}\right) \) to be a double suspension.

There is something like a universal coefficient theorem for these groups \( {\mu }_{n}\left( {X;G}\right) \) :

Proposition 4H.2. For \( n > 1 \) there are natural short exact sequences

\[
0 \rightarrow  \operatorname{Ext}\left( {G,{\pi }_{n + 1}\left( X\right) }\right)  \rightarrow  {\mu }_{n}\left( {X;G}\right)  \rightarrow  \operatorname{Hom}\left( {G,{\pi }_{n}\left( X\right) }\right)  \rightarrow  0.
\]

The similarity with the universal coefficient theorem for cohomology is apparent, but with a reversal of the variables in the Ext and Hom terms, reflecting the fact that \( {\mu }_{n}\left( {X;G}\right) \) is covariant as a functor of \( X \) and contravariant as a functor of \( G \) , just like the Ext and Hom terms.

Proof: Let \( 0 \rightarrow  R\overset{i}{ \rightarrow  }F \rightarrow  G \rightarrow  0 \) be a free resolution of \( G \) . The inclusion map \( i \) is realized by a map \( M\left( {R, n}\right)  \rightarrow  M\left( {F, n}\right) \) , where \( M\left( {R, n}\right) \) and \( M\left( {F, n}\right) \) are wedges of \( {S}^{n} \) ’s corresponding to bases for \( F \) and \( R \) . Converting this map into a cofibration via the mapping cylinder, the cofiber is an \( M\left( {G, n}\right) \) , as one sees from the long exact sequence of homology groups. As in §4.3, the cofibration sequence

\[
M\left( {R, n}\right)  \rightarrow  M\left( {F, n}\right)  \rightarrow  M\left( {G, n}\right)  \rightarrow  M\left( {R, n + 1}\right)  \rightarrow  M\left( {F, n + 1}\right)
\]

gives rise to the exact sequence across the top of the following diagram:

\[
{\mu }_{n + 1}\left( {X;F}\right)  \rightarrow  {\mu }_{n + 1}\left( {X;R}\right)  \rightarrow  {\mu }_{n}\left( {X;G}\right)  \rightarrow  {\mu }_{n}\left( {X;F}\right)  \rightarrow  {\mu }_{n}\left( {X;R}\right)
\]

The four outer terms of the exact sequence can be identified with the indicated Hom terms since mapping a wedge sum of \( {S}^{n} \) ’s into \( X \) amounts to choosing an element of \( {\pi }_{n}\left( X\right) \) for each wedge summand. The kernel and cokernel of \( {i}^{ * } \) are \( \operatorname{Hom}\left( {G, - }\right) \) and \( \operatorname{Ext}\left( {G, - }\right) \) by definition, and so we obtain the short exact sequence we are looking for. Naturality will be left for the reader to verify.

Unlike in the universal coefficient theorems for homology and cohomology, the short exact sequence in this proposition does not split in general. For an example, take \( G = {\mathbb{Z}}_{2} \) and \( X = M\left( {{\mathbb{Z}}_{2}, n}\right) \) for \( n \geq  2 \) , where the identity map of \( M\left( {{\mathbb{Z}}_{2}, n}\right) \) defines an element of \( {\mu }_{n}\left( {M\left( {{\mathbb{Z}}_{2}, n}\right) ;{\mathbb{Z}}_{2}}\right)  = \left\langle  {M\left( {{\mathbb{Z}}_{2}, n}\right) , M\left( {{\mathbb{Z}}_{2}, n}\right) }\right\rangle \) having order 4, as we show in Example 4L.7, whereas the two outer terms in the short exact sequence can only contain elements of order 2 since \( G = {\mathbb{Z}}_{2} \) . This example shows also that \( {\mu }_{n}\left( {X;{\mathbb{Z}}_{m}}\right) \) need not be a module over \( {\mathbb{Z}}_{m} \) , as homology and cohomology groups with \( {\mathbb{Z}}_{m} \) coefficients are.

The proposition implies that the first nonzero \( {\mu }_{i}\left( {{S}^{n};{\mathbb{Z}}_{m}}\right) \) is \( {\mu }_{n - 1}\left( {{S}^{n};{\mathbb{Z}}_{m}}\right)  = {\mathbb{Z}}_{m} \) , from the Ext term. This result would look more reasonable if we changed notation to replace the subscript \( n - 1 \) by \( n \) . So let us make the definition

\[
{\pi }_{n}\left( {X;{\mathbb{Z}}_{m}}\right)  = \left\langle  {M\left( {{\mathbb{Z}}_{m}, n - 1}\right) , X}\right\rangle   = {\mu }_{n - 1}\left( {X;{\mathbb{Z}}_{m}}\right)
\]

There are two good reasons to expect this to be the right definition. The first is formal: \( M\left( {{\mathbb{Z}}_{m}, n - 1}\right) \) is a ’cohomology \( M\left( {{\mathbb{Z}}_{m}, n}\right) \) ’ since its only nontrivial cohomology group \( {\widetilde{H}}^{i}\left( {M\left( {{\mathbb{Z}}_{m}, n - 1}\right) ;\mathbb{Z}}\right) \) is \( {\mathbb{Z}}_{m} \) in dimension \( n \) . The second reason is more geometric: Elements of \( {\pi }_{n}\left( {X;{\mathbb{Z}}_{m}}\right) \) should be homotopy classes of ’homotopy-theoretic cycles \( {\;\operatorname{mod}\;m} \) ’, meaning maps \( {D}^{n} \rightarrow  X \) whose boundary is not necessarily a constant map as would be the case for \( {\pi }_{n}\left( X\right) \) , but rather whose boundary is \( m \) times a cycle \( {S}^{n - 1} \rightarrow  X \) . This is precisely what a map \( M\left( {{\mathbb{Z}}_{m}, n - 1}\right)  \rightarrow  X \) is, if we choose \( M\left( {{\mathbb{Z}}_{m}, n - 1}\right) \) to be \( {S}^{n - 1} \) with a cell \( {e}^{n} \) attached by a map of degree \( m \) .

Besides the calculation \( {\pi }_{n}\left( {{S}^{n};{\mathbb{Z}}_{m}}\right)  \approx  {\mathbb{Z}}_{m} \) , the proposition also yields an isomorphism \( {\pi }_{n}\left( {M\left( {{\mathbb{Z}}_{m}, n}\right) ;{\mathbb{Z}}_{m}}\right)  \approx  \operatorname{Ext}\left( {{\mathbb{Z}}_{m},{\mathbb{Z}}_{m}}\right)  = {\mathbb{Z}}_{m} \) . Both these results are in fact special cases of a Hurewicz-type theorem relating \( {\pi }_{n}\left( {X;{\mathbb{Z}}_{m}}\right) \) and \( {H}_{n}\left( {X;{\mathbb{Z}}_{m}}\right) \) , which is proved in [Neisendorfer 1980].

Along with \( \mathbb{Z} \) and \( {\mathbb{Z}}_{m} \) , another extremely useful coefficient group for homology and cohomology is \( \mathbb{Q} \) . We pointed out above the difficulty that there is no cohomology analog of \( M\left( {\mathbb{Q}, n}\right) \) . The groups \( {\mu }_{n}\left( {X;\mathbb{Q}}\right) \) are also problematic. For example the proposition gives \( {\mu }_{n - 1}\left( {{S}^{n};\mathbb{Q}}\right)  \approx  \operatorname{Ext}\left( {\mathbb{Q},\mathbb{Z}}\right) \) , which is a somewhat complicated uncountable group as we showed in \( \text{ § }3 \) .F. However, there is an alternative approach that turns out to work rather well. One defines rational homotopy groups simply as \( {\pi }_{n}\left( X\right)  \otimes  \mathbb{Q} \) , analogous to the isomorphism \( {H}_{n}\left( {X;\mathbb{Q}}\right)  \approx  {H}_{n}\left( {X;\mathbb{Z}}\right)  \otimes  \mathbb{Q} \) from the universal coefficient theorem for homology. See [SSAT] for more on this.

## Homology Decompositions

Eckmann-Hilton duality can be extremely helpful as an organizational principle, reducing significantly what one has to remember, and providing valuable hints on how to proceed in various situations. To illustrate, let us consider what would happen if we dualized the notion of a Postnikov tower of principal fibrations, where a space is represented as an inverse limit of a sequence of fibers of maps to Eilenberg-MacLane spaces. In the dual representation, a space would be realized as a direct limit of a sequence of cofibers of maps from Moore spaces.

In more detail, suppose we are given a sequence of abelian groups \( {G}_{n}, n \geq  1 \) , and we build a CW complex \( X \) with \( {H}_{n}\left( X\right)  \approx  {G}_{n} \) for all \( n \) by constructing inductively an increasing sequence of subcomplexes \( {X}_{1} \subset  {X}_{2} \subset  \cdots \) with \( {H}_{i}\left( {X}_{n}\right)  \approx  {G}_{i} \) for \( i \leq  n \) and \( {H}_{i}\left( {X}_{n}\right)  = 0 \) for \( i > n \) , where:

(1) \( {X}_{1} \) is a Moore space \( M\left( {{G}_{1},1}\right) \) .

(2) \( {X}_{n + 1} \) is the mapping cone of a cellular map \( {h}_{n} : M\left( {{G}_{n + 1}, n}\right)  \rightarrow  {X}_{n} \) such that the induced map \( {h}_{n * } : {H}_{n}\left( {M\left( {{G}_{n + 1}, n}\right) }\right)  \rightarrow  {H}_{n}\left( {X}_{n}\right) \) is trivial.

(3) \( X = \mathop{\bigcup }\limits_{n}{X}_{n} \) .

One sees inductively that \( {X}_{n + 1} \) has the desired homology groups by comparing the long exact sequences of the pairs \( \left( {{X}_{n + 1},{X}_{n}}\right) \) and \( \left( {{CM}, M}\right) \) where \( M = M\left( {{G}_{n + 1}, n}\right) \) and \( {CM} \) is the cone \( M \times  I/M \times  \{ 0\} \) :

\[
0 \rightarrow  {H}_{n + 1}\left( {X}_{n + 1}\right)  \rightarrow  {H}_{n + 1}\left( {{X}_{n + 1},{X}_{n}}\right) \overset{\partial }{ \rightarrow  }{H}_{n}\left( {X}_{n}\right)  \rightarrow  {H}_{n}\left( {X}_{n + 1}\right)  \rightarrow  0
\]

\[
\uparrow   \approx  \; \uparrow  {h}_{n * }
\]

\[
{H}_{n + 1}\left( {{CM}, M}\right) \overset{\partial }{ \rightarrow  }{H}_{n}\left( M\right)  \approx  {G}_{n + 1}
\]

The assumption that \( {h}_{n * } \) is trivial means that the boundary map in the upper row is zero, hence \( {H}_{n + 1}\left( {X}_{n + 1}\right)  \approx  {G}_{n + 1} \) . The other homology groups of \( {X}_{n + 1} \) are the same as those of \( {X}_{n} \) since \( {H}_{i}\left( {{X}_{n + 1},{X}_{n}}\right)  \approx  {H}_{i}\left( {{CM}, M}\right) \) for all \( i \) by excision, and \( {H}_{i}\left( {{CM}, M}\right)  \approx  {\widetilde{H}}_{i - 1}\left( M\right) \) since \( {CM} \) is contractible.

In case all the maps \( {h}_{n} \) are trivial, \( X \) is the wedge sum of the Moore spaces \( M\left( {{G}_{n}, n}\right) \) since in this case the mapping cone construction in (2) produces a wedge sum with the suspension of \( M\left( {{G}_{n + 1}, n}\right) \) , a Moore space \( M\left( {{G}_{n + 1}, n + 1}\right) \) .

For a space \( Y \) , a homotopy equivalence \( f : X \rightarrow  Y \) where \( X \) is constructed as in (1)-(3) is called a homology decomposition of \( Y \) .

|| Theorem 4H.3. Every simply-connected CW complex has a homology decomposition.

Proof: Given a simply-connected CW complex \( Y \) , let \( {G}_{n} = {H}_{n}\left( Y\right) \) . Suppose inductively that we have constructed \( {X}_{n} \) via maps \( {h}_{i} \) as in (2), together with a map \( f : {X}_{n} \rightarrow  Y \) inducing an isomorphism on \( {H}_{i} \) for \( i \leq  n \) . The induction can start with \( {X}_{1} \) a point since \( Y \) is simply-connected. To construct \( {X}_{n + 1} \) we first replace \( Y \) by the mapping cylinder of \( f : {X}_{n} \rightarrow  Y \) , converting \( f \) into an inclusion. By the Hurewicz theorem and the homology exact sequence of the pair \( \left( {Y,{X}_{n}}\right) \) we have \( {\pi }_{n + 1}\left( {Y,{X}_{n}}\right)  \approx \; {H}_{n + 1}\left( {Y,{X}_{n}}\right)  \approx  {H}_{n + 1}\left( Y\right)  = {G}_{n + 1} \) . We will use this isomorphism to construct a map \( {h}_{n} : M\left( {{G}_{n + 1}, n}\right)  \rightarrow  {X}_{n} \) and an extension \( f : {X}_{n + 1} \rightarrow  Y \) .

The standard construction of an \( M\left( {{G}_{n + 1}, n}\right) \) consists of a wedge of spheres \( {S}_{\alpha }^{n} \) corresponding to generators \( {g}_{\alpha } \) of \( {G}_{n + 1} \) , with cells \( {e}_{\beta }^{n + 1} \) attached according to certain linear combinations \( {r}_{\beta } = \mathop{\sum }\limits_{\alpha }{n}_{\alpha \beta }{g}_{\alpha } \) that are zero in \( {G}_{n + 1} \) . Under the isomorphism \( {G}_{n + 1} \approx  {\pi }_{n + 1}\left( {Y,{X}_{n}}\right) \) each \( {g}_{\alpha } \) corresponds to a basepoint-preserving map \( {f}_{\alpha } : \left( {C{S}^{n},{S}^{n}}\right)  \rightarrow  \left( {Y,{X}_{n}}\right) \) where \( C{S}^{n} \) is the cone on \( {S}^{n} \) . The restrictions of these \( {f}_{\alpha } \) ’s to \( {S}^{n} \) define \( {h}_{n} : \mathop{\bigvee }\limits_{\alpha }{S}_{\alpha }^{n} \rightarrow  {X}_{n} \) , and the maps \( {f}_{\alpha } : C{S}^{n} \rightarrow  Y \) themselves give an extension of \( f : {X}_{n} \rightarrow  Y \) to the mapping cone of \( {h}_{n} : \mathop{\bigvee }\limits_{\alpha }{S}_{\alpha }^{n} \rightarrow  {X}_{n} \) . Each relation \( {r}_{\beta } \) gives a homotopy \( {F}_{\beta } : \left( {C{S}^{n},{S}^{n}}\right)  \times  I \rightarrow  \left( {Y,{X}_{n}}\right) \) from \( \mathop{\sum }\limits_{\alpha }{n}_{\alpha \beta }{f}_{\alpha } \) to the constant map. We use \( {F}_{\beta } \mid  {S}^{n} \times  \{ 0\} \) to attach \( {e}_{\beta }^{n + 1} \) , and then \( {F}_{\beta } \mid  {S}^{n} \times  I \) gives \( {h}_{n} \) on \( {e}_{\beta }^{n + 1} \) and \( {F}_{\beta } \) gives an extension of \( f \) over the cone on \( {e}_{\beta }^{n + 1} \) .

This construction assures that \( {f}_{ * } : {H}_{n + 1}\left( {{X}_{n + 1},{X}_{n}}\right)  \rightarrow  {H}_{n + 1}\left( {Y,{X}_{n}}\right) \) is an isomorphism, so from the five-lemma applied to the long exact sequences of these pairs we deduce that \( {f}_{ * } : {H}_{i}\left( {X}_{n + 1}\right)  \rightarrow  {H}_{i}\left( Y\right) \) is an isomorphism for \( i \leq  n + 1 \) . This finishes the induction step. We may assume the maps \( {f}_{\alpha } \) and \( {F}_{\beta } \) are cellular, so \( X = \mathop{\bigcup }\limits_{n}{X}_{n} \) is a CW complex with subcomplexes \( {X}_{n} \) . Since \( f : X \rightarrow  Y \) is a homology isomorphism between simply-connected CW complexes, it is a homotopy equivalence.

As an example, suppose that \( Y \) is a simply-connected CW complex having all its homology groups free. Then the Moore spaces used in the construction of \( X \) can be taken to be wedges of spheres, and so \( {X}_{n} \) is obtained from \( {X}_{n - 1} \) by attaching an \( n \) -cell for each \( \mathbb{Z} \) summand of \( {H}_{n}\left( Y\right) \) . The attaching maps may be taken to be cellular, making \( X \) into a CW complex whose cellular chain complex has trivial boundary maps. Similarly, finite cyclic summands of \( {H}_{n}\left( Y\right) \) can be realized by wedge summands of the form \( {S}^{n - 1} \cup  {e}^{n} \) in \( M\left( {{H}_{n}\left( Y\right) , n - 1}\right) \) , contributing an \( n \) -cell and an \( \left( {n + 1}\right) \) -cell to \( X \) . This is Proposition 4C.1, but the present result is stronger because it tells us that a finite cyclic summand of \( {H}_{n} \) can be realized in one step by attaching the cone on a Moore space \( M\left( {{\mathbb{Z}}_{k}, n - 1}\right) \) , rather than in two steps of attaching an \( n \) -cell and then an \( \left( {n + 1}\right) \) -cell.

Show that if \( A \hookrightarrow  X \) is a cofibration of compact Hausdorff spaces, then for any space \( Y \) , the map \( {Y}^{X} \rightarrow  {Y}^{A} \) obtained by restriction of functions is a fibration. [If \( A \hookrightarrow  X \) is a cofibration, so is \( A \times  Y \hookrightarrow  X \times  Y \) for any space \( Y \) .]

2. Consider a pushout diagram as at the right, where \( B{ \sqcup  }_{f}X \) is \( B \) with \( X \) attached along \( A \) via \( f \) . Show that if \( A \hookrightarrow  X \) is a cofibration, so is \( B \hookrightarrow  B{ \sqcup  }_{f}X \) .

3. For fibrations \( {E}_{1} \rightarrow  B \) and \( {E}_{2} \rightarrow  B \) , show that a fiber-preserving map \( {E}_{1} \rightarrow  {E}_{2} \) that is a homotopy equivalence is in fact a fiber homotopy equivalence. [This is dual to Proposition 0.19.]

4. Define the dual of an iterated mapping cylinder precisely, in terms of maps from \( {\Delta }^{n} \) , and use this to give a definition of \( \nabla X \) , the dual of \( {\Delta X} \) , for \( X \) a complex of spaces.
