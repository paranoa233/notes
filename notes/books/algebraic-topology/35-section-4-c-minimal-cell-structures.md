# 4.C Minimal Cell Structures

We can apply the homology version of Whitehead's theorem, Corollary 4.33, to show that a simply-connected CW complex with finitely generated homology groups is always homotopy equivalent to a CW complex having the minimum number of cells consistent with its homology, namely, one \( n \) -cell for each \( \mathbb{Z} \) summand of \( {H}_{n} \) and a pair of cells of dimension \( n \) and \( n + 1 \) for each \( {\mathbb{Z}}_{k} \) summand of \( {H}_{n} \) .

Proposition 4C.1. Given a simply-connected CW complex \( X \) and a decomposition of each of its homology groups \( {H}_{n}\left( X\right) \) as a direct sum of cyclic groups with specified generators, then there is a CW complex \( Z \) and a cellular homotopy equivalence \( f : Z \rightarrow  X \) such that each cell of \( Z \) is either:

(a) a ’generator’ \( n \) -cell \( {e}_{\alpha }^{n} \) , which is a cycle in cellular homology mapped by \( f \) to a cellular cycle representing the specified generator \( \alpha \) of one of the cyclic summands of \( {H}_{n}\left( X\right) \) ; or

(b) a ’relator’ \( \left( {n + 1}\right) \) -cell \( {e}_{\alpha }^{n + 1} \) , with cellular boundary equal to a multiple of the generator \( n \) -cell \( {e}_{\alpha }^{n} \) , in the case that \( \alpha \) has finite order.

In the nonsimply-connected case this result can easily be false, counterexamples being provided by acyclic spaces and the space \( X = \left( {{S}^{1} \vee  {S}^{n}}\right)  \cup  {e}^{n + 1} \) constructed in Example 4.35, which has the same homology as \( {S}^{1} \) but which must have cells of dimension greater than 1 in order to have \( {\pi }_{n} \) nontrivial.

Proof: We build \( Z \) inductively over skeleta, starting with \( {Z}^{1} \) a point since \( X \) is simply-connected. For the inductive step, suppose we have constructed \( f : {Z}^{n} \rightarrow  X \) inducing an isomorphism on \( {H}_{i} \) for \( i < n \) and a surjection on \( {H}_{n} \) . For the mapping cylinder \( {M}_{f} \) we then have \( {H}_{i}\left( {{M}_{f},{Z}^{n}}\right)  = 0 \) for \( i \leq  n \) and \( {H}_{n + 1}\left( {{M}_{f},{Z}^{n}}\right)  \approx  {\pi }_{n + 1}\left( {{M}_{f},{Z}^{n}}\right) \) by the Hurewicz theorem. To construct \( {Z}^{n + 1} \) we use the following diagram:

\[
\begin{matrix} {H}_{n + 1}\left( X\right) & {\pi }_{n + 1}\left( {{M}_{f},{Z}^{n}}\right) & {H}_{n}\left( X\right) \\  l & l & l \end{matrix}
\]

\[
{H}_{n + 1}\left( {M}_{f}\right)  \rightarrow  {H}_{n + 1}\left( {{M}_{f},{Z}^{n}}\right)  \rightarrow  {H}_{n}\left( {Z}^{n}\right)  \rightarrow  {H}_{n}\left( {M}_{f}\right)  \rightarrow  0
\]

\[
\begin{matrix}  \uparrow  \\  {H}_{n + 1}\left( {Z}^{n + 1}\right)  \rightarrow  {H}_{n + 1}\left( {{Z}^{n + 1},{Z}^{n}}\right)  \rightarrow  {H}_{n}\left( {Z}^{n}\right)  \rightarrow  {H}_{n}\left( {Z}^{n + 1}\right)  \rightarrow  0 \end{matrix}
\]

By induction we know the map \( {H}_{n}\left( {Z}^{n}\right)  \rightarrow  {H}_{n}\left( {M}_{f}\right)  \approx  {H}_{n}\left( X\right) \) exactly, namely, \( {Z}^{n} \) has generator \( n \) -cells, which are cellular cycles mapping to the given generators of \( {H}_{n}\left( X\right) \) , along with relator \( n \) -cells that do not contribute to \( {H}_{n}\left( {Z}^{n}\right) \) . Thus \( {H}_{n}\left( {Z}^{n}\right) \) is free with basis the generator \( n \) -cells, and the kernel of \( {H}_{n}\left( {Z}^{n}\right)  \rightarrow  {H}_{n}\left( X\right) \) is free with basis given by certain multiples of some of the generator \( n \) -cells. Choose ’relator’ elements \( {\rho }_{i} \) in \( {H}_{n + 1}\left( {{M}_{f},{Z}^{n}}\right) \) mapping to this basis for the kernel, and let the ’generator’ elements \( {\gamma }_{i} \in  {H}_{n + 1}\left( {{M}_{f},{Z}^{n}}\right) \) be the images of the chosen generators of \( {H}_{n + 1}\left( {M}_{f}\right)  \approx  {H}_{n + 1}\left( X\right) \) .

Via the Hurewicz isomorphism \( {H}_{n + 1}\left( {{M}_{f},{Z}^{n}}\right)  \approx  {\pi }_{n + 1}\left( {{M}_{f},{Z}^{n}}\right) \) , the homology classes \( {\rho }_{i} \) and \( {\gamma }_{i} \) are represented by maps \( {r}_{i},{g}_{i} : \left( {{D}^{n + 1},{S}^{n}}\right)  \rightarrow  \left( {{M}_{f},{Z}^{n}}\right) \) . We form \( {Z}^{n + 1} \) from \( {Z}^{n} \) by attaching \( \left( {n + 1}\right) \) -cells via the restrictions of the maps \( {r}_{i} \) and \( {g}_{i} \) to \( {S}^{n} \) . The maps \( {r}_{i} \) and \( {g}_{i} \) themselves then give an extension of the inclusion \( {Z}^{n} \hookrightarrow  {M}_{f} \) to a map \( {Z}^{n + 1} \rightarrow  {M}_{f} \) , whose composition with the retraction \( {M}_{f} \rightarrow  X \) is the extended map \( f : {Z}^{n + 1} \rightarrow  X \) . This gives us the lower row of the preceding diagram, with commutative squares. By construction, the subgroup of \( {H}_{n + 1}\left( {{Z}^{n + 1},{Z}^{n}}\right) \) generated by the relator \( \left( {n + 1}\right) \) -cells maps injectively to \( {H}_{n}\left( {Z}^{n}\right) \) , with image the kernel of \( {H}_{n}\left( {Z}^{n}\right)  \rightarrow  {H}_{n}\left( X\right) \) , so \( {f}_{ * } : {H}_{n}\left( {Z}^{n + 1}\right)  \rightarrow  {H}_{n}\left( X\right) \) is an isomorphism. The elements of \( {H}_{n + 1}\left( {{Z}^{n + 1},{Z}^{n}}\right) \) represented by the generator \( \left( {n + 1}\right) \) -cells map to the \( {\gamma }_{i} \) ’s, hence map to zero in \( {H}_{n}\left( {Z}^{n}\right) \) , so by exactness of the second row these generator \( \left( {n + 1}\right) \) -cells are cellular cycles representing elements of \( {H}_{n + 1}\left( {Z}^{n + 1}\right) \) mapped by \( {f}_{ * } \) to the given generators of \( {H}_{n + 1}\left( X\right) \) . In particular, \( {f}_{ * } : {H}_{n + 1}\left( {Z}^{n + 1}\right)  \rightarrow  {H}_{n + 1}\left( X\right) \) is surjective, and the induction step is finished.

Doing this for all \( n \) , we produce a CW complex \( Z \) and a map \( f : Z \rightarrow  X \) with the desired properties.

Example 4C.2. Suppose \( X \) is a simply-connected CW complex such that for some \( n \geq  2 \) , the only nonzero reduced homology groups of \( X \) are \( {H}_{n}\left( X\right) \) , which is finitely generated, and \( {H}_{n + 1}\left( X\right) \) , which is finitely generated and free. Then the proposition says that \( X \) is homotopy equivalent to a CW complex \( Z \) obtained from a wedge sum of \( n \) -spheres by attaching \( \left( {n + 1}\right) \) -cells. The attaching maps of these cells are determined up to homotopy by the cellular boundary map \( {H}_{n + 1}\left( {{Z}^{n + 1},{Z}^{n}}\right)  \rightarrow  {H}_{n}\left( {Z}^{n}\right) \) since \( {\pi }_{n}\left( {Z}^{n}\right)  \approx  {H}_{n}\left( {Z}^{n}\right) \) . So the attaching maps are either trivial, in the case of generator \( \left( {n + 1}\right) \) -cells, or they represent some multiple of an inclusion of one of the wedge summands, in the case of a relator \( \left( {n + 1}\right) \) -cell. Hence \( Z \) is the wedge sum of spheres \( {S}^{n} \) and \( {S}^{n + 1} \) together with Moore spaces \( M\left( {{\mathbb{Z}}_{m}, n}\right) \) of the form \( {S}^{n} \cup  {e}^{n + 1} \) . In particular, the homotopy type of \( X \) is uniquely determined by its homology groups.

Proposition 4C.3. Let \( X \) be a simply-connected space homotopy equivalent to a CW complex, such that the only nontrivial reduced homology groups of \( X \) are \( {H}_{2}\left( X\right)  \approx \; {\mathbb{Z}}^{m} \) and \( {H}_{4}\left( X\right)  \approx  \mathbb{Z} \) . Then the homotopy type of \( X \) is uniquely determined by the cup product ring \( {H}^{ * }\left( {X;\mathbb{Z}}\right) \) . In particular, this applies to any simply-connected closed 4-manifold.

Proof: By the previous proposition we may assume \( X \) is a complex \( {X}_{\varphi } \) obtained from a wedge sum \( \mathop{\bigvee }\limits_{j}{S}_{j}^{2} \) of \( m \) 2-spheres \( {S}_{j}^{2} \) by attaching a cell \( {e}^{4} \) via a map \( \varphi  : {S}^{3} \rightarrow  \mathop{\bigvee }\limits_{j}{S}_{j}^{2} \) . As shown in Example 4.52, \( {\pi }_{3}\left( {\mathop{\bigvee }\limits_{j}{S}_{j}^{2}}\right) \) is free with basis the Hopf maps \( {\eta }_{j} : {S}^{3} \rightarrow  {S}_{j}^{2} \) and the Whitehead products \( \left\lbrack  {{i}_{j},{i}_{k}}\right\rbrack  , j < k \) , where \( {i}_{j} \) is the inclusion \( {S}_{j}^{2} \hookrightarrow  \mathop{\bigvee }\limits_{j}{S}_{j}^{2} \) . Since a homotopy of \( \varphi \) does not change the homotopy type of \( {X}_{\varphi } \) , we may assume \( \varphi \) is a linear combination \( \mathop{\sum }\limits_{j}{a}_{j}{\eta }_{j} + \mathop{\sum }\limits_{{j < k}}{a}_{jk}\left\lbrack  {{i}_{j},{i}_{k}}\right\rbrack \) . We need to see how the coefficients \( {a}_{j} \) and \( {a}_{jk} \) determine the cup product \( {H}^{2}\left( {X;\mathbb{Z}}\right)  \times  {H}^{2}\left( {X;\mathbb{Z}}\right)  \rightarrow  {H}^{4}\left( {X;\mathbb{Z}}\right) \) .

This cup product can be represented by an \( m \times  m \) symmetric matrix \( \left( {b}_{jk}\right) \) where the cup product of the cohomology classes dual to the \( {j}^{\text{ th }} \) and \( {k}^{\text{ th }} \) 2-cells is \( {b}_{jk} \) times the class dual to the 4-cell. We claim that \( {b}_{jk} = {a}_{jk} \) for \( j < k \) and \( {b}_{jj} = {a}_{j} \) . If \( \varphi \) is one of the generators \( {\eta }_{i} \) or \( \left\lbrack  {{i}_{j},{i}_{k}}\right\rbrack \) this is clear, since if \( \varphi  = {\eta }_{j} \) then \( {X}_{\varphi } \) is the wedge sum of \( {\mathbb{{CP}}}^{2} \) with \( m - 1 \) 2-spheres, while if \( \varphi  = \left\lbrack  {{i}_{j},{i}_{k}}\right\rbrack \) then \( {X}_{\varphi } \) is the wedge sum of \( {S}_{j}^{2} \times  {S}_{k}^{2} \) with \( m - 2 \) 2-spheres. The claim is also true when \( \varphi \) is \( - {\eta }_{j} \) or \( - \left\lbrack  {{i}_{j},{i}_{k}}\right\rbrack \) since changing the sign of \( \varphi \) amounts to composing \( \varphi \) with a reflection of \( {S}^{3} \) , and this changes the generator of \( {H}^{4}\left( {{X}_{\varphi };\mathbb{Z}}\right) \) to its negative. The general case now follows by induction from the assertion that the matrix \( \left( {b}_{jk}\right) \) for \( {X}_{\varphi  + \psi } \) is the sum of the corresponding matrices for \( {X}_{\varphi } \) and \( {X}_{\psi } \) . This assertion can be proved as follows. By attaching two 4-cells to \( \mathop{\bigvee }\limits_{j}{S}_{j}^{2} \) by \( \varphi \) and \( \psi \) we obtain a complex \( {X}_{\varphi ,\psi } \) which we can view as \( {X}_{\varphi } \cup  {X}_{\psi } \) . There is a quotient map \( q : {X}_{\varphi  + \psi } \rightarrow  {X}_{\varphi ,\psi } \) that is a homeomorphism on the 2-skeleton and collapses the closure of an equatorial 3-disk in the 4-cell of \( {X}_{\varphi  + \psi } \) to a point. The induced map \( {q}^{ * } : {H}^{4}\left( {X}_{\varphi ,\psi }\right)  \rightarrow  {H}^{4}\left( {X}_{\varphi  + \psi }\right) \) sends each of the two generators corresponding to the 4-cells of \( {X}_{\varphi ,\psi } \) to a generator, and the assertion follows.

Now suppose \( {X}_{\varphi } \) and \( {X}_{\psi } \) have isomorphic cup product rings. This means bases for \( {H}^{ * }\left( {{X}_{\varphi };\mathbb{Z}}\right) \) and \( {H}^{ * }\left( {{X}_{\psi };\mathbb{Z}}\right) \) can be chosen so that the matrices specifying the cup product \( {H}^{2} \times  {H}^{2} \rightarrow  {H}^{4} \) with respect to these bases are the same. The preceding proposition says that any choice of basis can be realized as the dual basis to a cell structure on a CW complex homotopy equivalent to the given complex. Therefore we may assume the matrices \( \left( {b}_{jk}\right) \) for \( {X}_{\varphi } \) and \( {X}_{\psi } \) are the same. By what we have shown in the preceding paragraph, this means \( \varphi \) and \( \psi \) are homotopic, hence \( {X}_{\varphi } \) and \( {X}_{\psi } \) are homotopy equivalent.

For the statement about simply-connected closed 4-manifolds, Corollaries A. 8 and A. 9 and Proposition A. 11 in the Appendix say that such a manifold \( M \) has the homotopy type of a CW complex with finitely generated homology groups. Then Poincaré duality and the universal coefficient theorem imply that the only nontrivial homology groups \( {H}_{i}\left( M\right) \) are \( \mathbb{Z} \) for \( i = 0,4 \) and \( {\mathbb{Z}}^{m} \) for \( i = 2 \) , for some \( m \geq  0 \) .

This result and the example preceding it are special cases of a homotopy classification by Whitehead of simply-connected CW complexes with positive-dimensional cells in three adjacent dimensions \( n, n + 1 \) , and \( n + 2 \) ; see [Baues 1996] for a full treatment of this.
