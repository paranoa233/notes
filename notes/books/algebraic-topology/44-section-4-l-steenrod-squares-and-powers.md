# 4.L Steenrod Squares and Powers

The main objects of study in this section are certain homomorphisms called Steen-rod squares and Steenrod powers:

\[
{\mathrm{{Sq}}}^{i} : {H}^{n}\left( {X;{\mathbb{Z}}_{2}}\right)  \rightarrow  {H}^{n + i}\left( {X;{\mathbb{Z}}_{2}}\right)
\]

\[
{P}^{i} : {H}^{n}\left( {X;{\mathbb{Z}}_{p}}\right)  \rightarrow  {H}^{n + {2i}\left( {p - 1}\right) }\left( {X;{\mathbb{Z}}_{p}}\right) \;\text{ for odd primes }p
\]

The terms ’squares’ and ’powers’ arise from the fact that \( S{q}^{i} \) and \( {P}^{i} \) are related to the maps \( \alpha  \mapsto  {\alpha }^{2} \) and \( \alpha  \mapsto  {\alpha }^{p} \) sending a cohomology class \( \alpha \) to the 2-fold or \( p \) -fold cup product with itself. Unlike cup products, however, the operations \( S{q}^{i} \) and \( {P}^{i} \) are stable, that is, invariant under suspension.

The operations \( S{q}^{i} \) generate an algebra \( {\mathcal{A}}_{2} \) , called the Steenrod algebra, such that \( {H}^{ * }\left( {X;{\mathbb{Z}}_{2}}\right) \) is a module over \( {\mathcal{A}}_{2} \) for every space \( X \) , and maps between spaces induce homomorphisms of \( {\mathcal{A}}_{2} \) -modules. Similarly, for odd primes \( p,{H}^{ * }\left( {X;{\mathbb{Z}}_{p}}\right) \) is a module over a corresponding Steenrod algebra \( {\mathcal{A}}_{p} \) generated by the \( {P}^{i} \) ’s and Bockstein homomorphisms. Like the ring structure given by cup product, these module structures impose strong constraints on spaces and maps. For example, we will use them to show that there do not exist spaces \( X \) with \( {H}^{ * }\left( {X;\mathbb{Z}}\right) \) a polynomial ring \( \mathbb{Z}\left\lbrack  \alpha \right\rbrack \) unless \( \alpha \) has dimension 2 or 4, where there are the familiar examples of \( {\mathbb{{CP}}}^{\infty } \) and \( {\mathbb{{HP}}}^{\infty } \) .

This rather lengthy section is divided into two main parts. The first part describes the basic properties of Steenrod squares and powers and gives a number of examples and applications. The second part is devoted to constructing the squares and powers and showing they satisfy the basic properties listed in the first part. More extensive applications will be given in [SSAT] after spectral sequences have been introduced. Most applications of Steenrod squares and powers do not depend on how these operations are actually constructed, but only on their basic properties. This is similar to the situation for ordinary homology and cohomology, where the axioms generally suffice for most applications. The construction of Steenrod squares and powers and the verification of their basic properties, or axioms, is rather interesting in its own way, but does involve a certain amount of work, particularly for the Steenrod powers, and this is why we delay the work until later in the section.

We begin with a few generalities. A cohomology operation is a transformation \( \Theta  = {\Theta }_{X} : {H}^{m}\left( {X;G}\right)  \rightarrow  {H}^{n}\left( {X;H}\right) \) defined for all spaces \( X \) , with a fixed choice of \( m, n \) , \( G \) , and \( H \) , and satisfying the naturality property that for all maps \( f : X \rightarrow  Y \) there is a commuting diagram as shown at the right. For example, with coefficients in a ring \( R \) the transformation \( {H}^{m}\left( {X;R}\right)  \rightarrow  {H}^{mp}\left( {X;R}\right) \) , \( \alpha  \mapsto  {\alpha }^{p} \) , is a cohomology operation since \( {f}^{ * }\left( {\alpha }^{p}\right)  = {\left( {f}^{ * }\left( \alpha \right) \right) }^{p} \) . Taking \( R = \mathbb{Z} \) , this example shows that cohomology operations need not be homomorphisms. On the other hand, when \( R = {\mathbb{Z}}_{p} \) with \( p \) prime, the operation \( \alpha  \mapsto  {\alpha }^{p} \) is a homomorphism. Other examples of cohomology operations we have already encountered are the Bockstein homomorphisms defined in §3.E. As a more trivial example, a homomorphism \( G \rightarrow  H \) induces change-of-coefficient homomorphisms \( {H}^{m}\left( {X;G}\right)  \rightarrow  {H}^{m}\left( {X;H}\right) \) which can be viewed as cohomology operations.

In spite of their rather general definition, cohomology operations can be described in somewhat more concrete terms:

Proposition 4L.1. For fixed \( m, n, G \) , and \( H \) there is a bijection between the set of all cohomology operations \( \Theta  : {H}^{m}\left( {X;G}\right)  \rightarrow  {H}^{n}\left( {X;H}\right) \) and \( {H}^{n}\left( {K\left( {G, m}\right) ;H}\right) \) , defined by \( \Theta  \mapsto  \Theta \left( \iota \right) \) where \( \iota  \in  {H}^{m}\left( {K\left( {G, m}\right) ;G}\right) \) is a fundamental class.

Proof: Via CW approximations to spaces, it suffices to restrict attention to CW complexes, so we can identify \( {H}^{m}\left( {X;G}\right) \) with \( \langle X, K\left( {G, m}\right) \rangle \) when \( m > 0 \) by Theorem 4.57, and with \( \left\lbrack  {X, K\left( {G,0}\right) }\right\rbrack \) when \( m = 0 \) . If an element \( \alpha  \in  {H}^{m}\left( {X;G}\right) \) corresponds to a map \( \varphi  : X \rightarrow  K\left( {G, m}\right) \) , so \( {\varphi }^{ * }\left( \iota \right)  = \alpha \) , then \( \Theta \left( \alpha \right)  = \Theta \left( {{\varphi }^{ * }\left( \iota \right) }\right)  = {\varphi }^{ * }\left( {\Theta \left( \iota \right) }\right) \) and \( \Theta \) is uniquely determined by \( \Theta \left( \iota \right) \) . Thus \( \Theta  \mapsto  \Theta \left( \iota \right) \) is injective. For surjectivity, given an element \( \alpha  \in  {H}^{n}\left( {K\left( {G, m}\right) ;H}\right) \) corresponding to a map \( \theta  : K\left( {G, m}\right)  \rightarrow  K\left( {H, n}\right) \) , then composing with \( \theta \) defines a transformation \( \langle X, K\left( {G, m}\right) \rangle  \rightarrow  \langle X, K\left( {H, n}\right) \rangle \) , that is, \( \Theta  : {H}^{m}\left( {X;G}\right)  \rightarrow  {H}^{n}\left( {X;H}\right) \) , with \( \Theta \left( \iota \right)  = \alpha \) . The naturality property for \( \Theta \) amounts to associativity of the compositions \( X\overset{f}{ \rightarrow  }Y\overset{\varphi }{ \rightarrow  }K\left( {G, m}\right) \overset{\theta }{ \rightarrow  }K\left( {H, n}\right) \) and so \( \Theta \) is a cohomology operation.

A consequence of the proposition is that cohomology operations that decrease dimension are all rather trivial since \( K\left( {G, m}\right) \) is \( \left( {m - 1}\right) \) -connected. Moreover, since \( {H}^{m}\left( {K\left( {G, m}\right) ;H}\right)  \approx  \operatorname{Hom}\left( {G, H}\right) \) , it follows that the only cohomology operations that preserve dimension are given by coefficient homomorphisms.

The Steenrod squares \( S{q}^{i} : {H}^{n}\left( {X;{\mathbb{Z}}_{2}}\right)  \rightarrow  {H}^{n + i}\left( {X;{\mathbb{Z}}_{2}}\right) , i \geq  0 \) , will satisfy the following list of properties, beginning with naturality:

(1) \( S{q}^{i}\left( {{f}^{ * }\left( \alpha \right) }\right)  = {f}^{ * }\left( {S{q}^{i}\left( \alpha \right) }\right) \) for \( f : X \rightarrow  Y \) .

(2) \( S{q}^{i}\left( {\alpha  + \beta }\right)  = S{q}^{i}\left( \alpha \right)  + S{q}^{i}\left( \beta \right) \) .

(3) \( S{q}^{i}\left( {\alpha  \smile  \beta }\right)  = \mathop{\sum }\limits_{j}S{q}^{j}\left( \alpha \right)  \smile  S{q}^{i - j}\left( \beta \right) \) (the Cartan formula).

(4) \( S{q}^{i}\left( {\sigma \left( \alpha \right) }\right)  = \sigma \left( {S{q}^{i}\left( \alpha \right) }\right) \) where \( \sigma  : {H}^{n}\left( {X;{\mathbb{Z}}_{2}}\right)  \rightarrow  {H}^{n + 1}\left( {{\sum X};{\mathbb{Z}}_{2}}\right) \) is the suspension isomorphism given by reduced cross product with a generator of \( {H}^{1}\left( {{S}^{1};{\mathbb{Z}}_{2}}\right) \) .

(5) \( S{q}^{i}\left( \alpha \right)  = {\alpha }^{2} \) if \( i = \left| \alpha \right| \) , and \( S{q}^{i}\left( \alpha \right)  = 0 \) if \( i > \left| \alpha \right| \) .

(6) \( S{q}^{0} = \mathbb{1} \) , the identity.

(7) \( S{q}^{1} \) is the \( {\mathbb{Z}}_{2} \) Bockstein homomorphism \( \beta \) associated with the coefficient sequence \( 0 \rightarrow  {\mathbb{Z}}_{2} \rightarrow  {\mathbb{Z}}_{4} \rightarrow  {\mathbb{Z}}_{2} \rightarrow  0 \) .

The first part of (5) says that the Steenrod squares extend the squaring operation \( \alpha  \mapsto  {\alpha }^{2} \) , which has the nice feature of being a homomorphism with \( {\mathbb{Z}}_{2} \) coefficients. Property (4) says that the \( {\mathrm{{Sq}}}^{i} \) ’s are stable operations, invariant under suspension. The actual squaring operation \( \alpha  \mapsto  {\alpha }^{2} \) does not have this property since in a suspension \( {\sum X} \) all cup products of positive-dimensional classes are zero, according to an exercise for §3.2.

The fact that Steenrod squares are stable operations extending the cup product square yields the following theorem, which implies that the stable homotopy groups of spheres \( {\pi }_{1}^{s},{\pi }_{3}^{s} \) , and \( {\pi }_{7}^{s} \) are nontrivial:

Theorem 4L.2. If \( f : {S}^{{2n} - 1} \rightarrow  {S}^{n} \) has Hopf invariant 1, then \( \left\lbrack  f\right\rbrack   \in  {\pi }_{n - 1}^{s} \) is nonzero, so the iterated suspensions \( {\sum }^{k}f : {S}^{{2n} + k - 1} \rightarrow  {S}^{n + k} \) are all homotopically nontrivial.

Proof: Associated to a map \( f : {S}^{\ell } \rightarrow  {S}^{m} \) is the mapping cone \( {C}_{f} = {S}^{m}{ \cup  }_{f}{e}^{\ell  + 1} \) with the cell \( {e}^{\ell  + 1} \) attached via \( f \) . Assuming \( f \) is basepoint-preserving, we have the relation \( {C}_{\sum f} = \sum {C}_{f} \) where \( \sum \) denotes reduced suspension.

If \( f : {S}^{{2n} - 1} \rightarrow  {S}^{n} \) has Hopf invariant 1, then by (5), \( S{q}^{n} : {H}^{n}\left( {{C}_{f};{\mathbb{Z}}_{2}}\right)  \rightarrow  {H}^{2n}\left( {{C}_{f};{\mathbb{Z}}_{2}}\right) \) is nontrivial. By (4) the same is true for \( S{q}^{n} : {H}^{n + k}\left( {{\sum }^{k}{C}_{f};{\mathbb{Z}}_{2}}\right)  \rightarrow  {H}^{{2n} + k}\left( {{\sum }^{k}{C}_{f};{\mathbb{Z}}_{2}}\right) \) for all \( k \) . If \( {\sum }^{k}f \) were homotopically trivial we would have a retraction \( r : {\sum }^{k}{C}_{f} \rightarrow  {S}^{n + k} \) . The diagram at the right would then commute by naturality of \( S{q}^{n} \) , but since the group in the lower left corner of the diagram is zero, this gives a contradiction.

\[
\begin{matrix} {H}^{n + k}\left( {{S}^{n + k};{\mathbb{Z}}_{2}}\right) \overset{{r}^{ * }}{ \approx  }{H}^{n + k}\left( {{\sum }^{k}{C}_{f};{\mathbb{Z}}_{2}}\right) \\  \int S{q}^{n} \end{matrix}
\]

\[
{H}^{{2n} + k}\left( {{S}^{n + k};{\mathbb{Z}}_{2}}\right) \overset{{r}^{ * }}{ \rightarrow  }{H}^{{2n} + k}\left( {{\sum }^{k}{C}_{f};{\mathbb{Z}}_{2}}\right)
\]

The Steenrod power operations \( {P}^{i} : {H}^{n}\left( {X;{\mathbb{Z}}_{p}}\right)  \rightarrow  {H}^{n + {2i}\left( {p - 1}\right) }\left( {X;{\mathbb{Z}}_{p}}\right) \) for \( p \) an odd prime will satisfy analogous properties:

(1) \( {P}^{i}\left( {{f}^{ * }\left( \alpha \right) }\right)  = {f}^{ * }\left( {{P}^{i}\left( \alpha \right) }\right) \) for \( f : X \rightarrow  Y \) .

(2) \( {P}^{i}\left( {\alpha  + \beta }\right)  = {P}^{i}\left( \alpha \right)  + {P}^{i}\left( \beta \right) \) .

(3) \( {P}^{i}\left( {\alpha  \smile  \beta }\right)  = \mathop{\sum }\limits_{j}{P}^{j}\left( \alpha \right)  \smile  {P}^{i - j}\left( \beta \right) \) (the Cartan formula).

(4) \( {P}^{i}\left( {\sigma \left( \alpha \right) }\right)  = \sigma \left( {{P}^{i}\left( \alpha \right) }\right) \) where \( \sigma  : {H}^{n}\left( {X;{\mathbb{Z}}_{p}}\right)  \rightarrow  {H}^{n + 1}\left( {{\sum X};{\mathbb{Z}}_{p}}\right) \) is the suspension isomorphism given by reduced cross product with a generator of \( {H}^{1}\left( {{S}^{1};{\mathbb{Z}}_{p}}\right) \) .

(5) \( {P}^{i}\left( \alpha \right)  = {\alpha }^{p} \) if \( {2i} = \left| \alpha \right| \) , and \( {P}^{i}\left( \alpha \right)  = 0 \) if \( {2i} > \left| \alpha \right| \) .

(6) \( {P}^{0} = \mathbb{1} \) , the identity.

The germinal property \( {P}^{i}\left( \alpha \right)  = {\alpha }^{p} \) in (5) can only be expected to hold for even-dimensional classes \( \alpha \) since for odd-dimensional \( \alpha \) the commutativity property of cup product implies that \( {\alpha }^{2} = 0 \) with \( {\mathbb{Z}}_{p} \) coefficients if \( p \) is odd, and then \( {\alpha }^{p} = 0 \) since \( {\alpha }^{2} = 0 \) . Note that the formula \( {P}^{i}\left( \alpha \right)  = {\alpha }^{p} \) for \( \left| \alpha \right|  = {2i} \) implies that \( {P}^{i} \) raises dimension by \( {2i}\left( {p - 1}\right) \) , explaining the appearance of this number.

The Bockstein homomorphism \( \beta  : {H}^{n}\left( {X;{\mathbb{Z}}_{p}}\right)  \rightarrow  {H}^{n + 1}\left( {X;{\mathbb{Z}}_{p}}\right) \) is not included as one of the \( {P}^{i} \) ’s, but this is mainly a matter of notational convenience. As we shall see later when we discuss Adem relations, the operation \( S{q}^{{2i} + 1} \) is the same as the composition \( S{q}^{1}S{q}^{2i} = {\beta S}{q}^{2i} \) , so the \( S{q}^{2i} \) ’s can be regarded as the \( {P}^{i} \) ’s for \( p = 2 \) .

One might ask if there are elements of \( {\pi }_{ * }^{s} \) detectable by Steenrod powers in the same way that the Hopf maps are detected by Steenrod squares. The answer is yes for the operation \( {P}^{1} \) , as we show in Example 4L.6. It is a perhaps disappointing fact that no other squares or powers besides \( S{q}^{1}, S{q}^{2}, S{q}^{4}, S{q}^{8} \) , and \( {P}^{1} \) detect elements of homotopy groups of spheres. ( \( S{q}^{1} \) detects a map \( {S}^{n} \rightarrow  {S}^{n} \) of degree 2.) We will prove this for certain \( S{q}^{i} \) ’s and \( {P}^{i} \) ’s later in this section. The general case for \( p = 2 \) is Adams’ theorem on the Hopf invariant discussed in §4.B, while the case of odd \( p \) is proved in [Adams & Atiyah 1966]; see also [VBKT].

The Cartan formulas can be expressed in a more concise form by defining total Steenrod square and power operations by \( {Sq} = S{q}^{0} + S{q}^{1} + \cdots \) and \( P = {P}^{0} + {P}^{1} + \cdots \) . These act on \( {H}^{ * }\left( {X;{\mathbb{Z}}_{p}}\right) \) since by property (5), only a finite number of \( S{q}^{i} \) ’s or \( {P}^{i} \) ’s can be nonzero on a given cohomology class. The Cartan formulas then say that \( {Sq}\left( {\alpha  \smile  \beta }\right)  = {Sq}\left( \alpha \right)  \smile  {Sq}\left( \beta \right) \) and \( P\left( {\alpha  \smile  \beta }\right)  = P\left( \alpha \right)  \smile  P\left( \beta \right) \) , so \( {Sq} \) and \( P \) are ring homomorphisms.

We can use \( {Sq} \) and \( P \) to compute the operations \( S{q}^{i} \) and \( {P}^{i} \) for projective spaces and lens spaces via the following general formulas:

(*)

\[
S{q}^{i}\left( {\alpha }^{n}\right)  = \left( \begin{matrix} n \\  i \end{matrix}\right) {\alpha }^{n + i}\text{ for }\alpha  \in  {H}^{1}\left( {X;{\mathbb{Z}}_{2}}\right)
\]

\[
{P}^{i}\left( {\alpha }^{n}\right)  = \left( \begin{matrix} n \\  i \end{matrix}\right) {\alpha }^{n + i\left( {p - 1}\right) }\text{ for }\alpha  \in  {H}^{2}\left( {X;{\mathbb{Z}}_{p}}\right)
\]

To derive the first formula, properties (5) and (6) give \( {Sq}\left( \alpha \right)  = \alpha  + {\alpha }^{2} = \alpha \left( {1 + \alpha }\right) \) , so \( {Sq}\left( {\alpha }^{n}\right)  = {Sq}{\left( \alpha \right) }^{n} = {\alpha }^{n}{\left( 1 + \alpha \right) }^{n} = \mathop{\sum }\limits_{i}\left( \begin{matrix} n \\  i \end{matrix}\right) {\alpha }^{n + i} \) and hence \( S{q}^{i}\left( {\alpha }^{n}\right)  = \left( \begin{matrix} n \\  i \end{matrix}\right) {\alpha }^{n + i} \) . The second formula is obtained in similar fashion: \( P\left( \alpha \right)  = \alpha  + {\alpha }^{p} = \alpha \left( {1 + {\alpha }^{p - 1}}\right) \) so \( P\left( {\alpha }^{n}\right)  = {\alpha }^{n}{\left( 1 + {\alpha }^{p - 1}\right) }^{n} = \mathop{\sum }\limits_{i}\left( \begin{matrix} n \\  i \end{matrix}\right) {\alpha }^{n + i\left( {p - 1}\right) }. \)

Example 4L.3: Stable Splittings. The formula \( \left( *\right) \) tells us how to compute Steen-rod squares for \( {\mathbb{{RP}}}^{\infty } \) , hence also for any suspension of \( {\mathbb{{RP}}}^{\infty } \) . The explicit formulas for \( {Sq}\left( {\alpha }^{{2}^{k}}\right) \) and \( {Sq}\left( {\alpha }^{{2}^{k} - 1}\right) \) above show that all the powers of the generator \( \alpha  \in  {H}^{1}\left( {{\mathbb{{RP}}}^{\infty };{\mathbb{Z}}_{2}}\right) \) are tied together by Steenrod squares since the first formula connects \( \alpha \) inductively to all the powers \( {\alpha }^{{2}^{k}} \) and the second formula connects these powers to all the other powers. This shows that no suspension \( {\sum }^{k}{\mathbb{{RP}}}^{\infty } \) has the homotopy type of a wedge sum \( X \vee  Y \) with both \( X \) and \( Y \) having nontrivial cohomology. In the case of \( {\mathbb{{RP}}}^{\infty } \) itself we could have deduced this from the ring structure of \( {H}^{ * }\left( {{\mathbb{{RP}}}^{\infty };{\mathbb{Z}}_{2}}\right)  \approx  {\mathbb{Z}}_{2}\left\lbrack  \alpha \right\rbrack \) , but cup products become trivial in a suspension.

The same reasoning shows that \( {\mathbb{{CP}}}^{\infty } \) and \( {\mathbb{{HP}}}^{\infty } \) have no nontrivial stable splittings. The \( {\mathbb{Z}}_{2} \) cohomology in these cases is again \( {\mathbb{Z}}_{2}\left\lbrack  \alpha \right\rbrack \) , though with \( \alpha \) no longer 1-dimensional. However, we still have \( {Sq}\left( \alpha \right)  = \alpha  + {\alpha }^{2} \) since these spaces have no nontrivial cohomology in the dimensions between \( \alpha \) and \( {\alpha }^{2} \) , so we have \( S{q}^{2i}\left( {\alpha }^{n}\right)  = \; \left( \begin{matrix} n \\  i \end{matrix}\right) {\alpha }^{n + i} \) for \( {\mathbb{{CP}}}^{\infty } \) and \( S{q}^{4i}\left( {\alpha }^{n}\right)  = \left( \begin{matrix} n \\  i \end{matrix}\right) {\alpha }^{n + i} \) for \( {\mathbb{{HP}}}^{\infty } \) . Then the arguments from the real case carry over using the operations \( S{q}^{2i} \) and \( S{q}^{4i} \) in place of \( S{q}^{i} \) .

Suppose we consider the same question for \( K\left( {{\mathbb{Z}}_{3},1}\right) \) instead of \( {\mathbb{{RP}}}^{\infty } \) . Taking cohomology with \( {\mathbb{Z}}_{3} \) coefficients, the Bockstein \( \beta \) is nonzero on odd-dimensional classes in \( {H}^{ * }\left( {K\left( {{\mathbb{Z}}_{3},1}\right) ;{\mathbb{Z}}_{3}}\right) \) , thus tying them to the even-dimensional classes, so we only need to see which even-dimensional classes are connected by \( {P}^{i} \) ’s. The even-dimensional part of \( {H}^{ * }\left( {K\left( {{\mathbb{Z}}_{3},1}\right) ;{\mathbb{Z}}_{3}}\right) \) is a polynomial algebra \( {\mathbb{Z}}_{3}\left\lbrack  \alpha \right\rbrack \) with \( \left| \alpha \right|  = 2 \) , so we have \( {P}^{i}\left( {\alpha }^{n}\right)  = \left( \begin{matrix} n \\  i \end{matrix}\right) {\alpha }^{n + i\left( {p - 1}\right) } = \left( \begin{matrix} n \\  i \end{matrix}\right) {\alpha }^{n + {2i}} \) by our earlier formula. Since \( {P}^{i} \) raises dimension by \( {4i} \) when \( p = 3 \) , there is no chance that all the even-dimensional cohomology will be connected by the \( {P}^{i} \) ’s. In fact, we showed in Proposition 4I.3 that \( {\sum K}\left( {{\mathbb{Z}}_{3},1}\right)  \simeq  {X}_{1} \vee  {X}_{2} \) where \( {X}_{1} \) has the cohomology of \( {\sum K}\left( {{\mathbb{Z}}_{3},1}\right) \) in dimensions congruent to 2 and 3 mod 4, while \( {X}_{2} \) has the remaining cohomology. Thus the best one could hope would be that all the odd powers of \( \alpha \) are connected by \( {P}^{i} \) ’s and likewise all the even powers are connected, since this would imply that neither \( {X}_{1} \) nor \( {X}_{2} \) splits nontrivially. This is indeed the case, as one sees by an examination of the coefficients in the formula \( {P}^{i}\left( {\alpha }^{n}\right)  = \left( \begin{matrix} n \\  i \end{matrix}\right) {\alpha }^{n + {2i}} \) . In the Pascal triangle mod 3, shown at the right, \( P\left( {\alpha }^{n}\right) \) is determined by the \( \left( {n + 1}\right) \) st row. For example the sixth row says that \( P\left( {\alpha }^{5}\right)  = {\alpha }^{5} + 2{\alpha }^{7} + {\alpha }^{9} + {\alpha }^{11} + 2{\alpha }^{13} + {\alpha }^{15} \) . A few moments’ thought shows that the rows that compute \( P\left( {\alpha }^{n}\right) \) for \( n = {3}^{k}m - 1 \) have all nonzero entries, and these rows together with the rows right after them suffice to connect the powers of \( \alpha \) in the desired way, so \( {X}_{1} \) and \( {X}_{2} \) have no stable splittings. One can also see that \( {\sum }^{2}{X}_{1} \) and \( {X}_{2} \) are not homotopy equivalent, even stably, since the operations \( {P}^{i} \) act differently in the two spaces. For example \( {P}^{2} \) is trivial on suspensions of \( \alpha \) but not on suspensions of \( {\alpha }^{2} \) .

The situation for \( K\left( {{\mathbb{Z}}_{p},1}\right) \) for larger primes \( p \) is entirely similar, with \( {\sum K}\left( {{\mathbb{Z}}_{p},1}\right) \) splitting as a wedge sum of \( p - 1 \) spaces. The same arguments work more generally for \( K\left( {{\mathbb{Z}}_{{p}^{i}},1}\right) \) , though for \( i > 1 \) the usual Bockstein \( \beta \) is identically zero so one has to use instead a Bockstein involving \( {\mathbb{Z}}_{{p}^{i}} \) coefficients. We leave the details of these arguments as exercises.

Example 4L.4: Maps of \( {\mathbb{{HP}}}^{\infty } \) . We can use the operations \( {P}^{i} \) together with a bit of number theory to demonstrate an interesting distinction between \( {\mathbb{{HP}}}^{\infty } \) and \( {\mathbb{{CP}}}^{\infty } \) , namely, we will show that if a map \( f : {\mathbb{{HP}}}^{\infty } \rightarrow  {\mathbb{{HP}}}^{\infty } \) has \( {f}^{ * }\left( \gamma \right)  = {dy} \) for \( \gamma \) a generator of \( {H}^{4}\left( {{\mathbb{{HP}}}^{\infty };\mathbb{Z}}\right) \) , then the integer \( d \) , which we call the degree of \( f \) , must be a square. By contrast, since \( {\mathbb{{CP}}}^{\infty } \) is a \( K\left( {\mathbb{Z},2}\right) \) , there are maps \( {\mathbb{{CP}}}^{\infty } \rightarrow  {\mathbb{{CP}}}^{\infty } \) carrying a generator \( \alpha  \in  {H}^{2}\left( {{\mathbb{{CP}}}^{\infty };\mathbb{Z}}\right) \) onto any given multiple of itself. Explicitly, the map \( z \mapsto  {z}^{d}, z \in  \mathbb{C} \) , induces a map \( f \) of \( {\mathbb{{CP}}}^{\infty } \) with \( {f}^{ * }\left( \alpha \right)  = {d\alpha } \) , but commutativity of \( \mathbb{C} \) is needed for this construction so it does not extend to the quaternionic case.

We shall deduce the action of Steenrod powers on \( {H}^{ * }\left( {{\mathbb{{HP}}}^{\infty };{\mathbb{Z}}_{p}}\right) \) from their action on \( {H}^{ * }\left( {{\mathbb{{CP}}}^{\infty };{\mathbb{Z}}_{p}}\right) \) , given by the earlier formula (*) which says that \( {P}^{i}\left( {\alpha }^{n}\right)  = \; \left( \begin{matrix} n \\  i \end{matrix}\right) {\alpha }^{n + i\left( {p - 1}\right) } \) for \( \alpha \) a generator of \( {H}^{2}\left( {{\mathbb{{CP}}}^{\infty };{\mathbb{Z}}_{p}}\right) \) . There is a natural quotient map \( {\mathbb{{CP}}}^{\infty } \rightarrow  {\mathbb{{HP}}}^{\infty } \) arising from the definition of both spaces as quotients of \( {S}^{\infty } \) . This map takes the 4-cell of \( {\mathbb{{CP}}}^{\infty } \) homeomorphically onto the 4-cell of \( {\mathbb{{HP}}}^{\infty } \) , so the induced map on cohomology sends a generator \( \gamma  \in  {H}^{4}\left( {{\mathbb{{HP}}}^{\infty };{\mathbb{Z}}_{p}}\right) \) to \( {\alpha }^{2} \) , hence \( {\gamma }^{n} \) to \( {\alpha }^{2n} \) . Thus the formula \( {P}^{i}\left( {\alpha }^{2n}\right)  = \left( \begin{matrix} {2n} \\  i \end{matrix}\right) {\alpha }^{{2n} + i\left( {p - 1}\right) } \) implies that \( {P}^{i}\left( {\gamma }^{n}\right)  = \left( \begin{matrix} {2n} \\  i \end{matrix}\right) {\gamma }^{n + i\left( {p - 1}\right) /2} \) . For example, \( {P}^{1}\left( \gamma \right)  = 2{\gamma }^{\left( {p + 1}\right) /2} \) .

Now let \( f : {\mathbb{{HP}}}^{\infty } \rightarrow  {\mathbb{{HP}}}^{\infty } \) be any map. Applying the formula \( {P}^{1}\left( \gamma \right)  = 2{\gamma }^{\left( {p + 1}\right) /2} \) in two ways, we get

\[
{P}^{1}{f}^{ * }\left( \gamma \right)  = {f}^{ * }{P}^{1}\left( \gamma \right)  = {f}^{ * }\left( {2{\gamma }^{\left( {p + 1}\right) /2}}\right)  = 2{d}^{\left( {p + 1}\right) /2}{\gamma }^{\left( {p + 1}\right) /2}
\]

and \( {P}^{1}{f}^{ * }\left( \gamma \right)  = {P}^{1}\left( {dy}\right)  = {2d}{y}^{\left( {p + 1}\right) /2} \)

Hence the degree \( d \) satisfies \( {d}^{\left( {p + 1}\right) /2} \equiv  d{\;\operatorname{mod}\;p} \) for all odd primes \( p \) . Thus either \( d \equiv  0{\;\operatorname{mod}\;p} \) or \( {d}^{\left( {p - 1}\right) /2} \equiv  1{\;\operatorname{mod}\;p} \) . In both cases \( d \) is a square mod \( p \) since the congruence \( {d}^{\left( {p - 1}\right) /2} \equiv  1{\;\operatorname{mod}\;p} \) is equivalent to \( d \) being a nonzero square mod \( p \) , the multiplicative group of nonzero elements of the field \( {\mathbb{Z}}_{p} \) being cyclic of order \( p - 1 \) .

The argument is completed by appealing to the number theory fact that an integer which is a square mod \( p \) for all sufficiently large primes \( p \) must be a square. This can be deduced from quadratic reciprocity and Dirichlet's theorem on primes in arithmetic progressions as follows. Suppose on the contrary that the result is false for the integer \( d \) . Consider primes \( p \) not dividing \( d \) . Since the product of two squares in \( {\mathbb{Z}}_{p} \) is again a square, we may assume that \( d \) is a product of distinct primes \( {q}_{1},\cdots ,{q}_{n} \) , where one of these primes is allowed to be -1 if \( d \) is negative. In terms of the Legendre symbol \( \left( \frac{d}{p}\right) \) which is defined to be +1 if \( d \) is a square mod \( p \) and -1 otherwise, we have

\[
\left( \frac{d}{p}\right)  = \left( \frac{{q}_{1}}{p}\right) \cdots \left( \frac{{q}_{n}}{p}\right)
\]

The left side is +1 for all large \( p \) by hypothesis, so it will suffice to see that \( p \) can be chosen to give each term on the right an arbitrary preassigned value. The values of \( \left( \frac{-1}{p}\right) \) and \( \left( \frac{2}{p}\right) \) depend only on \( p{\;\operatorname{mod}\;8} \) , and the four combinations of values are realized by the four residues \( 1,3,5,7{\;\operatorname{mod}\;8} \) . Having specified the value of \( p{\;\operatorname{mod}\;8} \) , the quadratic reciprocity law then says that for odd primes \( q \) , specifying \( \left( \frac{q}{p}\right) \) is equivalent to specifying \( \left( \frac{p}{q}\right) \) . Thus we need only choose \( p \) in the appropriate residue classes mod 8 and mod \( {q}_{i} \) for each odd \( {q}_{i} \) . By the Chinese remainder theorem, this means specifying \( p \) modulo 8 times a product of odd primes. Dirichlet’s theorem guarantees that in fact infinitely many primes \( p \) exist satisfying this congruence condition.

It is known that the integers realizable as degrees of maps \( {\mathbb{{HP}}}^{\infty } \rightarrow  {\mathbb{{HP}}}^{\infty } \) are exactly the odd squares and zero. The construction of maps of odd square degree will be given in [SSAT] using localization techniques, following [Sullivan 1974]. Ruling out nonzero even squares can be done using K-theory; see [Feder & Gitler 1978], which also treats maps \( {\mathbb{{HP}}}^{n} \rightarrow  {\mathbb{{HP}}}^{n} \) .

The preceding calculations can also be used to show that every map \( {\mathbb{{HP}}}^{n} \rightarrow  {\mathbb{{HP}}}^{n} \) must have a fixed point if \( n > 1 \) . For, taking \( p = 3 \) , the element \( {P}^{1}\left( \gamma \right) \) lies in \( {H}^{8}\left( {{\mathbb{{HP}}}^{n};{\mathbb{Z}}_{3}}\right) \) which is nonzero if \( n > 1 \) , so, when the earlier argument is specialized to the case \( p = 3 \) , the congruence \( {d}^{\left( {p + 1}\right) /2} \equiv  d{\;\operatorname{mod}\;p} \) becomes \( {d}^{2} = d \) in \( {\mathbb{Z}}_{3} \) , which is satisfied only by 0 and 1 in \( {\mathbb{Z}}_{3} \) . In particular, \( d \) is not equal to -1 . The Lefschetz number \( \lambda \left( f\right)  = 1 + d + \cdots  + {d}^{n} = \left( {{d}^{n + 1} - 1}\right) /\left( {d - 1}\right) \) is therefore nonzero since the only integer roots of unity are \( \pm  1 \) . The Lefschetz fixed point theorem then gives the result.

Example 4L.5: Vector Fields on Spheres. Let us now apply Steenrod squares to determine the maximum number of orthonormal tangent vector fields on a sphere in all cases except when the dimension of the sphere is congruent to -1 mod 16 . The first step is to rephrase the question in terms of Stiefel manifolds. Recall from the end of §3.D and Example 4.53 the space \( {V}_{n, k} \) of orthonormal \( k \) -frames in \( {\mathbb{R}}^{n} \) . Projection of a \( k \) -frame onto its first vector gives a map \( p : {V}_{n, k} \rightarrow  {S}^{n - 1} \) , and a section for this projection, that is, a map \( f : {S}^{n - 1} \rightarrow  {V}_{n, k} \) such that \( {pf} = \mathbb{1} \) , is exactly a set of \( k - 1 \) orthonormal tangent vector fields \( {v}_{1},\cdots ,{v}_{k - 1} \) on \( {S}^{n - 1} \) since \( f \) assigns to each \( x \in  {S}^{n - 1} \) an orthonormal \( k \) -frame \( \left( {x,{v}_{1}\left( x\right) ,\cdots ,{v}_{k - 1}\left( x\right) }\right) \) .

We described a cell structure on \( {V}_{n, k} \) at the end of §3.D, and we claim that the \( \left( {n - 1}\right) \) -skeleton of this cell structure is \( {\mathbb{{RP}}}^{n - 1}/{\mathbb{{RP}}}^{n - k - 1} \) if \( {2k} - 1 \leq  n \) . The cells of \( {V}_{n, k} \) were products \( {e}^{{i}_{1}} \times  \cdots  \times  {e}^{{i}_{m}} \) with \( n > {i}_{1} > \cdots  > {i}_{m} \geq  n - k \) , so the products with a single factor account for all of the \( \left( {{2n} - {2k}}\right) \) -skeleton, hence they account for all of the \( \left( {n - 1}\right) \) -skeleton if \( n - 1 \leq  {2n} - {2k} \) , that is, if \( {2k} - 1 \leq  n \) . The cells that are products with a single factor are the homeomorphic images of cells of \( {\mathbb{{RP}}}^{n - 1} \) under a map \( {\mathbb{{RP}}}^{n - 1} \rightarrow  {SO}\left( n\right)  \rightarrow  {SO}\left( n\right) /{SO}\left( {n - k}\right)  = {V}_{n, k} \) . This map collapses \( {\mathbb{{RP}}}^{n - k - 1} \) to a point, so we get the desired conclusion that \( {\mathbb{{RP}}}^{n - 1}/{\mathbb{{RP}}}^{n - k - 1} \) is the \( \left( {n - 1}\right) \) -skeleton of \( {V}_{n, k} \) if \( {2k} - 1 \leq  n \) .

Now suppose we have \( f : {S}^{n - 1} \rightarrow  {V}_{n, k} \) with \( {pf} = \mathbb{1} \) . In particular, \( {f}^{ * } \) is surjective on \( {H}^{n - 1}\left( {-;{\mathbb{Z}}_{2}}\right) \) . If we deform \( f \) to a cellular map, with image in the \( \left( {n - 1}\right) \) -skeleton, then by the preceding paragraph this will give a map \( g : {S}^{n - 1} \rightarrow  {\mathbb{{RP}}}^{n - 1}/{\mathbb{{RP}}}^{n - k - 1} \) if \( {2k} - 1 \leq  n \) , and this map will still induce a surjection on \( {H}^{n - 1}\left( {-;{\mathbb{Z}}_{2}}\right) \) , hence an isomorphism. If the number \( k \) happens to be such that \( \left( \begin{array}{l} n - k \\  k - 1 \end{array}\right)  \equiv  1{\;\operatorname{mod}\;2} \) , then by the earlier formula \( \left( *\right) \) the operation

\[
S{q}^{k - 1} : {H}^{n - k}\left( {{\mathbb{{RP}}}^{n - 1}/{\mathbb{{RP}}}^{n - k - 1};{\mathbb{Z}}_{2}}\right)  \rightarrow  {H}^{n - 1}\left( {{\mathbb{{RP}}}^{n - 1}/{\mathbb{{RP}}}^{n - k - 1};{\mathbb{Z}}_{2}}\right)
\]

will be nonzero, contradicting the existence of the map \( g \) since obviously the operation \( S{q}^{k - 1} : {H}^{n - k}\left( {{S}^{n - 1};{\mathbb{Z}}_{2}}\right)  \rightarrow  {H}^{n - 1}\left( {{S}^{n - 1};{\mathbb{Z}}_{2}}\right) \) is zero.

In order to guarantee that \( \left( \begin{array}{l} n - k \\  k - 1 \end{array}\right)  \equiv  1{\;\operatorname{mod}\;2} \) , write \( n = {2}^{r}\left( {{2s} + 1}\right) \) and choose \( k = {2}^{r} + 1 \) . Assume for the moment that \( s \geq  1 \) . Then \( \left( \begin{array}{l} n - k \\  k - 1 \end{array}\right)  = \left( \begin{matrix} {2}^{r + 1}s - 1 \\  {2}^{r} \end{matrix}\right) \) , and in view of the rule for computing binomial coefficients in \( {\mathbb{Z}}_{2} \) , this is nonzero since the dyadic expansion of \( {2}^{r + 1}s - 1 \) ends with a string of 1 ’s including a 1 in the single digit where the expansion of \( {2}^{r} \) is nonzero. Note that the earlier condition \( {2k} - 1 \leq  n \) is satisfied since it becomes \( {2}^{r + 1} + 1 \leq  {2}^{r + 1}s + {2}^{r} \) and we assume \( s \geq  1 \) .

Summarizing, we have shown that for \( n = {2}^{r}\left( {{2s} + 1}\right) \) , the sphere \( {S}^{n - 1} \) cannot have \( {2}^{r} \) orthonormal tangent vector fields if \( s \geq  1 \) . This is also trivially true for \( s = 0 \) since \( {S}^{n - 1} \) cannot have \( n \) orthonormal tangent vector fields.

It is easy to see that this result is best possible when \( r \leq  3 \) by explicitly constructing \( {2}^{r} - 1 \) orthonormal tangent vector fields on \( {S}^{n - 1} \) when \( n = {2}^{r}m \) . When \( r = 1 \) , view \( {S}^{n - 1} \) as the unit sphere in \( {\mathbb{C}}^{m} \) , and then \( x \mapsto  {ix} \) defines a tangent vector field since the unit complex numbers 1 and \( i \) are orthogonal and multiplication by a unit complex number is an isometry of \( \mathbb{C} \) , so \( x \) and \( {ix} \) are orthogonal in each coordinate of \( {\mathbb{C}}^{m} \) , hence are orthogonal. When \( r = 2 \) the same construction works with \( \mathbb{H} \) in place of \( \mathbb{C} \) , using the maps \( x \mapsto  {ix}, x \mapsto  {jx} \) , and \( x \mapsto  {kx} \) to define three orthonormal tangent vector fields on the unit sphere in \( {\mathbb{H}}^{m} \) . When \( r = 3 \) we can follow the same procedure with the octonions, constructing seven orthonormal tangent vector fields to the unit sphere in \( {\mathbb{O}}^{m} \) via an orthonormal basis \( 1, i, j, k,\cdots \) for \( \mathbb{O} \) .

The upper bound of \( {2}^{r} - 1 \) for the number of orthonormal vector fields on \( {S}^{n - 1} \) is not best possible in the remaining case \( n \equiv  0{\;\operatorname{mod}\;{16}} \) . The optimal upper bound is obtained instead using K-theory; see [VBKT] or [Husemoller 1966]. The construction of the requisite number of vector fields is again algebraic, this time using Clifford algebras.

Example 4L.6: A Map of mod \( p \) Hopf Invariant One. Let us describe a construction for a map \( f : {S}^{2p} \rightarrow  {S}^{3} \) such that in the mapping cone \( {C}_{f} = {S}^{3}{ \cup  }_{f}{e}^{{2p} + 1} \) , the first Steen-rod power \( {P}^{1} : {H}^{3}\left( {{C}_{f};{\mathbb{Z}}_{p}}\right)  \rightarrow  {H}^{{2p} + 1}\left( {{C}_{f};{\mathbb{Z}}_{p}}\right) \) is nonzero, hence \( f \) is nonzero in \( {\pi }_{{2p} - 3}^{s} \) . The construction starts with the fact that a generator of \( {H}^{2}\left( {K\left( {{\mathbb{Z}}_{p},1}\right) ;{\mathbb{Z}}_{p}}\right) \) has nontrivial \( {p}^{th} \) power, so the operation \( {P}^{1} : {H}^{2}\left( {K\left( {{\mathbb{Z}}_{p},1}\right) ;{\mathbb{Z}}_{p}}\right)  \rightarrow  {H}^{2p}\left( {K\left( {{\mathbb{Z}}_{p},1}\right) ;{\mathbb{Z}}_{p}}\right) \) is nontrivial by property (5). This remains true after we suspend to \( {\sum K}\left( {{\mathbb{Z}}_{p},1}\right) \) , and we showed in Proposition 4I.3 that \( {\sum K}\left( {{\mathbb{Z}}_{p},1}\right) \) has the homotopy type of a wedge sum of CW complexes \( {X}_{i},1 \leq  i \leq  p - 1 \) , with \( {\widetilde{H}}_{ * }\left( {{X}_{i};\mathbb{Z}}\right) \) consisting only of a \( {\mathbb{Z}}_{p} \) in each dimension congruent to \( {2i}{\;\operatorname{mod}\;2}p - 2 \) . We are interested here in the space \( X = {X}_{1} \) , which has nontrivial \( {\mathbb{Z}}_{p} \) cohomology in dimensions \( 2,3,{2p},{2p} + 1,\cdots \) . Since \( X \) is, up to homotopy, a wedge summand of \( {\sum K}\left( {{\mathbb{Z}}_{p},1}\right) \) , the operation \( {P}^{1} : {H}^{3}\left( {X;{\mathbb{Z}}_{p}}\right)  \rightarrow  {H}^{{2p} + 1}\left( {X;{\mathbb{Z}}_{p}}\right) \) is nontrivial. Since \( X \) is simply-connected, the construction in §4.C shows that we may take \( X \) to have \( \left( {{2p} + 1}\right) \) -skeleton of the form \( {S}^{2} \cup  {e}^{3} \cup  {e}^{2p} \cup  {e}^{{2p} + 1} \) . In fact, using the notion of homology decomposition in \( \text{ § }4.\mathrm{H} \) , we can take this skeleton to be the reduced mapping cone \( {C}_{g} \) of a map of Moore spaces \( g : M\left( {{\mathbb{Z}}_{p},{2p} - 1}\right)  \rightarrow  M\left( {{\mathbb{Z}}_{p},2}\right) \) . It follows that the quotient \( {C}_{g}/{S}^{2} \) is the reduced mapping cone of the composition \( h : M\left( {{\mathbb{Z}}_{p},{2p} - 1}\right) \overset{g}{ \rightarrow  }M\left( {{\mathbb{Z}}_{p},2}\right)  \rightarrow  M\left( {{\mathbb{Z}}_{p},2}\right) /{S}^{2} = {S}^{3} \) . The restriction \( h \mid  {S}^{{2p} - 1} \) represents an element of \( {\pi }_{{2p} - 1}\left( {S}^{3}\right) \) that is either trivial or has order \( p \) since this restriction extends over the \( {2p} \) -cell of \( M\left( {{\mathbb{Z}}_{p},{2p} - 1}\right) \) which is attached by a map \( {S}^{{2p} - 1} \rightarrow  {S}^{{2p} - 1} \) of degree \( p \) . In fact, \( h \mid  {S}^{{2p} - 1} \) is nullhomotopic since, as we will see in [SSAT] using the Serre spectral sequence, \( {\pi }_{i}\left( {S}^{3}\right) \) contains no elements of order \( p \) if \( i \leq  {2p} - 1 \) . This implies that the space \( {C}_{h} = {C}_{g}/{S}^{2} \) is homotopy equivalent to a CW complex \( Y \) obtained from \( {S}^{3} \vee  {S}^{2p} \) by attaching a cell \( {e}^{{2p} + 1} \) . The quotient \( Y/{S}^{2p} \) then has the form \( {S}^{3} \cup  {e}^{{2p} + 1} \) , so it is the mapping cone of a map \( f : {S}^{2p} \rightarrow  {S}^{3} \) . By construction there is a map \( {C}_{g} \rightarrow  {C}_{f} \) inducing an isomorphism on \( {\mathbb{Z}}_{p} \) cohomology in dimensions 3 and \( {2p} + 1 \) , so the operation \( {P}^{1} \) is nontrivial in \( {H}^{ * }\left( {{C}_{f};{\mathbb{Z}}_{p}}\right) \) since this was true for \( {C}_{g} \) , the \( \left( {{2p} + 1}\right) \) -skeleton of \( X \) .

Example 4L.7: Moore Spaces. Let us use the operation \( S{q}^{2} \) to show that for \( n \geq  2 \) , the identity map of \( M\left( {{\mathbb{Z}}_{2}, n}\right) \) has order 4 in the group of basepoint-preserving homotopy classes of maps \( M\left( {{\mathbb{Z}}_{2}, n}\right)  \rightarrow  M\left( {{\mathbb{Z}}_{2}, n}\right) \) , with addition defined via the suspension structure on \( M\left( {{\mathbb{Z}}_{2}, n}\right)  = {\sum M}\left( {{\mathbb{Z}}_{2}, n - 1}\right) \) . According to Proposition 4H.2, this group is the middle term of a short exact sequence, the remaining terms of which contain only elements of order 2. Hence if the identity map of \( M\left( {{\mathbb{Z}}_{2}, n}\right) \) has order 4, this short exact sequence cannot split.

In view of the short exact sequence just referred to, it will suffice to show that twice the identity map of \( M\left( {{\mathbb{Z}}_{2}, n}\right) \) is not nullhomotopic. If twice the identity were nullhomotopic, then the mapping cone \( C \) of this map would have the homotopy type of \( M\left( {{\mathbb{Z}}_{2}, n}\right)  \vee  {\sum M}\left( {{\mathbb{Z}}_{2}, n}\right) \) . This would force \( S{q}^{2} : {H}^{n}\left( {C;{\mathbb{Z}}_{2}}\right)  \rightarrow  {H}^{n + 2}\left( {C;{\mathbb{Z}}_{2}}\right) \) to be trivial since the source and target groups would come from different wedge summands. However, we will now show that this \( S{q}^{2} \) operation is nontrivial. Twice the identity map of \( M\left( {{\mathbb{Z}}_{2}, n}\right) \) can be regarded as the smash product of the degree 2 map \( {S}^{1} \rightarrow  {S}^{1} \) , \( z \mapsto  {z}^{2} \) , with the identity map of \( M\left( {{\mathbb{Z}}_{2}, n - 1}\right) \) . If we smash the cofibration sequence \( {S}^{1} \rightarrow  {S}^{1} \rightarrow  {\mathbb{{RP}}}^{2} \) for this degree 2 map with \( M\left( {{\mathbb{Z}}_{2}, n - 1}\right) \) we get the cofiber sequence \( M\left( {{\mathbb{Z}}_{2}, n}\right)  \rightarrow  M\left( {{\mathbb{Z}}_{2}, n}\right)  \rightarrow  C \) , in view of the identity \( \left( {X/A}\right)  \land  Y = \left( {X \land  Y}\right) /\left( {A \land  Y}\right) \) . This means we can view \( C \) as \( {\mathbb{{RP}}}^{2} \land  M\left( {{\mathbb{Z}}_{2}, n - 1}\right) \) . The Cartan formula translated to cross products gives \( S{q}^{2}\left( {\alpha  \times  \beta }\right)  = S{q}^{0}\alpha  \times  S{q}^{2}\beta  + S{q}^{1}\alpha  \times  S{q}^{1}\beta  + S{q}^{2}\alpha  \times  S{q}^{0}\beta \) . This holds for smash products as well as ordinary products, by naturality. Taking \( \alpha \) to be a generator of \( {H}^{1}\left( {{\mathbb{{RP}}}^{2};{\mathbb{Z}}_{2}}\right) \) and \( \beta \) a generator of \( {H}^{n - 1}\left( {M\left( {{\mathbb{Z}}_{2}, n - 1}\right) ;{\mathbb{Z}}_{2}}\right) \) , we have \( {\operatorname{Sq}}^{2}\alpha  = 0 = \; S{q}^{2}\beta \) , but \( S{q}^{1}\alpha \) and \( S{q}^{1}\beta \) are nonzero since \( S{q}^{1} \) is the Bockstein. By the Künneth formula, \( S{q}^{1}\alpha  \times  S{q}^{1}\beta \) then generates \( {H}^{n + 2}\left( {{\mathbb{{RP}}}^{2} \land  M\left( {{\mathbb{Z}}_{2}, n - 1}\right) ;{\mathbb{Z}}_{2}}\right) \) and we are done.

## Adem Relations and the Steenrod Algebra

When Steenrod squares or powers are composed, the compositions satisfy certain relations, unfortunately rather complicated, known as Adem relations:

\[
S{q}^{a}S{q}^{b} = \mathop{\sum }\limits_{j}\left( \begin{matrix} b - j - 1 \\  a - {2j} \end{matrix}\right) S{q}^{a + b - j}S{q}^{j}\;\text{ if }a < {2b}
\]

\[
{P}^{a}{P}^{b} = \mathop{\sum }\limits_{j}{\left( -1\right) }^{a + j}\left( \begin{matrix} \left( {p - 1}\right) \left( {b - j}\right)  - 1 \\  a - {pj} \end{matrix}\right) {P}^{a + b - j}{P}^{j}\;\text{ if }a < {pb}
\]

\[
{P}^{a}\beta {P}^{b} = \mathop{\sum }\limits_{j}{\left( -1\right) }^{a + j}\left( \begin{matrix} \left( {p - 1}\right) \left( {b - j}\right) \\  a - {pj} \end{matrix}\right) \beta {P}^{a + b - j}{P}^{j}
\]

\[
- \mathop{\sum }\limits_{j}{\left( -1\right) }^{a + j}\left( \begin{matrix} \left( {p - 1}\right) \left( {b - j}\right)  - 1 \\  a - {pj} - 1 \end{matrix}\right) {P}^{a + b - j}\beta {P}^{j}\;\text{ if }a \leq  {pb}
\]

By convention, the binomial coefficient \( \left( \begin{array}{l} m \\  n \end{array}\right) \) is taken to be zero if \( m \) or \( n \) is negative or if \( m < n \) . Also \( \left( \begin{matrix} m \\  0 \end{matrix}\right)  = 1 \) for \( m \geq  0 \) .

For example, taking \( a = 1 \) in the Adem relation for the Steenrod squares we have \( S{q}^{1}S{q}^{b} = \left( {b - 1}\right) S{q}^{b + 1} \) , so \( S{q}^{1}S{q}^{2i} = S{q}^{{2i} + 1} \) and \( S{q}^{1}S{q}^{{2i} + 1} = 0 \) . The relations \( S{q}^{1}S{q}^{2i} = S{q}^{{2i} + 1} \) and \( S{q}^{1} = \beta \) explain the earlier comment that \( S{q}^{2i} \) is the analog of \( {P}^{i} \) for \( p = 2 \) .

The Steenrod algebra \( {\mathcal{A}}_{2} \) is defined to be the algebra over \( {\mathbb{Z}}_{2} \) that is the quotient of the algebra of polynomials in the noncommuting variables \( S{q}^{1}, S{q}^{2},\cdots \) by the two-sided ideal generated by the Adem relations, that is, by the polynomials given by the differences between the left and right sides of the Adem relations. In similar fashion, \( {\mathcal{A}}_{p} \) for odd \( p \) is defined to be the algebra over \( {\mathbb{Z}}_{p} \) formed by polynomials in the noncommuting variables \( \beta ,{P}^{1},{P}^{2},\cdots \) modulo the Adem relations and the relation \( {\beta }^{2} = 0 \) . Thus for every space \( X,{H}^{ * }\left( {X;{\mathbb{Z}}_{p}}\right) \) is a module over \( {\mathcal{A}}_{p} \) , for all primes \( p \) . The Steenrod algebra is a graded algebra, the elements of degree \( k \) being those that map \( {H}^{n}\left( {X;{\mathbb{Z}}_{p}}\right) \) to \( {H}^{n + k}\left( {X;{\mathbb{Z}}_{p}}\right) \) for all \( n \) .

The next proposition implies that \( {\mathcal{A}}_{2} \) is generated as an algebra by the elements \( S{q}^{{2}^{k}} \) , while \( {\mathcal{A}}_{p} \) for \( p \) odd is generated by \( \beta \) and the elements \( {P}^{{p}^{k}} \) .

Proposition 4L.8. There is a relation \( S{q}^{i} = \mathop{\sum }\limits_{{0 < j < i}}{a}_{j}S{q}^{i - j}S{q}^{j} \) with coefficients \( {a}_{j} \in  {\mathbb{Z}}_{2} \) whenever \( i \) is not a power of 2 . Similarly, if \( i \) is not a power of \( p \) there is a relation \( {P}^{i} = \mathop{\sum }\limits_{{0 < j < i}}{a}_{j}{P}^{i - j}{P}^{j} \) with \( {a}_{j} \in  {\mathbb{Z}}_{p} \) .

Proof: The argument is the same for \( p = 2 \) and \( p \) odd, so we describe the latter case. The idea is to write \( i \) as the sum \( a + b \) of integers \( a > 0 \) and \( b > 0 \) with \( a < {pb} \) , such that the coefficient of the \( j = 0 \) term in the Adem relation for \( {P}^{a}{P}^{b} \) is nonzero. Then one can solve this relation for \( {P}^{a + b} = {P}^{i} \) .

Let the \( p \) -adic representation of \( i \) be \( i = {i}_{0} + {i}_{1}p + \cdots  + {i}_{k}{p}^{k} \) with \( {i}_{k} \neq  0 \) . Let \( b = {p}^{k} \) and \( a = i - {p}^{k} \) , so \( b > 0 \) and \( a > 0 \) if \( i \) is not a power of \( p \) . The claim is that \( \left( \begin{matrix} \left( {p - 1}\right) b - 1 \\  a \end{matrix}\right) \) is nonzero in \( {\mathbb{Z}}_{p} \) . The \( p \) -adic expansion of \( \left( {p - 1}\right) b - 1 = \; \left( {{p}^{k + 1} - 1}\right)  - {p}^{k} \) is \( \left( {p - 1}\right)  + \left( {p - 1}\right) p + \cdots  + \left( {p - 2}\right) {p}^{k} \) , and the \( p \) -adic expansion of \( a \) is \( {i}_{0} + {i}_{1}p + \cdots  + \left( {{i}_{k} - 1}\right) {p}^{k} \) . Hence \( \left( \begin{matrix} \left( {p - 1}\right) b - 1 \\  a \end{matrix}\right)  \equiv  \left( \begin{matrix} p - 1 \\  {i}_{0} \end{matrix}\right) \cdots \left( \begin{matrix} p - 2 \\  {i}_{k} - 1 \end{matrix}\right) \) and in each factor of the latter product the numerator is nonzero in \( {\mathbb{Z}}_{p} \) so the product is nonzero in \( {\mathbb{Z}}_{p} \) . When \( p = 2 \) the last factor is omitted, and the product is still nonzero in \( {\mathbb{Z}}_{2} \) .

This proposition says that most of the \( S{q}^{i} \) ’s and \( {P}^{i} \) ’s are decomposable, where an element \( a \) of a graded algebra such as \( {\mathcal{A}}_{p} \) is decomposable if it can be expressed in the form \( \mathop{\sum }\limits_{i}{a}_{i}{b}_{i} \) with each \( {a}_{i} \) and \( {b}_{i} \) having lower degree than \( a \) . The operation \( S{q}^{{2}^{k}} \) is indecomposable since for \( \alpha \) a generator of \( {H}^{1}\left( {{\mathbb{{RP}}}^{\infty };{\mathbb{Z}}_{2}}\right) \) we saw that \( S{q}^{{2}^{k}}\left( {\alpha }^{{2}^{k}}\right)  = \; {\alpha }^{{2}^{k + 1}} \) but \( S{q}^{i}\left( {\alpha }^{{2}^{k}}\right)  = 0 \) for \( 0 < i < {2}^{k} \) . Similarly \( {P}^{{p}^{k}} \) is indecomposable since if \( \alpha  \in  {H}^{2}\left( {{\mathbb{{CP}}}^{\infty };{\mathbb{Z}}_{p}}\right) \) is a generator then \( {P}^{{p}^{k}}\left( {\alpha }^{{p}^{k}}\right)  = {\alpha }^{{p}^{k + 1}} \) but \( {P}^{i}\left( {\alpha }^{{p}^{k}}\right)  = 0 \) for \( 0 < i < {p}^{k} \) and also \( \beta \left( {\alpha }^{{p}^{k}}\right)  = 0 \) .

Here is an application of the preceding proposition:

Theorem 4L.9. Suppose \( {H}^{ * }\left( {X;{\mathbb{Z}}_{p}}\right) \) is the polynomial algebra \( {\mathbb{Z}}_{p}\left\lbrack  \alpha \right\rbrack \) on a generator \( \alpha \) of dimension \( n \) , possibly truncated by the relation \( {\alpha }^{m} = 0 \) for \( m > p \) . Then if \( p = 2, n \) must be a power of 2, and if \( p \) is an odd prime, \( n \) must be of the form \( {p}^{k}\ell \) where \( \ell \) is an even divisor of \( 2\left( {p - 1}\right) \) .

As we mentioned in §3.2, there is a stronger theorem that \( n \) must be1,2,4, or 8 when \( p = 2 \) , and \( n \) must be an even divisor of \( 2\left( {p - 1}\right) \) when \( p \) is an odd prime. We also gave examples showing the necessity of the hypothesis \( m > p \) in the case of a truncated polynomial algebra.

Proof: In the case \( p = 2, S{q}^{n}\left( \alpha \right)  = {\alpha }^{2} \neq  0 \) . If \( n \) is not a power of 2 then \( S{q}^{n} \) decomposes into compositions \( S{q}^{n - j}S{q}^{j} \) with \( 0 < j < n \) . Such compositions must be zero since they pass through the group \( {H}^{n + j}\left( {X;{\mathbb{Z}}_{2}}\right) \) which is zero for \( 0 < j < n \) .

For odd \( p \) , the fact that \( {\alpha }^{2} \) is nonzero implies that \( n \) is even, say \( n = {2k} \) . Then \( {P}^{k}\left( \alpha \right)  = {\alpha }^{p} \neq  0 \) . Since \( {P}^{k} \) can be expressed in terms of \( {P}^{{p}^{i}} \) ’s, some \( {P}^{{p}^{i}} \) must be nonzero in \( {H}^{ * }\left( {X;{\mathbb{Z}}_{p}}\right) \) . This implies that \( 2{p}^{i}\left( {p - 1}\right) \) , the amount by which \( {P}^{{p}^{i}} \) raises dimension, must be a multiple of \( n \) since \( {H}^{ * }\left( {X;{\mathbb{Z}}_{p}}\right) \) is concentrated in dimensions that are multiples of \( n \) . Since \( n \) divides \( 2{p}^{i}\left( {p - 1}\right) \) , it must be a power of \( p \) times a divisor of \( 2\left( {p - 1}\right) \) , and this divisor must be even since \( n \) is even and \( p \) is odd.

Corollary 4L.10. If \( {H}^{ * }\left( {X;\mathbb{Z}}\right) \) is a polynomial algebra \( \mathbb{Z}\left\lbrack  \alpha \right\rbrack \) , possibly truncated by \( {\alpha }^{m} = 0 \) with \( m > 3 \) , then \( \left| \alpha \right|  = 2 \) or 4 .

Proof: Passing from \( \mathbb{Z} \) to \( {\mathbb{Z}}_{2} \) coefficients, the theorem implies that \( \left| \alpha \right| \) is a power of 2, and taking \( {\mathbb{Z}}_{3} \) coefficients we see that \( \left| \alpha \right| \) is a power of 3 times a divisor of \( 2\left( {3 - 1}\right)  = 4 \) .

In particular, the octonionic projective plane \( {\mathbb{{OP}}}^{2} \) , constructed in Example 4.47 by attaching a 16-cell to \( {S}^{8} \) via the Hopf map \( {S}^{15} \rightarrow  {S}^{8} \) , does not generalize to an octonionic projective \( n \) -space \( {\mathbb{{OP}}}^{n} \) with \( n \geq  3 \) .

In a similar vein, decomposability implies that if an element of \( {\pi }_{ * }^{s} \) is detected by a \( S{q}^{i} \) or \( {P}^{i} \) then \( i \) must be a power of 2 for \( S{q}^{i} \) and a power of \( p \) for \( {P}^{i} \) . For if \( S{q}^{i} \) is decomposable, then the map \( S{q}^{i} : {H}^{n}\left( {{C}_{f} : {\mathbb{Z}}_{2}}\right)  \rightarrow  {H}^{n + i}\left( {{C}_{f};{\mathbb{Z}}_{2}}\right) \) must be trivial since it is a sum of compositions that pass through trivial cohomology groups, and similarly for \( {P}^{i} \) .

Interestingly enough, the Adem relations can also be used in a positive way to detect elements of \( {\pi }_{ * }^{s} \) , as the proof of the following result will show.

Proposition 4L.11. If \( \eta  \in  {\pi }_{1}^{s} \) is represented by the Hopf map \( {S}^{3} \rightarrow  {S}^{2} \) , then \( {\eta }^{2} \) is nonzero in \( {\pi }_{2}^{s} \) . Similarly, the other two Hopf maps represent elements \( v \in  {\pi }_{3}^{s} \) and \( \sigma  \in  {\pi }_{7}^{s} \) whose squares are nontrivial in \( {\pi }_{6}^{s} \) and \( {\pi }_{14}^{s} \) .

Proof: Let \( \eta  : {S}^{n + 1} \rightarrow  {S}^{n} \) be a suspension of the Hopf map, with mapping cone \( {C}_{\eta } \) obtained from \( {S}^{n} \) by attaching a cell \( {e}^{n + 2} \) via \( \eta \) . If we assume the composition \( \left( {\sum \eta }\right) \eta \) is nullhomotopic, then we can define a map \( f : {S}^{n + 3} \rightarrow  {C}_{\eta } \) in the following way. Decompose \( {S}^{n + 3} \) as the union of two cones \( C{S}^{n + 2} \) . On one of these cones let \( f \) be a nullhomotopy of \( \left( {\sum \eta }\right) \eta \) . On the other cone let \( f \) be the composition \( C{S}^{n + 2} \rightarrow  C{S}^{n + 1} \rightarrow  {C}_{\eta } \) where the first map is obtained by coning \( {\sum \eta } \) and the second map is a characteristic map for the cell \( {e}^{n + 2} \) .

![bo_d7dtv0s91nqc73eq8nv0_506_325_1962_1154_291_0.jpg](images/bo_d7dtv0s91nqc73eq8nv0_506_325_1962_1154_291_0.jpg)

We use the map \( f \) to attach a cell \( {e}^{n + 4} \) to \( {C}_{\eta } \) , forming a space \( X \) . This has \( {C}_{\eta } \) as its \( \left( {n + 2}\right) \) -skeleton, so \( S{q}^{2} : {H}^{n}\left( {X;{\mathbb{Z}}_{2}}\right)  \rightarrow  {H}^{n + 2}\left( {X;{\mathbb{Z}}_{2}}\right) \) is an isomorphism. The map \( S{q}^{2} : {H}^{n + 2}\left( {X;{\mathbb{Z}}_{2}}\right)  \rightarrow  {H}^{n + 4}\left( {X;{\mathbb{Z}}_{2}}\right) \) is also an isomorphism since the quotient map \( X \rightarrow  X/{S}^{n} \) induces an isomorphism on cohomology groups above dimension \( n \) and \( X/{S}^{n} \) is homotopy equivalent to the mapping cone of \( {\sum }^{2}\eta \) . Thus the composition \( {\mathrm{{Sq}}}^{2}{\mathrm{{Sq}}}^{2} : {H}^{n}\left( {X;{\mathbb{Z}}_{2}}\right)  \rightarrow  {H}^{n + 4}\left( {X;{\mathbb{Z}}_{2}}\right) \) is an isomorphism. But this is impossible in view of the Adem relation \( S{q}^{2}S{q}^{2} = S{q}^{3}S{q}^{1} \) , since \( S{q}^{1} \) is trivial on \( {H}^{n}\left( {X;{\mathbb{Z}}_{2}}\right) \) .

The same argument shows that \( {v}^{2} \) and \( {\sigma }^{2} \) are nontrivial using the relations \( S{q}^{4}S{q}^{4} = S{q}^{7}S{q}^{1} + S{q}^{6}S{q}^{2} \) and \( S{q}^{8}S{q}^{8} = S{q}^{15}S{q}^{1} + S{q}^{14}S{q}^{2} + S{q}^{12}S{q}^{4}. \)

This line of reasoning does not work for odd primes and the element \( \alpha  \in  {\pi }_{{2p} - 3}^{s} \) detected by \( {P}^{1} \) since the Adem relation for \( {P}^{1}{P}^{1} \) is \( {P}^{1}{P}^{1} = 2{P}^{2} \) , which is not helpful. And in fact \( {\alpha }^{2} = 0 \) by the commutativity property of the product in \( {\pi }_{ * }^{s} \) .

When dealing with \( {\mathcal{A}}_{2} \) it is often convenient to abbreviate notation by writing a monomial \( S{q}^{{i}_{1}}S{q}^{{i}_{2}}\cdots \) as \( S{q}^{I} \) where \( I \) is the finite sequence of nonnegative integers \( {i}_{1},{i}_{2},\cdots \) . Call \( S{q}^{I} \) admissible if no Adem relation can be applied to it, that is, if \( {i}_{j} \geq  2{i}_{j + 1} \) for all \( j \) . The Adem relations imply that every monomial \( S{q}^{I} \) can be written as a sum of admissible monomials. For if \( S{q}^{I} \) is not admissible, it contains a pair \( S{q}^{a}S{q}^{b} \) to which an Adem relation can be applied, yielding a sum of terms \( S{q}^{J} \) for which \( J > I \) with respect to the lexicographic ordering on finite sequences of integers. These \( S{q}^{J} \) ’s have the same degree \( {i}_{1} + \cdots  + {i}_{k} \) as \( S{q}^{I} \) , and since the number of monomials \( S{q}^{I} \) of a fixed degree is finite, successive applications of the Adem relations eventually reduce any \( S{q}^{I} \) to a sum of admissible monomials.

For odd \( p \) , elements of \( {\mathcal{A}}_{p} \) are linear combinations of monomials \( {\beta }^{{\varepsilon }_{1}}{P}^{{i}_{1}}{\beta }^{{\varepsilon }_{2}}{P}^{{i}_{2}}\cdots \) with each \( {\varepsilon }_{j} = 0 \) or 1 . Such a monomial is admissible if \( {i}_{j} \geq  {\varepsilon }_{j + 1} + p{i}_{j + 1} \) for all \( j \) , which again means that no Adem relation can be applied to the monomial. As with \( {\mathcal{A}}_{2} \) , the Adem relations suffice to reduce every monomial to a linear combination of admissible monomials, by the same argument as before but now using the lexicographic ordering on tuples \( \left( {{\varepsilon }_{1} + p{i}_{1},{\varepsilon }_{2} + p{i}_{2},\cdots }\right) \) .

Define the excess of the admissible monomial \( S{q}^{I} \) to be \( \mathop{\sum }\limits_{j}\left( {{i}_{j} - 2{i}_{j + 1}}\right) \) , the amount by which \( S{q}^{I} \) exceeds being admissible. For odd \( p \) one might expect the excess of an admissible monomial \( {\beta }^{{\varepsilon }_{1}}{P}^{{i}_{1}}{\beta }^{{\varepsilon }_{2}}{P}^{{i}_{2}}\cdots \) to be defined as \( \mathop{\sum }\limits_{j}\left( {{i}_{j} - p{i}_{j + 1} - {\varepsilon }_{j + 1}}\right) \) , but instead it is defined to be \( \mathop{\sum }\limits_{j}\left( {2{i}_{j} - {2p}{i}_{j + 1} - {\varepsilon }_{j + 1}}\right) \) , for reasons which will become clear below.

As we explained at the beginning of this section, cohomology operations correspond to elements in the cohomology of Eilenberg-MacLane spaces. Here is a rather important theorem which will be proved in [SSAT] since the proof makes heavy use of spectral sequences:

Theorem. For each prime \( p,{H}^{ * }\left( {K\left( {{\mathbb{Z}}_{p}, n}\right) ;{\mathbb{Z}}_{p}}\right) \) is the free commutative algebra on the generators \( \Theta \left( {\iota }_{n}\right) \) where \( {\iota }_{n} \in  {H}^{n}\left( {K\left( {{\mathbb{Z}}_{p}, n}\right) ;{\mathbb{Z}}_{p}}\right) \) is a generator and \( \Theta \) ranges over all admissible monomials of excess less than \( n \) .

Here ’free commutative algebra’ means ’polynomial algebra’ when \( p = 2 \) and 'polynomial algebra on even-dimensional generators tensor exterior algebra on odd-dimensional generators’ when \( p \) is odd. We will say something about the rationale behind the ’excess less than \( n \) ’ condition in a moment.

Specializing the theorem to the first two cases \( n = 1,2 \) , we have the following cohomology algebras:

\[
K\left( {{\mathbb{Z}}_{2},1}\right)  : \;{\mathbb{Z}}_{2}\left\lbrack  \iota \right\rbrack
\]

\[
K\left( {{\mathbb{Z}}_{p},1}\right)  : \;{\Lambda }_{{\mathbb{Z}}_{p}}\left\lbrack  \iota \right\rbrack   \otimes  {\mathbb{Z}}_{p}\left\lbrack  {\beta \iota }\right\rbrack
\]

\[
K\left( {{\mathbb{Z}}_{2},2}\right)  : \;{\mathbb{Z}}_{2}\left\lbrack  {\iota , S{q}^{1}\iota , S{q}^{2}S{q}^{1}\iota , S{q}^{4}S{q}^{2}S{q}^{1}\iota ,\cdots }\right\rbrack
\]

\[
K\left( {{\mathbb{Z}}_{p},2}\right)  : \;{\mathbb{Z}}_{p}\left\lbrack  {\iota ,\beta {P}^{1}{\beta \iota },\beta {P}^{p}{P}^{1}{\beta \iota },\beta {P}^{{p}^{2}}{P}^{p}{P}^{1}{\beta \iota },\cdots }\right\rbrack
\]

\[
\otimes  {\Lambda }_{{\mathbb{Z}}_{p}}\left\lbrack  {{\beta \iota },{P}^{1}{\beta \iota },{P}^{p}{P}^{1}{\beta \iota },{P}^{{p}^{2}}{P}^{p}{P}^{1}{\beta \iota },\cdots }\right\rbrack
\]

The theorem implies that the admissible monomials in \( {\mathcal{A}}_{p} \) are linearly independent, hence form a basis for \( {\mathcal{A}}_{p} \) as a vector space over \( {\mathbb{Z}}_{p} \) . For if some linear combination of admissible monomials were zero, then it would be zero when applied to the class \( {\iota }_{n} \) , but if we choose \( n \) larger than the excess of each monomial in the linear combination, this would contradict the freeness of the algebra \( {H}^{ * }\left( {K\left( {{\mathbb{Z}}_{p}, n}\right) ;{\mathbb{Z}}_{p}}\right) \) . Even though the multiplicative structure of the Steenrod algebra is rather complicated, the Adem relations provide a way of performing calculations algorithmically by systematically reducing all products to sums of admissible monomials. A proof of the linear independence of admissible monomials using more elementary techniques can be found in [Steenrod & Epstein 1962].

Another consequence of the theorem is that all cohomology operations with \( {\mathbb{Z}}_{p} \) coefficients are polynomials in the \( S{q}^{i} \) ’s when \( p = 2 \) and polynomials in the \( {P}^{i} \) ’s and \( \beta \) when \( p \) is odd, in view of Proposition 4L.1. We can also conclude that \( {\mathcal{A}}_{p} \) consists precisely of all the \( {\mathbb{Z}}_{p} \) cohomology operations that are stable, commuting with suspension. For consider the map \( {\sum K}\left( {{\mathbb{Z}}_{p}, n}\right)  \rightarrow  K\left( {{\mathbb{Z}}_{p}, n + 1}\right) \) that pulls \( {\iota }_{n + 1} \) back to the suspension of \( {\iota }_{n} \) . This map induces an isomorphism on homotopy groups \( {\pi }_{i} \) for \( i \leq  {2n} \) and a surjection for \( i = {2n} + 1 \) by Corollary 4.24, hence the same is true for homology and cohomology. Letting \( n \) go to infinity, the limit \( \underline{\lim }{\widetilde{H}}^{ * }\left( {K\left( {{\mathbb{Z}}_{p}, n}\right) ;{\mathbb{Z}}_{p}}\right) \) then exists in a strong sense. On the one hand, this limit is exactly the stable operations by Proposition 4L.1 and the definition of a stable operation. On the other hand, the preceding theorem implies that this limit is \( {\mathcal{A}}_{p} \) since it says that all elements of \( {H}^{ * }\left( {K\left( {{\mathbb{Z}}_{p}, n}\right) ;{\mathbb{Z}}_{p}}\right) \) below dimension \( {2n} \) are uniquely expressible as sums of admissible monomials applied to \( {\iota }_{n} \) .

Now let us explain why the condition ’excess less than \( n \) ’ in the theorem is natural. For a monomial \( S{q}^{I} = S{q}^{{i}_{1}}S{q}^{{i}_{2}}\cdots \) the definition of the excess \( e\left( I\right) \) can be rewritten as an equation \( {i}_{1} = e\left( I\right)  + {i}_{2} + {i}_{3} + \cdots \) . Thus if \( e\left( I\right)  > n \) , we have \( {i}_{1} > \left| {S{q}^{{i}_{2}}S{q}^{{i}_{3}}\cdots \left( {\iota }_{n}\right) }\right| \) , hence \( S{q}^{I}\left( {\iota }_{n}\right)  = 0 \) . And if \( e\left( I\right)  = n \) then \( S{q}^{I}\left( {\iota }_{n}\right)  = {\left( S{q}^{{i}_{2}}S{q}^{{i}_{3}}\cdots \left( {\iota }_{n}\right) \right) }^{2} \) and either \( S{q}^{{i}_{2}}S{q}^{{i}_{3}}\cdots \) has excess less than \( n \) or it has excess equal to \( n \) and we can repeat the process to write \( S{q}^{{i}_{2}}S{q}^{{i}_{3}}\cdots \left( {\iota }_{n}\right)  = {\left( S{q}^{{i}_{3}}\cdots \left( {\iota }_{n}\right) \right) }^{2} \) , and so on, until we obtain an equation \( S{q}^{I}\left( {\iota }_{n}\right)  = {\left( S{q}^{J}\left( {\iota }_{n}\right) \right) }^{{2}^{k}} \) with \( e\left( J\right)  < n \) , so that \( S{q}^{I}\left( {\iota }_{n}\right) \) is already in the algebra generated by the elements \( S{q}^{J}\left( {\iota }_{n}\right) \) with \( e\left( J\right)  < n \) . The situation for odd \( p \) is similar. For an admissible monomial \( {P}^{I} = {\beta }^{{\varepsilon }_{1}}{P}^{{i}_{1}}{\beta }^{{\varepsilon }_{2}}{P}^{{i}_{2}}\cdots \) the definition of excess gives \( 2{i}_{1} = e\left( I\right)  + {\varepsilon }_{2} + 2\left( {p - 1}\right) {i}_{2} + \cdots \) , so if \( e\left( I\right)  > n \) we must have \( {P}^{I}\left( {\iota }_{n}\right)  = 0 \) , and if \( e\left( I\right)  = n \) then either \( {P}^{I}\left( {\iota }_{n}\right) \) is a power \( {\left( {P}^{J}\left( {\iota }_{n}\right) \right) }^{{p}^{k}} \) with \( e\left( J\right)  < n \) , or, if \( {P}^{I} \) begins with \( \beta \) , then \( {P}^{I}\left( {\iota }_{n}\right)  = \beta \left( {\left( {P}^{J}\left( {\iota }_{n}\right) \right) }^{{p}^{k}}\right)  = 0 \) by the formula \( \beta \left( {x}^{m}\right)  = m{x}^{m - 1}\beta \left( x\right) \) , which is valid when \( \left| x\right| \) is even, as we may assume is the case here, otherwise \( {\left( {P}^{J}\left( {\iota }_{n}\right) \right) }^{{p}^{k}} = 0 \) by commutativity of cup product.

There is another set of relations among Steenrod squares equivalent to the Adem relations and somewhat easier to remember:

\mathop{\sum }\limits_{j}\left( \begin{array}{l} k \\  j \end{array}\right) S{q}^{{2n} - k + j - 1}S{q}^{n - j} = 0

When \( k = 0 \) this is simply the relation \( S{q}^{{2n} - 1}S{q}^{n} = 0 \) , and the cases \( k > 0 \) are obtained from this via Pascal’s triangle. For example, from \( S{q}^{7}S{q}^{4} = 0 \) we obtain the following table of relations:

\]

\[
\begin{array}{l} S{q}^{4}S{q}^{4} + S{q}^{5}S{q}^{3} + S{q}^{6}S{q}^{2} + S{q}^{7}S{q}^{1} \end{array}
\]

\[
S{q}^{3}S{q}^{4}\; + \;S{q}^{5}S{q}^{0}\; = 0

These relations are not in simplest possible form. For example, \( S{q}^{5}S{q}^{3} = 0 \) in the fourth row and \( S{q}^{3}S{q}^{2} = 0 \) in the seventh row, instances of \( S{q}^{{2n} - 1}S{q}^{n} = 0 \) . For Steenrod powers there are similar relations \( \mathop{\sum }\limits_{j}\left( \begin{array}{l} k \\  j \end{array}\right) {P}^{{pn} - k + j - 1}{P}^{n - j} = 0 \) derived from the basic relation \( {P}^{{pn} - 1}{P}^{n} = 0 \) . We leave it to the interested reader to show that these relations follow from the Adem relations.

## Constructing the Squares and Powers

Now we turn to the construction of the Steenrod squares and powers, and the proof of their basic properties including the Adem relations. As will be seen, this all hinges on the fact that cohomology is maps into Eilenberg-MacLane spaces. The case \( p = 2 \) is in some ways simpler than the case \( p \) odd, so in the first part of the development we will specialize \( p \) to 2 whenever there is a significant difference between the two cases.

Before giving the construction in detail, let us describe the idea in the case \( p = 2 \) . The cup product square \( {\alpha }^{2} \) of an element \( \alpha  \in  {H}^{n}\left( {X;{\mathbb{Z}}_{2}}\right) \) can be viewed as a composition \( X \rightarrow  X \times  X \rightarrow  K\left( {{\mathbb{Z}}_{2},{2n}}\right) \) , with the first map the diagonal map and the second map representing the cross product \( \alpha  \times  \alpha \) . Since we have \( {\mathbb{Z}}_{2} \) coefficients, cup product and cross product are strictly commutative, so if \( T : X \times  X \rightarrow  X \times  X \) is the map \( T\left( {{x}_{1},{x}_{2}}\right)  = \left( {{x}_{2},{x}_{1}}\right) \) transposing the two factors, then \( {T}^{ * }\left( {\alpha  \times  \alpha }\right)  = \alpha  \times  \alpha \) . Thinking of \( \alpha  \times  \alpha \) as a map \( X \times  X \rightarrow  K\left( {{\mathbb{Z}}_{2},{2n}}\right) \) , this says there is a homotopy \( {f}_{t} \) from \( \alpha  \times  \alpha \) to \( \left( {\alpha  \times  \alpha }\right) T \) . If we follow the homotopy \( {f}_{t} \) by the homotopy \( {f}_{t}T \) , we obtain a homotopy from \( \alpha  \times  \alpha \) to \( \left( {\alpha  \times  \alpha }\right) T \) and then to \( \left( {\alpha  \times  \alpha }\right) {T}^{2} = \alpha  \times  \alpha \) , in other words a loop of maps \( X \times  X \rightarrow  K\left( {{\mathbb{Z}}_{2},{2n}}\right) \) . We can view this loop as a map \( {S}^{1} \times  X \times  X \rightarrow  K\left( {{\mathbb{Z}}_{2},{2n}}\right) \) . As we will see, if the homotopy \( {f}_{t} \) is chosen appropriately, the loop of maps will be null-homotopic, extending to a map \( {D}^{2} \times  X \times  X \rightarrow  K\left( {{\mathbb{Z}}_{2},{2n}}\right) \) . Since we can suspend repeatedly to make any class have dimension of this form, the Adem relations must hold for all cohomology classes.

The complement of the section at infinity is a bundle \( E \rightarrow  {L}^{p} \) with fibers \( {\mathbb{R}}^{p} \) . In general, the one-point compactification of a fiber bundle \( E \) over a compact base space with fibers \( {\mathbb{R}}^{n} \) is called the Thom space \( T\left( E\right) \) of the bundle, and a class in \( {H}^{n}\left( {T\left( E\right) }\right) \) that restricts to a generator of \( {H}^{n} \) of the one-point compactification of each fiber \( {\mathbb{R}}^{n} \) is called a Thom class. Our first task is to construct subbundles \( {E}_{0},{E}_{1},\cdots ,{E}_{m} \) of \( E \) , where \( {E}_{0} \) has fiber \( \mathbb{R} \) and the other \( {E}_{j} \) ’s have fiber \( {\mathbb{R}}^{2} \) , so \( p = {2m} + 1 \) . The bundle \( E \) comes from the linear transformation \( T : {\mathbb{R}}^{p} \rightarrow  {\mathbb{R}}^{p} \) permuting the coordinates cyclically. An easy way to get the decomposition \( {\mathbb{R}}^{p} = {V}_{0} \oplus  {V}_{1} \oplus  \cdots  \oplus  {V}_{m} \) is to regard \( {\mathbb{R}}^{p} \) as a module over the principal ideal domain \( \mathbb{R}\left\lbrack  t\right\rbrack \) by setting \( {tv} = T\left( v\right) \) for \( v \in  {\mathbb{R}}^{p} \) . We may assume \( {K}_{n} \) has a single \( n \) -cell and a single \( \left( {n + 1}\right) \) -cell, attached by a map of degree \( p \) . Let \( \varphi \) and \( \psi \) be the cellular cochains assigning the value 1 to the \( n \) -cell and the \( \left( {n + 1}\right) \) -cell, respectively, so \( {\delta \varphi } = {p\psi } \) . In \( {K}_{n}^{\land p} \) we then have

(*)

\[
\delta \left( {\varphi }^{\otimes p}\right)  = \mathop{\sum }\limits_{i}{\left( -1\right) }^{in}{\varphi }^{\otimes i} \otimes  {\delta \varphi } \otimes  {\varphi }^{\otimes \left( {p - i - 1}\right) } = p\mathop{\sum }\limits_{i}{\left( -1\right) }^{in}{\varphi }^{\otimes i} \otimes  \psi  \otimes  {\varphi }^{\otimes \left( {p - i - 1}\right) }
\]

where the tensor notation means cellular cross product, so for example \( {\varphi }^{\otimes p} \) is the cellular cochain dual to the \( {np} \) -cell \( {e}^{n} \times  \cdots  \times  {e}^{n} \) of \( {K}_{n}^{\land p} \) . The formula \( \left( *\right) \) holds also in \( \Lambda {K}_{n} \) since the latter space has only one \( \left( {{np} + 1}\right) \) -cell not in \( {K}_{n}^{\land p} \) , with cellular boundary zero. Namely, this cell is the product of the 1-cell of \( {L}^{\infty } \) and the \( {np} \) -cell of \( {K}_{n}^{\land p} \) with one end of this product attached to the \( {np} \) -cell by the identity map and the other end by the cyclic permutation \( T \) , which has degree +1 since \( p \) is odd, so these two terms in the boundary of this cell cancel, and there are no other terms since the rest of the attachment of this cell is at the basepoint.

Bockstein homomorphisms can be computed using cellular cochain complexes, so the formula \( \left( *\right) \) says that \( \mathop{\sum }\limits_{i}{\left( -1\right) }^{in}{\varphi }^{\otimes i} \otimes  \psi  \otimes  {\varphi }^{\otimes \left( {p - i - 1}\right) } \) represents \( {\beta \lambda }\left( \iota \right) \) . Via the quotient map \( \Gamma {K}_{n} \rightarrow  \Lambda {K}_{n} \) , the class \( \lambda \left( \iota \right) \) pulls back to a class \( \gamma \left( \iota \right) \) with \( {\beta \gamma }\left( \iota \right) \) also represented by \( \mathop{\sum }\limits_{i}{\left( -1\right) }^{in}{\varphi }^{\otimes i} \otimes  \psi  \otimes  {\varphi }^{\otimes \left( {p - i - 1}\right) } \) . To see what happens when we pull \( {\beta y}\left( \iota \right) \) back to \( \beta {\nabla }^{ * }\left( {\lambda \left( \iota \right) }\right) \) via the inclusion \( {L}^{\infty } \times  {K}_{n} \hookrightarrow  \Gamma {K}_{n} \) , consider the commutative diagram at the right. In the left-hand square the maps \( {\pi }^{ * } \) are induced by the covering space projections \( \pi  : {S}^{\infty } \times  {K}_{n}^{\land p} \rightarrow  \Gamma {K}_{n} \) and \( \pi  : {S}^{\infty } \times  {K}_{n} \rightarrow  {L}^{\infty } \times  {K}_{n} \) arising from the free \( {\mathbb{Z}}_{p} \) actions. The vertical maps are induced by the diagonal inclusion \( {S}^{\infty } \times  K \hookrightarrow  {S}^{\infty } \times  {K}_{n}^{\land p} \) . The maps \( \tau \) are the transfer homomorphisms defined in §3.G. Recall the definition: If \( \pi  : \widetilde{X} \rightarrow  X \) is a \( p \) -sheeted covering space, a chain map \( {C}_{ * }\left( X\right)  \rightarrow  {C}_{ * }\left( \widetilde{X}\right) \) is defined by sending a singular simplex \( \sigma  : {\Delta }^{k} \rightarrow  X \) to the sum of its \( p \) lifts to \( \widetilde{X} \) , and \( \tau \) is the induced map on cohomology. The key property of \( \tau \) is that \( \tau {\pi }^{ * } : {H}^{ * }\left( X\right)  \rightarrow  {H}^{ * }\left( X\right) \) is multiplication by \( p \) , for any choice of coefficient group, since when we project the \( p \) lifts of \( \sigma  : {\Delta }^{k} \rightarrow  X \) back to \( X \) we get \( {p\sigma } \) . When \( X \) is a CW complex and \( \widetilde{X} \) is given the lifted CW structure, then \( \tau \) can also be defined in cellular cohomology by the same procedure.

Let us compute the value of the upper \( \tau \) in the diagram on \( 1 \otimes  \psi  \otimes  {\varphi }^{\otimes \left( {p - 1}\right) } \) where ’ 1 ’ is the cellular cocycle assigning the value 1 to each 0-cell of \( {S}^{\infty } \) . By the definition of \( \tau \) we have \( \tau \left( {1 \otimes  \psi  \otimes  {\varphi }^{\otimes \left( {p - 1}\right) }}\right)  = \mathop{\sum }\limits_{i}{T}^{i}\left( {\psi  \otimes  {\varphi }^{\otimes \left( {p - 1}\right) }}\right) \) where \( T : {K}_{n}^{\land p} \rightarrow  {K}_{n}^{\land p} \) permutes the factors cyclically. It does not matter whether \( T \) moves coordinates one unit leftwards or one unit rightwards since we are summing over all the powers of \( T \) , so let us say \( T \) moves coordinates rightward. Then \( T\left( {\psi  \otimes  {\varphi }^{\otimes \left( {p - 1}\right) }}\right)  = \varphi  \otimes  \psi  \otimes  {\varphi }^{\otimes \left( {p - 2}\right) } \) , with the last \( \varphi \) moved into the first position. This move is achieved by transposing this \( \varphi \) with each of the preceding \( p - {2\varphi } \) ’s and with \( \psi \) . Transposing two \( \varphi \) ’s introduces a sign \( {\left( -1\right) }^{{n}^{2}} \) , and transposing \( \varphi \) with \( \psi \) introduces a sign \( {\left( -1\right) }^{n\left( {n + 1}\right) } =  + 1 \) , by the commutativity property of cross product. Thus the total sign introduced by \( T \) is \( {\left( -1\right) }^{{n}^{2}\left( {p - 2}\right) } \) , which equals \( {\left( -1\right) }^{n} \) since \( p \) is odd. Each successive iterate of \( T \) also introduces a sign of \( {\left( -1\right) }^{n} \) , so \( {T}^{i} \) introduces a sign \( {\left( -1\right) }^{in} \) for \( 0 \leq  i \leq  p - 1 \) . Thus

\[
\tau \left( {1 \otimes  \psi  \otimes  {\varphi }^{\otimes \left( {p - 1}\right) }}\right)  = \mathop{\sum }\limits_{i}{T}^{i}\left( {\psi  \otimes  {\varphi }^{\otimes \left( {p - 1}\right) }}\right)  = \mathop{\sum }\limits_{i}{\left( -1\right) }^{in}{\varphi }^{\otimes i} \otimes  \psi  \otimes  {\varphi }^{\otimes \left( {p - i - 1}\right) }
\]

As observed earlier, this last cocycle represents the class \( {\beta \gamma }\left( \iota \right) \) .

Since \( {\beta \gamma }\left( \iota \right) \) is in the image of the upper \( \tau \) in the diagram, the image of \( {\beta \gamma }\left( \iota \right) \) in \( {H}^{ * }\left( {{L}^{\infty } \times  {K}_{n}}\right) \) , which is \( {\nabla }^{ * }\left( {{\beta \lambda }\left( \iota \right) }\right) \) , is in the image of the lower \( \tau \) since the righthand square commutes. The map \( {\pi }^{ * } \) in the lower row is obviously onto since \( {S}^{\infty } \) is contractible, so \( {\nabla }^{ * }\left( {{\beta \lambda }\left( \iota \right) }\right) \) is in the image of the composition \( \tau {\pi }^{ * } \) across the bottom of the diagram. But this composition is multiplication by \( p \) , which is zero for \( {\mathbb{Z}}_{p} \) coefficients, so \( \beta {\nabla }^{ * }\left( {\lambda \left( \iota \right) }\right)  = {\nabla }^{ * }\left( {{\beta \lambda }\left( \iota \right) }\right)  = 0 \) .

The derivation of the Adem relations now follows the pattern for the case \( p = 2 \) . We had the formula \( {\nabla }_{2}^{ * }{\lambda }_{2}^{ * }\left( \iota \right)  = \mathop{\sum }\limits_{{i, j}}{\omega }_{\left( {p - 1}\right) {pn} - i} \otimes  {\theta }_{i}\left( {{\omega }_{\left( {p - 1}\right) n - j} \otimes  {\theta }_{j}\left( \iota \right) }\right) \) . Since we are letting \( p = {2m} + 1 \) , this can be rewritten as \( \mathop{\sum }\limits_{{i, j}}{\omega }_{{2mpn} - i} \otimes  {\theta }_{i}\left( {{\omega }_{{2mn} - j} \otimes  {\theta }_{j}\left( \iota \right) }\right) \) . The only nonzero \( {\theta }_{i} \) ’s are \( {\theta }_{{2i}\left( {p - 1}\right) } = {\left( -1\right) }^{i}{a}_{n}{P}^{i} \) and \( {\theta }_{{2i}\left( {p - 1}\right)  + 1} =  - \beta {\theta }_{{2i}\left( {p - 1}\right) } \) so we have

\[
\mathop{\sum }\limits_{{i, j}}{\omega }_{{2mpn} - i} \otimes  {\theta }_{i}\left( {{\omega }_{{2mn} - j} \otimes  {\theta }_{j}\left( \iota \right) }\right)  =
\]

\[
\mathop{\sum }\limits_{{i, j}}{\left( -1\right) }^{i + j}{a}_{2mn}{a}_{n}{\omega }_{{2m}\left( {{pn} - {2i}}\right) } \otimes  {P}^{i}\left( {{\omega }_{{2m}\left( {n - {2j}}\right) } \otimes  {P}^{j}\left( \iota \right) }\right)
\]

\[
- \mathop{\sum }\limits_{{i, j}}{\left( -1\right) }^{i + j}{a}_{2mn}{a}_{n}{\omega }_{{2m}\left( {{pn} - {2i}}\right) } \otimes  {P}^{i}\left( {{\omega }_{{2m}\left( {n - {2j}}\right)  - 1} \otimes  \beta {P}^{j}\left( \iota \right) }\right)
\]

\[
- \mathop{\sum }\limits_{{i, j}}{\left( -1\right) }^{i + j}{a}_{2mn}{a}_{n}{\omega }_{{2m}\left( {{pn} - {2i}}\right)  - 1} \otimes  \beta {P}^{i}\left( {{\omega }_{{2m}\left( {n - {2j}}\right) } \otimes  {P}^{j}\left( \iota \right) }\right)
\]

\[
+ \mathop{\sum }\limits_{{i, j}}{\left( -1\right) }^{i + j}{a}_{2mn}{a}_{n}{\omega }_{{2m}\left( {{pn} - {2i}}\right)  - 1} \otimes  \beta {P}^{i}\left( {{\omega }_{{2m}\left( {n - {2j}}\right)  - 1} \otimes  \beta {P}^{j}\left( \iota \right) }\right)
\]

Since \( m \) and \( n \) will be fixed throughout the discussion, we may factor out the nonzero constant \( {a}_{2mn}{a}_{n} \) . Then applying the Cartan formula to expand the \( {P}^{i} \) terms, using also the formulas \( {P}^{k}\left( {\omega }_{2r}\right)  = \left( \begin{array}{l} r \\  k \end{array}\right) {\omega }_{{2r} + {2k}\left( {p - 1}\right) } \) and \( {P}^{k}\left( {\omega }_{{2r} + 1}\right)  = \left( \begin{array}{l} r \\  k \end{array}\right) {\omega }_{{2r} + {2k}\left( {p - 1}\right)  + 1} \) derived earlier in the section, we obtain

\[
\mathop{\sum }\limits_{{i, j, k}}{\left( -1\right) }^{i + j}\left( \begin{matrix} m\left( {n - {2j}}\right) \\  k \end{matrix}\right) {\omega }_{{2m}\left( {{pn} - {2i}}\right) } \otimes  {\omega }_{{2m}\left( {n - {2j} + {2k}}\right) } \otimes  {P}^{i - k}{P}^{j}\left( \iota \right)
\]

\[
- \mathop{\sum }\limits_{{i, j, k}}{\left( -1\right) }^{i + j}\left( \begin{aligned} {m\left( {n - {2j}}\right)  - 1} \\  k \end{aligned}\right) {\omega }_{{2m}\left( {{pn} - {2i}}\right) } \otimes  {\omega }_{{2m}\left( {n - {2j} + {2k}}\right)  - 1} \otimes  {P}^{i - k}\beta {P}^{j}\left( \iota \right)
\]

\[
- \mathop{\sum }\limits_{{i, j, k}}{\left( -1\right) }^{i + j}\left( \begin{matrix} m\left( {n - {2j}}\right) \\  k \end{matrix}\right) {\omega }_{{2m}\left( {{pn} - {2i}}\right)  - 1} \otimes  {\omega }_{{2m}\left( {n - {2j} + {2k}}\right) } \otimes  \beta {P}^{i - k}{P}^{j}\left( \iota \right)
\]

\[
+ \mathop{\sum }\limits_{{i, j, k}}{\left( -1\right) }^{i + j}\left( \begin{aligned} {m\left( {n - {2j}}\right)  - 1} \\  k \end{aligned}\right) {\omega }_{{2m}\left( {{pn} - {2i}}\right)  - 1} \otimes  {\omega }_{{2m}\left( {n - {2j} + {2k}}\right) } \otimes  {P}^{i - k}\beta {P}^{j}\left( \iota \right)
\]

\[
- \mathop{\sum }\limits_{{i, j, k}}{\left( -1\right) }^{i + j}\left( \begin{aligned} {m\left( {n - {2j}}\right)  - 1} \\  k \end{aligned}\right) {\omega }_{{2m}\left( {{pn} - {2i}}\right)  - 1} \otimes  {\omega }_{{2m}\left( {n - {2j} + {2k}}\right)  - 1} \otimes  \beta {P}^{i - k}\beta {P}^{j}\left( \iota \right)
\]

Letting \( \ell  = {mn} + j - k \) , so that \( n - {2j} + {2k} = {pn} - 2\ell \) , the first of these five summations becomes

\[
\mathop{\sum }\limits_{{i, j,\ell }}{\left( -1\right) }^{i + j}\left( \begin{matrix} m\left( {n - {2j}}\right) \\  {mn} + j - \ell  \end{matrix}\right) {\omega }_{{2m}\left( {{pn} - {2i}}\right) } \otimes  {\omega }_{{2m}\left( {{pn} - 2\ell }\right) } \otimes  {P}^{i + \ell  - {mn} - j}{P}^{j}\left( \iota \right)
\]

and similarly for the other four summations.

Now we bring in the symmetry property \( {\varphi }_{rs} = {\left( -1\right) }^{{rs} + {mnp}}{\varphi }_{sr} \) , where, as before, \( {\nabla }_{2}^{ * }{\lambda }_{2}^{ * }\left( \iota \right)  = \mathop{\sum }\limits_{{r, s}}{\omega }_{r} \otimes  {\omega }_{s} \otimes  {\varphi }_{rs} \) . Of the five summations, only the first has both \( \omega \) terms with even subscripts, namely \( r = {2m}\left( {{pn} - {2i}}\right) \) and \( s = {2m}\left( {{pn} - 2\ell }\right) \) , so the coefficient of \( {\omega }_{r} \otimes  {\omega }_{s} \) in this summation must be symmetric with respect to switching \( i \) and \( \ell \) , up to a sign which will be + if we choose \( n \) to be even, as we will do. This gives the relation

(1)

\[
\mathop{\sum }\limits_{j}{\left( -1\right) }^{i + j}\left( \begin{matrix} m\left( {n - {2j}}\right) \\  {mn} + j - \ell  \end{matrix}\right) {P}^{i + \ell  - {mn} - j}{P}^{j}\left( \iota \right)  = \mathop{\sum }\limits_{j}{\left( -1\right) }^{\ell  + j}\left( \begin{matrix} m\left( {n - {2j}}\right) \\  {mn} + j - i \end{matrix}\right) {P}^{i + \ell  - {mn} - j}{P}^{j}\left( \iota \right)
\]

Similarly, the second, third, and fourth summations involve \( \omega \) ’s with subscripts of opposite parity, yielding the relation

\[
{\left( \mathop{\sum }\limits_{j}{\left( -1\right) }^{i + j}\left( \begin{matrix} m\left( {n - {2j}}\right)  - 1 \\  {mn} + j - \ell  \end{matrix}\right) {P}^{i + \ell  - {mn} - j}\beta {P}^{j}\left( \iota \right) \right) }^{-1} =
\]

\[
\mathop{\sum }\limits_{j}{\left( -1\right) }^{\ell  + j}\left( \begin{matrix} m\left( {n - {2j}}\right) \\  {mn} + j - i \end{matrix}\right) \beta {P}^{i + \ell  - {mn} - j}{P}^{j}\left( \iota \right)  - \mathop{\sum }\limits_{j}{\left( -1\right) }^{\ell  + j}\left( \begin{matrix} m\left( {n - {2j}}\right)  - 1 \\  {mn} + j - i \end{matrix}\right) {P}^{i + \ell  - {mn} - j}\beta {P}^{j}\left( \iota \right)
\]

The relations (1) and (2) will yield the two Adem relations, so we will not need to consider the relation arising from the fifth summation.

To get the first Adem relation from (1) we choose \( n \) and \( \ell \) so that the left side of (1) has only one term, namely we take \( n = 2\left( {1 + p + \cdots  + {p}^{r - 1}}\right)  + {2s} \) and \( \ell  = {mn} + s \) for given integers \( r \) and \( s \) . Then

\[
\left( \begin{array}{l} m\left( {n - {2j}}\right) \\  {mn} + j - \ell  \end{array}\right)  = \left( \begin{matrix} {p}^{r} - 1 - \left( {p - 1}\right) \left( {j - s}\right) \\  j - s \end{matrix}\right)
\]

and if \( r \) is large, this binomial coefficient is 1 if \( j = s \) and 0 otherwise since if the rightmost nonzero digit in the \( p \) -adic expansion of the ’denominator’ \( j - s \) is \( x \) , then the corresponding digit of the ’numerator’ \( \left( {p - 1}\right) \left\lbrack  {\left( {1 + p + \cdots  + {p}^{r - 1}}\right)  - \left( {j - s}\right) }\right\rbrack \) is obtained by reducing \( \left( {p - 1}\right) \left( {1 - x}\right) {\;\operatorname{mod}\;p} \) , giving \( x - 1 \) , and \( \left( \begin{matrix} x - 1 \\  x \end{matrix}\right)  = 0 \) . Then (1) becomes

\[
{\left( -1\right) }^{i + s}{P}^{i}{P}^{s}\left( \iota \right)  = \mathop{\sum }\limits_{j}{\left( -1\right) }^{\ell  + j}\left( \begin{matrix} m\left( {n - {2j}}\right) \\  {mn} + j - i \end{matrix}\right) {P}^{i + s - j}{P}^{j}\left( \iota \right)
\]

\[
\text{ or }\;{P}^{i}{P}^{s}\left( \iota \right)  = \mathop{\sum }\limits_{j}{\left( -1\right) }^{i + j}\left( \begin{matrix} m\left( {n - {2j}}\right) \\  {mn} + j - i \end{matrix}\right) {P}^{i + s - j}{P}^{j}\left( \iota \right) \;\text{ since }\;\ell  \equiv  s{\;\operatorname{mod}\;2}
\]

\[
= \mathop{\sum }\limits_{j}{\left( -1\right) }^{i + j}\left( \begin{matrix} m\left( {n - {2j}}\right) \\  i - {pj} \end{matrix}\right) {P}^{i + s - j}{P}^{j}\left( \iota \right) \;\text{ since }\;\left( \begin{array}{l} x \\  y \end{array}\right)  = \left( \begin{matrix} x \\  x - y \end{matrix}\right)
\]

\[
= \mathop{\sum }\limits_{j}{\left( -1\right) }^{i + j}\left( \begin{matrix} {p}^{r} + \left( {p - 1}\right) \left( {s - j}\right)  - 1 \\  i - {pj} \end{matrix}\right) {P}^{i + s - j}{P}^{j}\left( \iota \right)
\]

If \( r \) is large and \( i < {ps} \) , the term \( {p}^{r} \) in the binomial coefficient can be omitted since we may assume \( i \geq  {pj} \) , hence \( j < s \) , so \( - 1 + \left( {p - 1}\right) \left( {s - j}\right)  \geq  0 \) and the \( {p}^{r} \) has no effect on the binomial coefficient if \( r \) is large. This shows the first Adem relation holds for the class \( \iota \) , and the general case follows as in the case \( p = 2 \) .

To get the second Adem relation we choose \( n = 2{p}^{r} + {2s} \) and \( \ell  = {mn} + s \) . Reasoning as before, the left side of (2) then reduces to \( {\left( -1\right) }^{i + s}{P}^{i}\beta {P}^{s}\left( \iota \right) \) and (2) becomes

\[
{P}^{i}\beta {P}^{s}\left( \iota \right)  = \mathop{\sum }\limits_{j}{\left( -1\right) }^{i + j}\left( \begin{aligned} {\left( {p - 1}\right) \left( {{p}^{r} + s - j}\right) } \\  {i - {pj}} \end{aligned}\right) \beta {P}^{i + s - j}{P}^{j}\left( \iota \right)
\]

\[
- \mathop{\sum }\limits_{j}{\left( -1\right) }^{i + j}\left( \begin{matrix} \left( {p - 1}\right) \left( {{p}^{r} + s - j}\right)  - 1 \\  i - {pj} - 1 \end{matrix}\right) {P}^{i + s - j}\beta {P}^{j}\left( \iota \right)
\]

This time the term \( {p}^{r} \) can be omitted if \( r \) is large and \( i \leq  {ps} \) .
