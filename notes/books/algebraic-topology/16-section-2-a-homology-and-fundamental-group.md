# 2.A Homology and Fundamental Group

There is a close connection between \( {H}_{1}\left( X\right) \) and \( {\pi }_{1}\left( X\right) \) , arising from the fact that a map \( f : I \rightarrow  X \) can be viewed as either a path or a singular 1-simplex. If \( f \) is a loop, with \( f\left( 0\right)  = f\left( 1\right) \) , this singular 1-simplex is a cycle since \( \partial f = f\left( 1\right)  - f\left( 0\right) \) .

Theorem 2A.1. By regarding loops as singular 1-cycles, we obtain a homomorphism \( h : {\pi }_{1}\left( {X,{x}_{0}}\right)  \rightarrow  {H}_{1}\left( X\right) \) . If \( X \) is path-connected, then \( h \) is surjective and has kernel the commutator subgroup of \( {\pi }_{1}\left( X\right) \) , so \( h \) induces an isomorphism from the abelian-ization of \( {\pi }_{1}\left( X\right) \) onto \( {H}_{1}\left( X\right) \) .

Proof: Recall the notation \( f \simeq  g \) for the relation of homotopy, fixing endpoints, between paths \( f \) and \( g \) . Regarding \( f \) and \( g \) as chains, the notation \( f \sim  g \) will mean that \( f \) is homologous to \( g \) , that is, \( f - g \) is the boundary of some 2-chain. Here are some facts about this relation.

(i) If \( f \) is a constant path, then \( f \sim  0 \) . Namely, \( f \) is a cycle since it is a loop, and since \( {H}_{1}\left( \text{ point }\right)  = 0, f \) must then be a boundary. Explicitly, \( f \) is the boundary of the constant singular 2-simplex \( \sigma \) having the same image as \( f \) since

\[
\partial \sigma  = \sigma \left| {\left\lbrack  {{v}_{1},{v}_{2}}\right\rbrack   - \sigma }\right| \left\lbrack  {{v}_{0},{v}_{2}}\right\rbrack   + \sigma  \mid  \left\lbrack  {{v}_{0},{v}_{1}}\right\rbrack   = f - f + f = f
\]

(ii) If \( f \simeq  g \) then \( f \sim  g \) . To see this, consider a homotopy \( F : I \times  I \rightarrow  X \) from \( f \) to \( g \) . This yields a pair of singular 2-simplices \( {\sigma }_{1} \) and \( {\sigma }_{2} \) in \( X \) by subdividing the square \( I \times  I \) into two triangles \( \left\lbrack  {{v}_{0},{v}_{1},{v}_{3}}\right\rbrack \) and \( \left\lbrack  {{v}_{0},{v}_{2},{v}_{3}}\right\rbrack \) as shown in the figure. When one computes \( \partial \left( {{\sigma }_{1} - {\sigma }_{2}}\right) \) , the two restrictions of \( F \) to the diagonal of the square cancel, leaving \( f - g \) together with two constant singular 1-simplices from the left and right edges of the square. By (i) these are boundaries, so \( f - g \) is also a boundary.

(iii) \( f \cdot  g \sim  f + g \) , where \( f \cdot  g \) denotes the product of the paths \( f \) and \( g \) . For if \( \sigma  : {\Delta }^{2} \rightarrow  X \) is the composition of orthogonal projection of \( {\Delta }^{2} = \left\lbrack  {{v}_{0},{v}_{1},{v}_{2}}\right\rbrack \) onto the edge \( \left\lbrack  {{v}_{0},{v}_{2}}\right\rbrack \) followed by \( f \cdot  g : \left\lbrack  {{v}_{0},{v}_{2}}\right\rbrack   \rightarrow  X \) , then \( \partial \sigma  = g - f \cdot  g + f \) .

(iv) \( \bar{f} \sim   - f \) , where \( \bar{f} \) is the inverse path of \( f \) . This follows from the preceding three observations, which give \( f + \bar{f} \sim  f \cdot  \bar{f} \sim  0 \) .

Applying (ii) and (iii) to loops, it follows that we have a well-defined homomorphism \( h : {\pi }_{1}\left( {X,{x}_{0}}\right)  \rightarrow  {H}_{1}\left( X\right) \) sending the homotopy class of a loop \( f \) to the homology class of the 1-cycle \( f \) .

To show \( h \) is surjective when \( X \) is path-connected, let \( \mathop{\sum }\limits_{i}{n}_{i}{\sigma }_{i} \) be a 1-cycle representing a given element of \( {H}_{1}\left( X\right) \) . After relabeling the \( {\sigma }_{i} \) ’s we may assume each \( {n}_{i} \) is \( \pm  1 \) . By (iv) we may in fact take each \( {n}_{i} \) to be +1, so our 1-cycle is \( \mathop{\sum }\limits_{i}{\sigma }_{i} \) . If some \( {\sigma }_{i} \) is not a loop, then the fact that \( \partial \left( {\mathop{\sum }\limits_{i}{\sigma }_{i}}\right)  = 0 \) means there must be another \( {\sigma }_{j} \) such that the composed path \( {\sigma }_{i} \cdot  {\sigma }_{j} \) is defined. By (iii) we may then combine the terms \( {\sigma }_{i} \) and \( {\sigma }_{j} \) into a single term \( {\sigma }_{i} \cdot  {\sigma }_{j} \) . Iterating this, we reduce to the case that each \( {\sigma }_{i} \) is a loop. Since \( X \) is path-connected, we may choose a path \( {\gamma }_{i} \) from \( {x}_{0} \) to the basepoint of \( {\sigma }_{i} \) . We have \( {\gamma }_{i} \cdot  {\sigma }_{i} \cdot  {\overline{\gamma }}_{i} \sim  {\sigma }_{i} \) by (iii) and (iv), so we may assume all \( {\sigma }_{i} \) ’s are loops at \( {x}_{0} \) . Then we can combine all the \( {\sigma }_{i} \) ’s into a single \( \sigma \) by (iii). This says the given element of \( {H}_{1}\left( X\right) \) is in the image of \( h \) .

The commutator subgroup of \( {\pi }_{1}\left( X\right) \) is contained in the kernel of \( h \) since \( {H}_{1}\left( X\right) \) is abelian. To obtain the reverse inclusion we will show that every class \( \left\lbrack  f\right\rbrack \) in the kernel of \( h \) is trivial in the abelianization \( {\pi }_{1}{\left( X\right) }_{ab} \) of \( {\pi }_{1}\left( X\right) \) .

If an element \( \left\lbrack  f\right\rbrack   \in  {\pi }_{1}\left( X\right) \) is in the kernel of \( h \) , then \( f \) , as a 1-cycle, is the boundary of a 2-chain \( \mathop{\sum }\limits_{i}{n}_{i}{\sigma }_{i} \) . Again we may assume each \( {n}_{i} \) is \( \pm  1 \) . As in the discussion preceding Proposition 2.6, we can associate to the chain \( \mathop{\sum }\limits_{i}{n}_{i}{\sigma }_{i} \) a 2-dimensional \( \Delta \) -complex \( K \) by taking a 2-simplex \( {\Delta }_{i}^{2} \) for each \( {\sigma }_{i} \) and identifying certain pairs of edges of these 2-simplices. Namely, if we apply the usual boundary formula to write \( \partial {\sigma }_{i} = {\tau }_{i0} - {\tau }_{i1} + {\tau }_{i2} \) for singular 1-simplices \( {\tau }_{ij} \) , then the formula

\[
f = \partial \left( {\mathop{\sum }\limits_{i}{n}_{i}{\sigma }_{i}}\right)  = \mathop{\sum }\limits_{i}{n}_{i}\partial {\sigma }_{i} = \mathop{\sum }\limits_{{i, j}}{\left( -1\right) }^{j}{n}_{i}{\tau }_{ij}
\]

implies that we can group all but one of the \( {\tau }_{ij} \) ’s into pairs for which the two coefficients \( {\left( -1\right) }^{j}{n}_{i} \) in each pair are +1 and -1 . The one remaining \( {\tau }_{ij} \) is equal to \( f \) . We then identify edges of the \( {\Delta }_{j}^{2} \) ’s corresponding to the paired \( {\tau }_{ij} \) ’s, preserving orientations of these edges so that we obtain a \( \Delta \) -complex \( K \) .

The maps \( {\sigma }_{i} \) fit together to give a map \( \sigma  : K \rightarrow  X \) . We can deform \( \sigma \) , staying fixed on the edge corresponding to \( f \) , so that each vertex maps to the basepoint \( {x}_{0} \) , in the following way. Paths from the images of these vertices to \( {x}_{0} \) define such a homotopy on the union of the 0-skeleton of \( K \) with the edge corresponding to \( f \) , and then we can appeal to the homotopy extension property in Proposition 0.16 to extend this homotopy to all of \( K \) . Alternatively, it is not hard to construct such an extension by hand. Restricting the new \( \sigma \) to the simplices \( {\Delta }_{i}^{2} \) , we obtain a new chain \( \mathop{\sum }\limits_{i}{n}_{i}{\sigma }_{i} \) with boundary equal to \( f \) and with all \( {\tau }_{ij} \) ’s loops at \( {x}_{0} \) .

Using additive notation in the abelian group \( {\pi }_{1}{\left( X\right) }_{ab} \) , we have the formula \( \left\lbrack  f\right\rbrack   = \; \mathop{\sum }\limits_{{i, j}}{\left( -1\right) }^{j}{n}_{i}\left\lbrack  {\tau }_{ij}\right\rbrack \) because of the canceling pairs of \( {\tau }_{ij} \) ’s. We can rewrite the summation \( \mathop{\sum }\limits_{{i, j}}{\left( -1\right) }^{j}{n}_{i}\left\lbrack  {\tau }_{ij}\right\rbrack \) as \( \mathop{\sum }\limits_{i}{n}_{i}\left\lbrack  {\partial {\sigma }_{i}}\right\rbrack \) where \( \left\lbrack  {\partial {\sigma }_{i}}\right\rbrack   = \left\lbrack  {\tau }_{i0}\right\rbrack   - \left\lbrack  {\tau }_{i1}\right\rbrack   + \left\lbrack  {\tau }_{i2}\right\rbrack \) . Since \( {\sigma }_{i} \) gives a nullhomotopy of the composed loop \( {\tau }_{i0} - {\tau }_{i1} + {\tau }_{i2} \) , we conclude that \( \left\lbrack  f\right\rbrack   = 0 \) in \( {\pi }_{1}{\left( X\right) }_{ab} \) .

The end of this proof can be illuminated by looking more closely at the geometry. The complex \( K \) is in fact a compact surface with boundary consisting of a single circle formed by the edge corresponding to \( f \) . This is because any pattern of identifications of pairs of edges of a finite collection of disjoint 2-simplices produces a compact surface with boundary. We leave it as an exercise for the reader to check that the algebraic formula \( f = \partial \left( {\mathop{\sum }\limits_{i}{n}_{i}{\sigma }_{i}}\right) \) with each \( {n}_{i} =  \pm  1 \) implies that \( K \) is an orientable surface. The component of \( K \) containing the boundary circle is a standard closed orientable surface of some genus \( g \) with an open disk removed, by the basic structure theorem for compact orientable surfaces. Giving this surface the cell structure indicated in the figure, it then becomes obvious that \( f \) is homotopic to a product of \( g \) commutators in \( {\pi }_{1}\left( X\right) \) .

The map \( h : {\pi }_{1}\left( {X,{x}_{0}}\right)  \rightarrow  {H}_{1}\left( X\right) \) can also be defined by \( h\left( \left\lbrack  f\right\rbrack  \right)  = {f}_{ * }\left( \alpha \right) \) where \( f : {S}^{1} \rightarrow  X \) represents a given element of \( {\pi }_{1}\left( {X,{x}_{0}}\right) ,{f}_{ * } \) is the induced map on \( {H}_{1} \) , and \( \alpha \) is the generator of \( {H}_{1}\left( {S}^{1}\right)  \approx  \mathbb{Z} \) represented by the standard map \( \sigma  : I \rightarrow  {S}^{1},\sigma \left( s\right)  = \; {e}^{2\pi is} \) . This is because both \( \left\lbrack  f\right\rbrack   \in  {\pi }_{1}\left( {X,{x}_{0}}\right) \) and \( {f}_{ * }\left( \alpha \right)  \in  {H}_{1}\left( X\right) \) are represented by the loop \( {f\sigma } : I \rightarrow  X \) . A consequence of this definition is that \( h\left( \left\lbrack  f\right\rbrack  \right)  = h\left( \left\lbrack  g\right\rbrack  \right) \) if \( f \) and \( g \) are homotopic maps \( {S}^{1} \rightarrow  X \) , since \( {f}_{ * } = {g}_{ * } \) by Theorem 2.10.
