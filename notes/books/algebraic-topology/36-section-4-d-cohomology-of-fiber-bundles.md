# 4.D Cohomology of Fiber Bundles

While the homotopy groups of the three spaces in a fiber bundle fit into a long exact sequence, the relation between their homology or cohomology groups is much more complicated. The Künneth formula shows that there are some subtleties even for a product bundle, and for general bundles the machinery of spectral sequences, developed in [SSAT], is required. In this section we will describe a few special sorts of fiber bundles where more elementary techniques suffice. As applications we calculate the cohomology rings of some important spaces closely related to Lie groups. In particular we find a number of spaces with exterior and polynomial cohomology rings.

## The Leray-Hirsch Theorem

This theorem will be the basis for all the other results in this section. It gives hypotheses sufficient to guarantee that a fiber bundle has cohomology very much like that of a product bundle.

Theorem 4D.1. Let \( F \rightarrow  E\overset{p}{ \rightarrow  }B \) be a fiber bundle such that, for some commutative coefficient ring \( R \) :

(a) \( {H}^{n}\left( {F;R}\right) \) is a finitely generated free \( R \) -module for each \( n \) .

(b) There exist classes \( {c}_{j} \in  {H}^{{k}_{j}}\left( {E;R}\right) \) whose restrictions \( {i}^{ * }\left( {c}_{j}\right) \) form a basis for \( {H}^{ * }\left( {F;R}\right) \) in each fiber \( F \) , where \( i : F \rightarrow  E \) is the inclusion.

Then the map \( \Phi  : {H}^{ * }\left( {B;R}\right) { \otimes  }_{R}{H}^{ * }\left( {F;R}\right)  \rightarrow  {H}^{ * }\left( {E;R}\right) ,\mathop{\sum }\limits_{{ij}}{b}_{i} \otimes  {i}^{ * }\left( {c}_{j}\right)  \mapsto  \mathop{\sum }\limits_{{ij}}{p}^{ * }\left( {b}_{i}\right)  \smile  {c}_{j} \) , is an isomorphism of \( R \) -modules.

The conclusion can be restated as saying that \( {H}^{ * }\left( {E;R}\right) \) is a free \( {H}^{ * }\left( {B;R}\right) \) -module with basis \( \left\{  {c}_{j}\right\} \) , where we view \( {H}^{ * }\left( {E;R}\right) \) as a module over the ring \( {H}^{ * }\left( {B;R}\right) \) by defining scalar multiplication by \( {bc} = {p}^{ * }\left( b\right)  \smile  c \) for \( b \in  {H}^{ * }\left( {B;R}\right) \) and \( c \in  {H}^{ * }\left( {E;R}\right) \) .

In the case of a product \( E = B \times  F \) with \( {H}^{ * }\left( {F;R}\right) \) free over \( R \) , we can pull back a basis for \( {H}^{ * }\left( {F;R}\right) \) via the projection \( E \rightarrow  F \) to obtain the classes \( {c}_{j} \) . Thus the Leray-Hirsch theorem generalizes the version of the Künneth formula involving cup products, Theorem 3.15, at least as far as the additive structure and the module structure over \( {H}^{ * }\left( {B;R}\right) \) are concerned. However, the Leray-Hirsch theorem does not assert that the isomorphism \( {H}^{ * }\left( {E;R}\right)  \approx  {H}^{ * }\left( {B;R}\right) { \otimes  }_{R}{H}^{ * }\left( {F;R}\right) \) is a ring isomorphism, and in fact this need not be true, for example for the Klein bottle viewed as a bundle with fiber and base \( {S}^{1} \) , where the Leray-Hirsch theorem applies with \( {\mathbb{Z}}_{2} \) coefficients.

An example of a bundle where the classes \( {c}_{j} \) do not exist is the Hopf bundle \( {S}^{1} \rightarrow  {S}^{3} \rightarrow  {S}^{2} \) , since \( {H}^{ * }\left( {S}^{3}\right)  \text{ ≄ } {H}^{ * }\left( {S}^{2}\right)  \otimes  {H}^{ * }\left( {S}^{1}\right) \) .

Proof: We first prove the result for finite-dimensional CW complexes \( B \) by induction on their dimension. The case that \( B \) is 0-dimensional is trivial. For the induction step, suppose \( B \) has dimension \( n \) , and let \( {B}^{\prime } \subset  B \) be the subspace obtained by deleting a point \( {x}_{\alpha } \) from the interior of each \( n \) -cell \( {e}_{\alpha }^{n} \) of \( B \) . Let \( {E}^{\prime } = {p}^{-1}\left( {B}^{\prime }\right) \) . Then we have a commutative diagram, with coefficients in \( R \) understood:

The space \( {B}^{\prime } \) deformation retracts onto the skeleton \( {B}^{n - 1} \) , and the following lemma implies that the inclusion \( {p}^{-1}\left( {B}^{n - 1}\right)  \hookrightarrow  {E}^{\prime } \) is a weak homotopy equivalence, hence induces an isomorphism on all cohomology groups:

Lemma 4D.2. Given a fiber bundle \( p : E \rightarrow  B \) and a subspace \( A \subset  B \) such that \( \left( {B, A}\right) \) is \( k \) -connected, then \( \left( {E,{p}^{-1}\left( A\right) }\right) \) is also \( k \) -connected.

Proof: For a map \( g : \left( {{D}^{i},\partial {D}^{i}}\right)  \rightarrow  \left( {E,{p}^{-1}\left( A\right) }\right) \) with \( i \leq  k \) , there is by hypothesis a homotopy \( {f}_{t} : \left( {{D}^{i},\partial {D}^{i}}\right)  \rightarrow  \left( {B, A}\right) \) of \( {f}_{0} = {pg} \) to a map \( {f}_{1} \) with image in \( A \) . The homotopy lifting property then gives a homotopy \( {g}_{t} : \left( {{D}^{i},\partial {D}^{i}}\right)  \rightarrow  \left( {E,{p}^{-1}\left( A\right) }\right) \) of \( g \) to a map with image in \( {p}^{-1}\left( A\right) \) .

The theorem for finite-dimensional \( B \) will now follow by induction on \( n \) and the five-lemma once we show that the left-hand \( \Phi \) in the diagram is an isomorphism.

By the fiber bundle property there are open disk neighborhoods \( {U}_{\alpha } \subset  {e}_{\alpha }^{n} \) of the points \( {x}_{\alpha } \) such that the bundle is a product over each \( {U}_{\alpha } \) . Let \( U = \mathop{\bigcup }\limits_{\alpha }{U}_{\alpha } \) and let \( {U}^{\prime } = U \cap  {B}^{\prime } \) . By excision we have \( {H}^{ * }\left( {B,{B}^{\prime }}\right)  \approx  {H}^{ * }\left( {U,{U}^{\prime }}\right) \) , and \( {H}^{ * }\left( {E,{E}^{\prime }}\right)  \approx \; {H}^{ * }\left( {{p}^{-1}\left( U\right) ,{p}^{-1}\left( {U}^{\prime }\right) }\right) \) . This gives a reduction to the problem of showing that the map \( \Phi  : {H}^{ * }\left( {U,{U}^{\prime }}\right) { \otimes  }_{R}{H}^{ * }\left( F\right)  \rightarrow  {H}^{ * }\left( {U \times  F,{U}^{\prime } \times  F}\right) \) is an isomorphism. For this we can either appeal to the relative Künneth formula in Theorem 3.18 or we can argue again by induction, applying the five-lemma to the diagram with \( \left( {B,{B}^{\prime }}\right) \) replaced by \( \left( {U,{U}^{\prime }}\right) \) , induction implying that the theorem holds for \( U \) and \( {U}^{\prime } \) since they deformation retract onto complexes of dimensions 0 and \( n - 1 \) , respectively, and by the lemma we can restrict to the bundles over these complexes.

Next there is the case that \( B \) is an infinite-dimensional CW complex. Since \( \left( {B,{B}^{n}}\right) \) is \( n \) -connected, the lemma implies that the same is true of \( \left( {E,{p}^{-1}\left( {B}^{n}\right) }\right) \) . Hence in the commutative diagram at the right the horizontal maps are isomorphisms below dimension \( n \) . Then the fact that the righthand \( \Phi \) is an isomorphism, as we have already shown, implies that the left-hand \( \Phi \) is an isomorphism below dimension \( n \) . Since \( n \) is arbitrary, this gives the theorem for all CW complexes \( B \) .

\( p : E \rightarrow  B \) and a map \( f : A \rightarrow  B \) , let \( {f}^{ * }\left( E\right)  = \{ \left( {a, e}\right)  \in  A \times  E \mid  f\left( a\right)  = p\left( e\right) \} \) , so there is a commutative diagram as at the right, where the two maps from \( {f}^{ * }\left( E\right) \) are \( \left( {a, e}\right)  \mapsto  a \) and \( \left( {a, e}\right)  \mapsto  e \) . It is a simple exercise to verify that the projection \( {f}^{ * }\left( E\right)  \rightarrow  A \) is a fiber bundle with the same fiber as \( E \rightarrow  B \) , since a local trivialization of \( E \rightarrow  B \) over \( U \subset  B \) gives rise to a local trivialization of \( {f}^{ * }\left( E\right)  \rightarrow  A \) over \( {f}^{-1}\left( U\right) \) .

Corollary 4D.3. (a) \( {H}^{ * }\left( {U\left( n\right) ;\mathbb{Z}}\right)  \approx  {\Lambda }_{\mathbb{Z}}\left\lbrack  {{x}_{1},{x}_{3},\cdots ,{x}_{{2n} - 1}}\right\rbrack \) , the exterior algebra on generators \( {x}_{i} \) of odd dimension \( i \) .

(b) \( {H}^{ * }\left( {{SU}\left( n\right) ;\mathbb{Z}}\right)  \approx  {\Lambda }_{\mathbb{Z}}\left\lbrack  {{x}_{3},{x}_{5},\cdots ,{x}_{{2n} - 1}}\right\rbrack \) .

The products of distinct \( {x}_{i} \) ’s form an additive basis for \( {H}^{ * }\left( {U\left( {n - 1}\right) ;\mathbb{Z}}\right)  \approx  {\Lambda }_{\mathbb{Z}}\left\lbrack  {{x}_{1},\cdots ,{x}_{{2n} - 3}}\right\rbrack \) , and these products are restrictions of the corresponding products of \( {c}_{i} \) ’s, so the Leray-Hirsch theorem applies to give an additive basis for \( {H}^{ * }\left( {U\left( n\right) ;\mathbb{Z}}\right) \) consisting of all products of distinct elements \( {x}_{1} = {c}_{1},\cdots ,{x}_{{2n} - 3} = {c}_{{2n} - 3} \) and a new generator \( {x}_{{2n} - 1} \) coming from \( {H}^{{2n} - 1}\left( {{S}^{{2n} - 1};\mathbb{Z}}\right) \) . ## Cohomology of Grassmannians

Here is an important application of the Leray-Hirsch theorem, generalizing the calculation of the cohomology rings of projective spaces:

Theorem 4D.4. If \( {G}_{n}\left( {\mathbb{C}}^{\infty }\right) \) is the Grassmann manifold of \( n \) -dimensional vector subspaces of \( {\mathbb{C}}^{\infty } \) , then \( {H}^{ * }\left( {{G}_{n}\left( {\mathbb{C}}^{\infty }\right) ;\mathbb{Z}}\right) \) is a polynomial ring \( \mathbb{Z}\left\lbrack  {{c}_{1},\cdots ,{c}_{n}}\right\rbrack \) on generators \( {c}_{i} \) of dimension 2i. Similarly, \( {H}^{ * }\left( {{G}_{n}\left( {\mathbb{R}}^{\infty }\right) ;{\mathbb{Z}}_{2}}\right) \) is a polynomial ring \( {\mathbb{Z}}_{2}\left\lbrack  {{w}_{1},\cdots ,{w}_{n}}\right\rbrack \) on generators \( {w}_{i} \) of dimension \( i \) , and \( {H}^{ * }\left( {{G}_{n}\left( {\mathbb{H}}^{\infty }\right) ;\mathbb{Z}}\right)  \approx  \mathbb{Z}\left\lbrack  {{q}_{1},\cdots ,{q}_{n}}\right\rbrack \) with \( {q}_{i} \) of dimension \( {4i} \) .

The plan of the proof is to apply the Leray-Hirsch theorem to a fiber bundle \( F \rightarrow  E\overset{p}{ \rightarrow  }{G}_{n}\left( {\mathbb{C}}^{\infty }\right) \) where \( E \) has the same cohomology ring as the product of \( n \) copies of \( {\mathbb{{CP}}}^{\infty } \) , a polynomial ring \( \mathbb{Z}\left\lbrack  {{x}_{1},\cdots ,{x}_{n}}\right\rbrack \) with each \( {x}_{i} \) 2-dimensional. The induced map \( {p}^{ * } : {H}^{ * }\left( {{G}_{n}\left( {\mathbb{C}}^{\infty }\right) ;\mathbb{Z}}\right)  \rightarrow  {H}^{ * }\left( {E;\mathbb{Z}}\right) \) will be injective, and we will show that its image consists of the symmetric polynomials in \( \mathbb{Z}\left\lbrack  {{x}_{1},\cdots ,{x}_{n}}\right\rbrack \) , the polynomials invariant under permutations of the variables \( {x}_{i} \) . It is a classical theorem in algebra that the symmetric polynomials themselves form a polynomial ring \( \mathbb{Z}\left\lbrack  {{\sigma }_{1},\cdots ,{\sigma }_{n}}\right\rbrack \) where \( {\sigma }_{i} \) is a certain symmetric polynomial of degree \( i \) , namely the sum of all products of \( i \) distinct \( {x}_{j} \) ’s. This gives the result for \( {G}_{n}\left( {\mathbb{C}}^{\infty }\right) \) , and the same argument will also apply in the real and quaternionic cases.

Proof: Define an \( n \) -flag in \( {\mathbb{C}}^{k} \) to be an ordered \( n \) -tuple of orthogonal 1-dimensional vector subspaces of \( {\mathbb{C}}^{k} \) . Equivalently, an \( n \) -flag could be defined as a chain of vector subspaces \( {V}_{1} \subset  \cdots  \subset  {V}_{n} \) of \( {\mathbb{C}}^{k} \) where \( {V}_{i} \) has dimension \( i \) . Why either of these objects should be called a 'flag' is not exactly clear, but that is the traditional name. The set of all \( n \) -flags in \( {\mathbb{C}}^{k} \) forms a subspace \( {F}_{n}\left( {\mathbb{C}}^{k}\right) \) of the product of \( n \) copies of \( {\mathbb{{CP}}}^{k - 1} \) . There is a natural fiber bundle

\[
{F}_{n}\left( {\mathbb{C}}^{n}\right)  \rightarrow  {F}_{n}\left( {\mathbb{C}}^{k}\right) \overset{p}{ \rightarrow  }{G}_{n}\left( {\mathbb{C}}^{k}\right)
\]

where \( p \) sends an \( n \) -tuple of orthogonal lines to the \( n \) -plane it spans. The local triviality property can be verified just as was done for the analogous Stiefel bundle \( {V}_{n}\left( {\mathbb{C}}^{n}\right)  \rightarrow  {V}_{n}\left( {\mathbb{C}}^{k}\right)  \rightarrow  {G}_{n}\left( {\mathbb{C}}^{k}\right) \) in Example 4.53. The case \( k = \infty \) is covered by the same argument, and this case will be the bundle \( F \rightarrow  E \rightarrow  {G}_{n}\left( {\mathbb{C}}^{\infty }\right) \) alluded to in the paragraph preceding the proof.

The first step in the proof is to show that \( {H}^{ * }\left( {{F}_{n}\left( {\mathbb{C}}^{\infty }\right) ;\mathbb{Z}}\right)  \approx  \mathbb{Z}\left\lbrack  {{x}_{1},\cdots ,{x}_{n}}\right\rbrack \) where \( {x}_{i} \) is the pullback of a generator of \( {H}^{2}\left( {{\mathbb{{CP}}}^{\infty };\mathbb{Z}}\right) \) under the map \( {F}_{n}\left( {\mathbb{C}}^{\infty }\right)  \rightarrow  {\mathbb{{CP}}}^{\infty } \) projecting an \( n \) -flag onto its \( {i}^{th} \) line. This can be seen by considering the fiber bundle

\[
{\mathbb{{CP}}}^{\infty } \rightarrow  {F}_{n}\left( {\mathbb{C}}^{\infty }\right) \overset{p}{ \rightarrow  }{F}_{n - 1}\left( {\mathbb{C}}^{\infty }\right)
\]

where \( p \) projects an \( n \) -flag onto the \( \left( {n - 1}\right) \) -flag obtained by ignoring its last line. The local triviality property can be verified by the argument in Example 4.54. The Leray-Hirsch theorem applies since the powers of \( {x}_{n} \) restrict to a basis for \( {H}^{ * }\left( {{\mathbb{{CP}}}^{\infty };\mathbb{Z}}\right) \) in the fibers \( {\mathbb{{CP}}}^{\infty } \) , each fiber being the space of lines in a vector subspace \( {\mathbb{C}}^{\infty } \) of the standard \( {\mathbb{C}}^{\infty } \) . The elements \( {x}_{i} \) for \( i < n \) are the pullbacks via \( p \) of elements of \( {H}^{ * }\left( {{F}_{n - 1}\left( {\mathbb{C}}^{\infty }\right) ;\mathbb{Z}}\right) \) defined in the same way. By induction \( {H}^{ * }\left( {{F}_{n - 1}\left( {\mathbb{C}}^{\infty }\right) ;\mathbb{Z}}\right) \) is a polynomial ring on these elements. From the Leray-Hirsch theorem we conclude that the products of powers of the \( {x}_{i} \) ’s for \( 1 \leq  i \leq  n \) form an additive basis for \( {H}^{ * }\left( {{F}_{n}\left( {\mathbb{C}}^{\infty }\right) ;\mathbb{Z}}\right) \) , hence this ring is the polynomial ring on the \( {x}_{i} \) ’s.

There is a corresponding result for \( {F}_{n}\left( {\mathbb{C}}^{k}\right) \) , that \( {H}^{ * }\left( {{F}_{n}\left( {\mathbb{C}}^{k}\right) ;\mathbb{Z}}\right) \) is free with basis the monomials \( {x}_{1}^{{i}_{1}}\cdots {x}_{n}^{{i}_{n}} \) with \( {i}_{j} \leq  k - j \) for each \( j \) . This is proved in exactly the same way, using induction on \( n \) and the fiber bundle \( {\mathbb{{CP}}}^{k - n} \rightarrow  {F}_{n}\left( {\mathbb{C}}^{k}\right)  \rightarrow  {F}_{n - 1}\left( {\mathbb{C}}^{k}\right) \) . Thus the cohomology groups of \( {F}_{n}\left( {\mathbb{C}}^{k}\right) \) are isomorphic to those of \( {\mathbb{{CP}}}^{k - 1} \times  \cdots  \times  {\mathbb{{CP}}}^{k - n} \) .

After these preliminaries we can start the main argument, using the fiber bundle \( {F}_{n}\left( {\mathbb{C}}^{n}\right)  \rightarrow  {F}_{n}\left( {\mathbb{C}}^{\infty }\right) \overset{p}{ \rightarrow  }{G}_{n}\left( {\mathbb{C}}^{\infty }\right) \) . The preceding calculations show that the Leray-Hirsch theorem applies, so \( {H}^{ * }\left( {{F}_{n}\left( {\mathbb{C}}^{\infty }\right) ;\mathbb{Z}}\right) \) is a free module over \( {H}^{ * }\left( {{G}_{n}\left( {\mathbb{C}}^{\infty }\right) ;\mathbb{Z}}\right) \) with basis the monomials \( {x}_{1}^{{i}_{1}}\cdots {x}_{n}^{{i}_{n}} \) with \( {i}_{j} \leq  n - j \) for each \( j \) . In particular, since 1 is among the basis elements, the homomorphism \( {p}^{ * } \) is injective and its image is a direct summand of \( {H}^{ * }\left( {{F}_{n}\left( {\mathbb{C}}^{\infty }\right) ;\mathbb{Z}}\right) \) . It remains to show that the image of \( {p}^{ * } \) is exactly the symmetric polynomials.

To show that the image of \( {p}^{ * } \) is contained in the symmetric polynomials, consider a map \( \pi  : {F}_{n}\left( {\mathbb{C}}^{\infty }\right)  \rightarrow  {F}_{n}\left( {\mathbb{C}}^{\infty }\right) \) permuting the lines in each \( n \) -flag according to a given permutation of the numbers \( 1,\cdots , n \) . The induced map \( {\pi }^{ * } \) on \( {H}^{ * }\left( {{F}_{n}\left( {\mathbb{C}}^{\infty }\right) ;\mathbb{Z}}\right)  \approx \; \mathbb{Z}\left\lbrack  {{x}_{1},\cdots ,{x}_{n}}\right\rbrack \) is the corresponding permutation of the variables \( {x}_{i} \) . Since permuting the lines in an \( n \) -flag has no effect on the \( n \) -plane they span, we have \( {p\pi } = p \) , hence \( {\pi }^{ * }{p}^{ * } = {p}^{ * } \) , which says that polynomials in the image of \( {p}^{ * } \) are invariant under permutations of the variables.

As remarked earlier, the symmetric polynomials in \( \mathbb{Z}\left\lbrack  {{x}_{1},\cdots ,{x}_{n}}\right\rbrack \) form a polynomial ring \( \mathbb{Z}\left\lbrack  {{\sigma }_{1},\cdots ,{\sigma }_{n}}\right\rbrack \) where \( {\sigma }_{i} \) has degree \( i \) . We have shown that the image of \( {p}^{ * } \) is a direct summand, so to show that \( {p}^{ * } \) maps onto the symmetric polynomials it will suffice to show that the graded rings \( {H}^{ * }\left( {{G}_{n}\left( {\mathbb{C}}^{\infty }\right) ;\mathbb{Z}}\right) \) and \( \mathbb{Z}\left\lbrack  {{\sigma }_{1},\cdots ,{\sigma }_{n}}\right\rbrack \) have the same rank in each dimension, where the rank of a finitely generated free abelian group is the number of \( \mathbb{Z} \) summands.

For a graded free \( \mathbb{Z} \) -module \( A = {\bigoplus }_{i}{A}_{i} \) , define its Poincaré series to be the formal power series \( {p}_{A}\left( t\right)  = \mathop{\sum }\limits_{i}{a}_{i}{t}^{i} \) where \( {a}_{i} \) is the rank of \( {A}_{i} \) , which we assume to be finite for all \( i \) . The basic formula we need is that \( {p}_{A \otimes  B}\left( t\right)  = {p}_{A}\left( t\right) {p}_{B}\left( t\right) \) , which is immediate from the definition of the graded tensor product.

In the case at hand all nonzero cohomology is in even dimensions, so let us simplify notation by taking \( {A}_{i} \) to be the \( {2i} \) -dimensional cohomology of the space in question. Since the Poincaré series of \( \mathbb{Z}\left\lbrack  x\right\rbrack \) is \( \mathop{\sum }\limits_{i}{t}^{i} = {\left( 1 - t\right) }^{-1} \) , the Poincaré series of \( {H}^{ * }\left( {{F}_{n}\left( {\mathbb{C}}^{\infty }\right) ;\mathbb{Z}}\right) \) is \( {\left( 1 - t\right) }^{-n} \) . For \( {H}^{ * }\left( {{F}_{n}\left( {\mathbb{C}}^{n}\right) ;\mathbb{Z}}\right) \) the Poincaré series is

\[
\left( {1 + t}\right) \left( {1 + t + {t}^{2}}\right) \cdots \left( {1 + t + \cdots  + {t}^{n - 1}}\right)  = \mathop{\prod }\limits_{{i = 1}}^{n}\frac{1 - {t}^{i}}{1 - t} = {\left( 1 - t\right) }^{-n}\mathop{\prod }\limits_{{i = 1}}^{n}\left( {1 - {t}^{i}}\right)
\]

From the additive isomorphism \( {H}^{ * }\left( {{F}_{n}\left( {\mathbb{C}}^{\infty }\right) ;\mathbb{Z}}\right)  \approx  {H}^{ * }\left( {{G}_{n}\left( {\mathbb{C}}^{\infty }\right) ;\mathbb{Z}}\right)  \otimes  {H}^{ * }\left( {{F}_{n}\left( {\mathbb{C}}^{n}\right) ;\mathbb{Z}}\right) \) we see that the Poincaré series \( p\left( t\right) \) of \( {H}^{ * }\left( {{G}_{n}\left( {\mathbb{C}}^{\infty }\right) ;\mathbb{Z}}\right) \) satisfies

\[
p\left( t\right) {\left( 1 - t\right) }^{-n}\mathop{\prod }\limits_{{i = 1}}^{n}\left( {1 - {t}^{i}}\right)  = {\left( 1 - t\right) }^{-n}\;\text{ and hence }\;p\left( t\right)  = \mathop{\prod }\limits_{{i = 1}}^{n}{\left( 1 - {t}^{i}\right) }^{-1}
\]

This is exactly the Poincaré series of \( \mathbb{Z}\left\lbrack  {{\sigma }_{1},\cdots ,{\sigma }_{n}}\right\rbrack \) since \( {\sigma }_{i} \) has degree \( i \) . As noted before, this implies that the image of \( {p}^{ * } \) is all the symmetric polynomials.

This finishes the proof for \( {G}_{n}\left( {\mathbb{C}}^{\infty }\right) \) . The same arguments apply in the other two cases, using \( {\mathbb{Z}}_{2} \) coefficients throughout in the real case and replacing ’rank’ by ’dimension’ for \( {\mathbb{Z}}_{2} \) vector spaces.

These calculations show that the isomorphism \( {H}^{ * }\left( {E;R}\right)  \approx  {H}^{ * }\left( {B;R}\right) { \otimes  }_{R}{H}^{ * }\left( {F;R}\right) \) of the Leray-Hirsch theorem is not generally a ring isomorphism, for if it were, then the polynomial ring \( {H}^{ * }\left( {{F}_{n}\left( {\mathbb{C}}^{\infty }\right) ;\mathbb{Z}}\right) \) would contain a copy of \( {H}^{ * }\left( {{F}_{n}\left( {\mathbb{C}}^{n}\right) ;\mathbb{Z}}\right) \) as a subring, but in the latter ring some power of every positive-dimensional element is zero since \( {H}^{k}\left( {{F}_{n}\left( {\mathbb{C}}^{n}\right) ;\mathbb{Z}}\right)  = 0 \) for sufficiently large \( k. \)

## The Gysin Sequence

Besides the Leray-Hirsch theorem, which deals with fiber bundles that are coho-mologically like products, there is another special class of fiber bundles for which an elementary analysis of their cohomology structure is possible. These are fiber bundles \( {S}^{n - 1} \rightarrow  E\overset{p}{ \rightarrow  }B \) satisfying an orientability hypothesis that will always hold if \( B \) is simply-connected or if we take cohomology with \( {\mathbb{Z}}_{2} \) coefficients. For such bundles we will show there is an exact sequence, called the Gysin sequence,

\[
\cdots  \rightarrow  {H}^{i - n}\left( {B;R}\right) \overset{ \smile  e}{ \rightarrow  }{H}^{i}\left( {B;R}\right) \overset{{p}^{ * }}{ \rightarrow  }{H}^{i}\left( {E;R}\right)  \rightarrow  {H}^{i - n + 1}\left( {B;R}\right)  \rightarrow  \cdots
\]

where \( e \) is a certain ’Euler class’ in \( {H}^{n}\left( {B;R}\right) \) . Since \( {H}^{i}\left( {B;R}\right)  = 0 \) for \( i < 0 \) , the initial portion of the Gysin sequence gives isomorphisms \( {p}^{ * } : {H}^{i}\left( {B;R}\right) \overset{ \approx  }{ \rightarrow  }{H}^{i}\left( {E;R}\right) \) for \( i < n - 1 \) , and the more interesting part of the sequence begins

\[
0 \rightarrow  {H}^{n - 1}\left( {B;R}\right) \overset{{p}^{ * }}{ \rightarrow  }{H}^{n - 1}\left( {E;R}\right)  \rightarrow  {H}^{0}\left( {B;R}\right) \overset{ \smile  e}{ \rightarrow  }{H}^{n}\left( {B;R}\right) \overset{{p}^{ * }}{ \rightarrow  }{H}^{n}\left( {E;R}\right)  \rightarrow  \cdots
\]

In the case of a product bundle \( E = {S}^{n - 1} \times  B \) there is a section, a map \( s : B \rightarrow  E \) with \( {ps} = \mathbb{1} \) , so the Gysin sequence breaks up into split short exact sequences

\[
0 \rightarrow  {H}^{i}\left( {B;R}\right) \overset{{p}^{ * }}{ \rightarrow  }{H}^{i}\left( {{S}^{n - 1} \times  B;R}\right)  \rightarrow  {H}^{i - n + 1}\left( {B;R}\right)  \rightarrow  0
\]

which agrees with the Künneth formula \( {H}^{ * }\left( {{S}^{n - 1} \times  B;R}\right)  \approx  {H}^{ * }\left( {{S}^{n - 1};R}\right) { \otimes  }_{R}{H}^{ * }\left( {B;R}\right) \) . The splitting holds whenever the bundle has a section, even if it is not a product.

For example, consider the bundle \( {S}^{n - 1} \rightarrow  {V}_{2}\left( {\mathbb{R}}^{n + 1}\right) \overset{p}{ \rightarrow  }{S}^{n} \) . Points of \( {V}_{2}\left( {\mathbb{R}}^{n + 1}\right) \) are pairs \( \left( {{v}_{1},{v}_{2}}\right) \) of orthogonal unit vectors in \( {\mathbb{R}}^{n + 1} \) , and \( p\left( {{v}_{1},{v}_{2}}\right)  = {v}_{1} \) . If we think of \( {v}_{1} \) as a point of \( {S}^{n} \) and \( {v}_{2} \) as a unit vector tangent to \( {S}^{n} \) at \( {v}_{1} \) , then \( {V}_{2}\left( {\mathbb{R}}^{n + 1}\right) \) is exactly the bundle of unit tangent vectors to \( {S}^{n} \) . A section of this bundle is a field of unit tangent vectors to \( {S}^{n} \) , and such a vector field exists iff \( n \) is odd by Theorem 2.28. The fact that the Gysin sequence splits when there is a section then says that \( {V}_{2}\left( {\mathbb{R}}^{n + 1}\right) \) has the same cohomology as the product \( {S}^{n - 1} \times  {S}^{n} \) if \( n \) is odd, at least when \( n > 1 \) so that the base space \( {S}^{n} \) is simply-connected and the orientability hypothesis is satisfied. When \( n \) is even, the calculations at the end of §3.D show that \( {H}^{ * }\left( {{V}_{2}\left( {\mathbb{R}}^{n + 1}\right) ;\mathbb{Z}}\right) \) consists of \( \mathbb{Z} \) ’s in dimensions 0 and \( {2n} - 1 \) and a \( {\mathbb{Z}}_{2} \) in dimension \( n \) . The latter group appears in the Gysin sequence as

\[
{H}^{0}\left( {S}^{n}\right) \xrightarrow[]{ \smile  e}{H}^{n}\left( {S}^{n}\right)  \rightarrow  {H}^{n}\left( {V}_{2}\right) \left( {\mathbb{R}}^{n + 1}\right)  \rightarrow  {H}^{1}\left( {S}^{n}\right) \xrightarrow[\begin{matrix} \parallel \\  {\mathbb{Z}}_{2} \end{matrix}]{}{H}^{1}\left( {S}^{n}\right)
\]

hence the Euler class \( e \) must be twice a generator of \( {H}^{n}\left( {S}^{n}\right) \) in the case that \( n \) is even. When \( n \) is odd it must be zero in order for the Gysin sequence to split.

This example illustrates a theorem in differential topology that explains why the Euler class has this name: The Euler class of the unit tangent bundle of a closed orientable smooth \( n \) -manifold \( M \) is equal to the Euler characteristic \( \chi \left( M\right) \) times a generator of \( {H}^{n}\left( {M;\mathbb{Z}}\right) \) .

Whenever a bundle \( {S}^{n - 1} \rightarrow  E\overset{p}{ \rightarrow  }B \) has a section, the Euler class \( e \) must be zero from exactness of \( {H}^{0}\left( B\right) \overset{ \smile  e}{ \rightarrow  }{H}^{n}\left( B\right) \overset{{p}^{ * }}{ \rightarrow  }{H}^{n}\left( E\right) \) since \( {p}^{ * } \) is injective if there is a section. Thus the Euler class can be viewed as an obstruction to the existence of a section: If the Euler class is nonzero, there can be no section. This qualitative statement can be made more precise by bringing in the machinery of obstruction theory, as explained in [Milnor & Stasheff 1974] or [VBKT].

Before deriving the Gysin sequence let us look at some examples of how it can be used to compute cup products.

Example 4D.5. Consider a bundle \( {S}^{n - 1} \rightarrow  E\overset{p}{ \rightarrow  }B \) with \( E \) contractible, for example the bundle \( {S}^{1} \rightarrow  {S}^{\infty } \rightarrow  {\mathbb{{CP}}}^{\infty } \) or its real or quaternionic analogs. The long exact sequence of homotopy groups for the bundle shows that \( B \) is \( \left( {n - 1}\right) \) -connected. Thus if \( n > 1, B \) is simply-connected and we have a Gysin sequence for cohomology with \( \mathbb{Z} \) coefficients. For \( n = 1 \) we take \( {\mathbb{Z}}_{2} \) coefficients. If \( n > 1 \) then since \( E \) is contractible, the Gysin sequence implies that \( {H}^{i}\left( {B;\mathbb{Z}}\right)  = 0 \) for \( 0 < i < n \) and that \( \sim  e : {H}^{i}\left( {B;\mathbb{Z}}\right)  \rightarrow  {H}^{i + n}\left( {B;\mathbb{Z}}\right) \) is an isomorphism for \( i \geq  0 \) . It follows that \( {H}^{ * }\left( {B;\mathbb{Z}}\right) \) is the polynomial ring \( \mathbb{Z}\left\lbrack  e\right\rbrack \) . When \( n = 1 \) the map \( {p}^{ * } : {H}^{n - 1}\left( {B;{\mathbb{Z}}_{2}}\right)  \rightarrow  {H}^{n - 1}\left( {E;{\mathbb{Z}}_{2}}\right) \) in the Gysin sequence is surjective, so we see that \( \smile  e : {H}^{i}\left( {B;{\mathbb{Z}}_{2}}\right)  \rightarrow  {H}^{i + n}\left( {B;{\mathbb{Z}}_{2}}\right) \) is again an isomorphism for all \( i \geq  0 \) , and hence \( {H}^{ * }\left( {B;{\mathbb{Z}}_{2}}\right)  \approx  {\mathbb{Z}}_{2}\left\lbrack  e\right\rbrack \) . Thus the Gysin sequence gives a new derivation of the cup product structure in projective spaces. Also, since polynomial rings \( \mathbb{Z}\left\lbrack  e\right\rbrack \) are realizable as \( {H}^{ * }\left( {X;\mathbb{Z}}\right) \) only when \( e \) has dimension 2 or 4, as we show in Corollary 4L.10, we can conclude that there exist bundles \( {S}^{n - 1} \rightarrow  E \rightarrow  B \) with \( E \) contractible only when \( n \) is 1,2, or 4 .

Example 4D.6. For the Grassmann manifold \( {G}_{n} = {G}_{n}\left( {\mathbb{R}}^{\infty }\right) \) we have \( {\pi }_{1}\left( {G}_{n}\right)  \approx \; {\pi }_{0}O\left( n\right)  \approx  {\mathbb{Z}}_{2} \) , so the universal cover of \( {G}_{n} \) gives a bundle \( {S}^{0} \rightarrow  {\widetilde{G}}_{n} \rightarrow  {G}_{n} \) . One can view \( {\widetilde{G}}_{n} \) as the space of oriented \( n \) -planes in \( {\mathbb{R}}^{\infty } \) , which is obviously a 2-sheeted covering space of \( {G}_{n} \) , hence the universal cover since it is path-connected, being the quotient \( {V}_{n}\left( {\mathbb{R}}^{\infty }\right) /{SO}\left( n\right) \) of the contractible space \( {V}_{n}\left( {\mathbb{R}}^{\infty }\right) \) . A portion of the Gysin sequence for the bundle \( {S}^{0} \rightarrow  {\widetilde{G}}_{n} \rightarrow  {G}_{n} \) is \( {H}^{0}\left( {{G}_{n};{\mathbb{Z}}_{2}}\right) \overset{ \smile  e}{ \rightarrow  }{H}^{1}\left( {{G}_{n};{\mathbb{Z}}_{2}}\right)  \rightarrow  {H}^{1}\left( {{\widetilde{G}}_{n};{\mathbb{Z}}_{2}}\right) \) . This last group is zero since \( {\widetilde{G}}_{n} \) is simply-connected, and \( {H}^{1}\left( {{G}_{n};{\mathbb{Z}}_{2}}\right)  \approx  {\mathbb{Z}}_{2} \) since \( {H}^{ * }\left( {{G}_{n};{\mathbb{Z}}_{2}}\right)  \approx  {\mathbb{Z}}_{2}\left\lbrack  {{w}_{1},\cdots ,{w}_{n}}\right\rbrack \) as we showed earlier in this section, so \( e = {w}_{1} \) and the map \( \smile  e : {H}^{ * }\left( {{G}_{n};{\mathbb{Z}}_{2}}\right)  \rightarrow  {H}^{ * }\left( {{G}_{n};{\mathbb{Z}}_{2}}\right) \) is injective. The Gysin sequence then breaks up into short exact sequences \( 0 \rightarrow  {H}^{i}\left( {{G}_{n};{\mathbb{Z}}_{2}}\right) \overset{ \smile  e}{ \rightarrow  }{H}^{i + 1}\left( {{G}_{n};{\mathbb{Z}}_{2}}\right)  \rightarrow  {H}^{i + 1}\left( {{\widetilde{G}}_{n};{\mathbb{Z}}_{2}}\right)  \rightarrow  0 \) , from which it follows that \( {H}^{ * }\left( {{\widetilde{G}}_{n};{\mathbb{Z}}_{2}}\right) \) is the quotient ring \( {\mathbb{Z}}_{2}\left\lbrack  {{w}_{1},\cdots ,{w}_{n}}\right\rbrack  /\left( {w}_{1}\right)  \approx \; {\mathbb{Z}}_{2}\left\lbrack  {{w}_{2},\cdots ,{w}_{n}}\right\rbrack \)

Example 4D.7. The complex analog of the bundle in the preceding example is a bundle \( {S}^{1} \rightarrow  {\widetilde{G}}_{n}\left( {\mathbb{C}}^{\infty }\right)  \rightarrow  {G}_{n}\left( {\mathbb{C}}^{\infty }\right) \) with \( {\widetilde{G}}_{n}\left( {\mathbb{C}}^{\infty }\right) \) 2-connected. This can be constructed in the following way. There is a determinant homomorphism \( U\left( n\right)  \rightarrow  {S}^{1} \) with kernel \( {SU}\left( n\right) \) , the unitary matrices of determinant 1, so \( {S}^{1} \) is the coset space \( U\left( n\right) /{SU}\left( n\right) \) , and by restricting the action of \( U\left( n\right) \) on \( {V}_{n}\left( {\mathbb{C}}^{\infty }\right) \) to \( {SU}\left( n\right) \) we obtain the second row of the commutative diagram at the right. The second row is a fiber bundle by the usual argument of choosing continuously varying orthonormal bases in \( n \) -planes near a given \( n \) -plane. One sees that the space \( {\widetilde{G}}_{n}\left( {\mathbb{C}}^{\infty }\right)  = {V}_{n}\left( {\mathbb{C}}^{\infty }\right) /{SU}\left( n\right) \) is 2-connected by looking at the relevant portion of the diagram of homotopy groups associated to these two bundles:

\[
0 \rightarrow  {\pi }_{2}\left( {G}_{n}\right) \overset{\partial }{ \rightarrow  }{\pi }_{1}\left( {U\left( n\right) }\right)  \rightarrow  0
\]

\[
0 \rightarrow  {\pi }_{2}\left( {\widetilde{G}}_{n}\right)  \rightarrow  {\pi }_{2}\left( {G}_{n}\right)  \rightarrow  {\pi }_{2}\left( {G}_{n}\right)  \rightarrow  {\pi }_{1}\left( {S}^{1}\right)  \rightarrow  {\pi }_{1}\left( {\widetilde{G}}_{n}\right)  \rightarrow  0
\]

![bo_d7dtv0s91nqc73eq8nv0_447_929_2039_658_168_0.jpg](images/bo_d7dtv0s91nqc73eq8nv0_447_929_2039_658_168_0.jpg)

The second vertical map is an isomorphism since \( {S}^{1} \) embeds in \( U\left( n\right) \) as the subgroup \( U\left( 1\right) \) . Since the boundary map in the upper row is an isomorphism, so also is the boundary map in the lower row, and then exactness implies that \( {\widetilde{G}}_{n} \) is 2-connected.

The Gysin sequence for \( {S}^{1} \rightarrow  {\widetilde{G}}_{n}\left( {\mathbb{C}}^{\infty }\right)  \rightarrow  {G}_{n}\left( {\mathbb{C}}^{\infty }\right) \) can be analyzed just as in the preceding example. Part of the sequence is \( {H}^{0}\left( {{G}_{n};\mathbb{Z}}\right) \overset{ \smile  e}{ \rightarrow  }{H}^{2}\left( {{G}_{n};\mathbb{Z}}\right)  \rightarrow  {H}^{2}\left( {{\widetilde{G}}_{n};\mathbb{Z}}\right) \) , and this last group is zero since \( {\widetilde{G}}_{n} \) is 2-connected, so \( e \) must be a generator of \( {H}^{2}\left( {{G}_{n};\mathbb{Z}}\right)  \approx  \mathbb{Z} \) . Since \( {H}^{ * }\left( {{G}_{n};\mathbb{Z}}\right) \) is a polynomial algebra \( \mathbb{Z}\left\lbrack  {{c}_{1},\cdots ,{c}_{n}}\right\rbrack \) , we must have \( e =  \pm  {c}_{1} \) , so the map \( \smile  e : {H}^{ * }\left( {{G}_{n};\mathbb{Z}}\right)  \rightarrow  {H}^{ * }\left( {{G}_{n};\mathbb{Z}}\right) \) is injective, the Gysin sequence breaks up into short exact sequences, and \( {H}^{ * }\left( {{\widetilde{G}}_{n};\mathbb{Z}}\right) \) is the quotient ring \( \mathbb{Z}\left\lbrack  {{c}_{1},\cdots ,{c}_{n}}\right\rbrack  /\left( {c}_{1}\right)  \approx  \mathbb{Z}\left\lbrack  {{c}_{2},\cdots ,{c}_{n}}\right\rbrack  . \)

The spaces \( {\widetilde{G}}_{n} \) in the last two examples are often denoted \( {BSO}\left( n\right) \) and \( {BSU}\left( n\right) \) , expressing the fact that they are related to the groups \( {SO}\left( n\right) \) and \( {SU}\left( n\right) \) via bundles \( {SO}\left( n\right)  \rightarrow  {V}_{n}\left( {\mathbb{R}}^{\infty }\right)  \rightarrow  {BSO}\left( n\right) \) and \( {SU}\left( n\right)  \rightarrow  {V}_{n}\left( {\mathbb{C}}^{\infty }\right)  \rightarrow  {BSU}\left( n\right) \) with contractible total spaces \( {V}_{n} \) . There is no quaternion analog of \( {BSO}\left( n\right) \) and \( {BSU}\left( n\right) \) since for \( n = 2 \) this would give a space with cohomology ring \( \mathbb{Z}\left\lbrack  x\right\rbrack \) on an 8-dimensional generator, which is impossible by Corollary 4L.10.

Now we turn to the derivation of the Gysin sequence, which follows a rather roundabout route:

(1) Deduce a relative version of the Leray-Hirsch theorem from the absolute case.

(2) Specialize this to the case of bundles with fiber a disk, yielding a basic result called the Thom isomorphism.

(3) Show this applies to all orientable disk bundles.

(4) Deduce the Gysin sequence by plugging the Thom isomorphism into the long exact sequence of cohomology groups for the pair consisting of a disk bundle and its boundary sphere bundle.

(1) A fiber bundle pair consists of a fiber bundle \( p : E \rightarrow  B \) with fiber \( F \) , together with a subspace \( {E}^{\prime } \subset  E \) such that \( p : {E}^{\prime } \rightarrow  B \) is a bundle with fiber a subspace \( {F}^{\prime } \subset  F \) , with local trivializations for \( {E}^{\prime } \) obtained by restricting local trivializations for \( E \) . For example, if \( E \rightarrow  B \) is a bundle with fiber \( {D}^{n} \) and \( {E}^{\prime } \subset  E \) is the union of the boundary spheres of the fibers, then \( \left( {E,{E}^{\prime }}\right) \) is a fiber bundle pair since local trivializations of \( E \) restrict to local trivializations of \( {E}^{\prime } \) , in view of the fact that homeomorphisms from an \( n \) -disk to an \( n \) -disk restrict to homeomorphisms between their boundary spheres, boundary and interior points of \( {D}^{n} \) being distinguished by the local homology groups \( {H}_{n}\left( {{D}^{n},{D}^{n}-\{ x\} ;\mathbb{Z}}\right) \) .

Theorem 4D.8. Suppose that \( \left( {F,{F}^{\prime }}\right)  \rightarrow  \left( {E,{E}^{\prime }}\right) \overset{p}{ \rightarrow  }B \) is a fiber bundle pair such that \( {H}^{ * }\left( {F,{F}^{\prime };R}\right) \) is a free \( R \) -module, finitely generated in each dimension. If there exist classes \( {c}_{j} \in  {H}^{ * }\left( {E,{E}^{\prime };R}\right) \) whose restrictions form a basis for \( {H}^{ * }\left( {F,{F}^{\prime };R}\right) \) in each fiber \( \left( {F,{F}^{\prime }}\right) \) , then \( {H}^{ * }\left( {E,{E}^{\prime };R}\right) \) , as a module over \( {H}^{ * }\left( {B;R}\right) \) , is free with basis \( \left\{  {c}_{j}\right\} \) .

The module structure is defined just as in the absolute case by \( {bc} = {p}^{ * }\left( b\right)  \smile  c \) , but now we use the relative cup product \( {H}^{ * }\left( {E;R}\right)  \times  {H}^{ * }\left( {E,{E}^{\prime };R}\right)  \rightarrow  {H}^{ * }\left( {E,{E}^{\prime };R}\right) \) .

Proof: Construct a bundle \( \widehat{E} \rightarrow  B \) from \( E \) by attaching the mapping cylinder \( M \) of \( p : {E}^{\prime } \rightarrow  B \) to \( E \) by identifying the subspaces \( {E}^{\prime } \subset  E \) and \( {E}^{\prime } \subset  M \) . Thus the fibers \( \widehat{F} \) of \( \widehat{E} \) are obtained from the fibers \( F \) by attaching cones \( C{F}^{\prime } \) on the subspaces \( {F}^{\prime } \subset  F \) . Regarding \( B \) as the subspace of \( \widehat{E} \) at one end of the mapping cylinder \( M \) , we have \( {H}^{ * }\left( {\widehat{E}, M;R}\right)  \approx  {H}^{ * }\left( {\widehat{E} - B, M - B;R}\right)  \approx  {H}^{ * }\left( {E,{E}^{\prime };R}\right) \) via excision and the obvious deformation retraction of \( \widehat{E} - B \) onto \( E \) . The long exact sequence of a triple gives \( {H}^{ * }\left( {\widehat{E}, M;R}\right)  \approx  {H}^{ * }\left( {\widehat{E}, B;R}\right) \) since \( M \) deformation retracts to \( B \) . All these isomorphisms are \( {H}^{ * }\left( {B;R}\right) \) -module isomorphisms. Since \( B \) is a retract of \( \widehat{E} \) via the bundle projection \( \widehat{E} \rightarrow  B \) , we have a splitting \( {H}^{ * }\left( {\widehat{E};R}\right)  \approx  {H}^{ * }\left( {\widehat{E}, B;R}\right) \bigoplus {H}^{ * }\left( {B;R}\right) \) as \( {H}^{ * }\left( {B;R}\right) \) -modules. Let \( {\widehat{c}}_{j} \in  {H}^{ * }\left( {\widehat{E};R}\right) \) correspond to \( {c}_{j} \in  {H}^{ * }\left( {E,{E}^{\prime };R}\right)  \approx  {H}^{ * }\left( {\widehat{E}, B;R}\right) \) in this splitting. The classes \( {\widehat{c}}_{j} \) together with 1 restrict to a basis for \( {H}^{ * }\left( {\widehat{F};R}\right) \) in each fiber \( \widehat{F} = \; F \cup  C{F}^{\prime } \) , so the absolute form of the Leray-Hirsch theorem implies that \( {H}^{ * }\left( {\widehat{E};R}\right) \) is a free \( {H}^{ * }\left( {B;R}\right) \) -module with basis \( \left\{  {1,{\widehat{c}}_{j}}\right\} \) . It follows that \( \left\{  {c}_{j}\right\} \) is a basis for the free \( {H}^{ * }\left( {B;R}\right) \) -module \( {H}^{ * }\left( {E,{E}^{\prime };R}\right) \) .

(2) Now we specialize to the case of a fiber bundle pair \( \left( {{D}^{n},{S}^{n - 1}}\right)  \rightarrow  \left( {E,{E}^{\prime }}\right) \overset{p}{ \rightarrow  }B \) . An element \( c \in  {H}^{n}\left( {E,{E}^{\prime };R}\right) \) whose restriction to each fiber \( \left( {{D}^{n},{S}^{n - 1}}\right) \) is a generator of \( {H}^{n}\left( {{D}^{n},{S}^{n - 1};R}\right)  \approx  R \) is called a Thom class for the bundle. We are mainly interested in the cases \( R = \mathbb{Z} \) and \( {\mathbb{Z}}_{2} \) , but \( R \) could be any commutative ring with identity, in which case a 'generator' is an element with a multiplicative inverse, so all elements of \( R \) are multiples of the generator. A Thom class with \( \mathbb{Z} \) coefficients gives rise to a Thom class with any other coefficient ring \( R \) under the homomorphism \( {H}^{n}\left( {E,{E}^{\prime };\mathbb{Z}}\right)  \rightarrow  {H}^{n}\left( {E,{E}^{\prime };R}\right) \) induced by the homomorphism \( \mathbb{Z} \rightarrow  R \) sending 1 to the identity element of \( R \) .

Corollary 4D.9. If the disk bundle \( \left( {{D}^{n},{S}^{n - 1}}\right)  \rightarrow  \left( {E,{E}^{\prime }}\right) \overset{p}{ \rightarrow  }B \) has a Thom class \( c \in  {H}^{n}\left( {E,{E}^{\prime };R}\right) \) , then the map \( \Phi  : {H}^{i}\left( {B;R}\right)  \rightarrow  {H}^{i + n}\left( {E,{E}^{\prime };R}\right) ,\Phi \left( b\right)  = {p}^{ * }\left( b\right)  \smile  c \) , is an isomorphism for all \( i \geq  0 \) , and \( {H}^{i}\left( {E,{E}^{\prime };R}\right)  = 0 \) for \( i < n \) .

The isomorphism \( \Phi \) is called the Thom isomorphism. The corollary can be made into a statement about absolute cohomology by defining the Thom space \( T\left( E\right) \) to be the quotient \( E/{E}^{\prime } \) . Each disk fiber \( {D}^{n} \) of \( E \) becomes a sphere \( {S}^{n} \) in \( T\left( E\right) \) , and all these spheres coming from different fibers are disjoint except for the common basepoint \( {x}_{0} = {E}^{\prime }/{E}^{\prime } \) . A Thom class can be regarded as an element of \( {H}^{n}\left( {T\left( E\right) ,{x}_{0};R}\right)  \approx \; {H}^{n}\left( {T\left( E\right) ;R}\right) \) that restricts to a generator of \( {H}^{n}\left( {{S}^{n};R}\right) \) in each ’fiber’ \( {S}^{n} \) in \( T\left( E\right) \) , and the Thom isomorphism becomes \( {H}^{i}\left( {B;R}\right)  \approx  {\widetilde{H}}^{n + i}\left( {T\left( E\right) ;R}\right) \) .

(3) The major remaining step in the derivation of the Gysin sequence is to relate the existence of a Thom class for a disk bundle \( {D}^{n} \rightarrow  E \rightarrow  B \) to a notion of orientability of the bundle. First we define orientability for a sphere bundle \( {S}^{n - 1} \rightarrow  {E}^{\prime } \rightarrow  B \) . In the proof of Proposition 4.61 we described a procedure for lifting paths \( \gamma \) in \( B \) to homotopy equivalences \( {L}_{\gamma } \) between the fibers above the endpoints of \( \gamma \) . We did this for fibrations rather than fiber bundles, but the method applies equally well to fiber bundles whose fiber is a CW complex since the homotopy lifting property was used only for the fiber and for the product of the fiber with \( I \) . In the case of a sphere bundle \( {S}^{n - 1} \rightarrow  {E}^{\prime } \rightarrow  B \) , if \( \gamma \) is a loop in \( B \) then \( {L}_{\gamma } \) is a homotopy equivalence from the fiber \( {S}^{n - 1} \) over the basepoint of \( \gamma \) to itself, and we define the sphere bundle to be orientable if \( {L}_{\gamma } \) induces the identity map on \( {H}^{n - 1}\left( {{S}^{n - 1};\mathbb{Z}}\right) \) for each loop \( \gamma \) in \( B \) .

For example, the Klein bottle, regarded as a bundle over \( {S}^{1} \) with fiber \( {S}^{1} \) , is nonorientable since as we follow a path looping once around the base circle, the corresponding fiber circles sweep out the full Klein bottle, ending up where they started but with orientation reversed. The same reasoning shows that the torus, viewed as a circle bundle over \( {S}^{1} \) , is orientable. More generally, any sphere bundle that is a product is orientable since the maps \( {L}_{y} \) can be taken to be the identity for all loops \( y \) . Also, sphere bundles over simply-connected base spaces are orientable since \( \gamma  \simeq  \eta \) implies \( {L}_{\gamma } \simeq  {L}_{\eta } \) , hence all \( {L}_{\gamma } \) ’s are homotopic to the identity when all loops \( \gamma \) are nullhomotopic.

One could define orientability for a disk bundle \( {D}^{n} \rightarrow  E \rightarrow  B \) by relativizing the previous definition, constructing lifts \( {L}_{\gamma } \) which are homotopy equivalences of the fiber pairs \( \left( {{D}^{n},{S}^{n - 1}}\right) \) . However, since \( {H}^{n}\left( {{D}^{n},{S}^{n - 1};\mathbb{Z}}\right) \) is canonically isomorphic to \( {H}^{n - 1}\left( {{S}^{n - 1};\mathbb{Z}}\right) \) via the coboundary map in the long exact sequence of the pair, it is simpler and amounts to the same thing just to define \( E \) to be orientable if its boundary sphere subbundle \( {E}^{\prime } \) is orientable.

Theorem 4D.10. Every disk bundle has a Thom class with \( {\mathbb{Z}}_{2} \) coefficients, and ori-entable disk bundles have Thom classes with \( \mathbb{Z} \) coefficients.

An exercise at the end of the section is to show that the converse of the last statement is also true: A disk bundle is orientable if it has a Thom class with \( \mathbb{Z} \) coefficients.

Proof: The case of a non-CW base space \( B \) reduces to the CW case by pulling back over a CW approximation to \( B \) , as in the Leray-Hirsch theorem, applying the five-lemma to say that the pullback bundle has isomorphic homotopy groups, hence isomorphic absolute and relative cohomology groups. From the definition of the pullback bundle it is immediate that the pullback of an orientable sphere bundle is orientable. There is also no harm in assuming the base CW complex \( B \) is connected. We will show:

If the disk bundle \( {D}^{n} \rightarrow  E \rightarrow  B \) is orientable and \( B \) is a connected CW complex,

(*) then the restriction map \( {H}^{i}\left( {E,{E}^{\prime };\mathbb{Z}}\right)  \rightarrow  {H}^{i}\left( {{D}_{x}^{n},{S}_{x}^{n - 1};\mathbb{Z}}\right) \) is an isomorphism for all fibers \( {D}_{x}^{n}, x \in  B \) , and for all \( i \leq  n \) .

For \( {\mathbb{Z}}_{2} \) coefficients we will see that \( \left( *\right) \) holds without any orientability hypothesis. Hence with either \( \mathbb{Z} \) or \( {\mathbb{Z}}_{2} \) coefficients, a generator of \( {H}^{n}\left( {E,{E}^{\prime }}\right)  \approx  {H}^{n}\left( {{D}_{x}^{n},{S}_{x}^{n - 1}}\right) \) is a Thom class.

If the disk bundle \( {D}^{n} \rightarrow  E \rightarrow  B \) is orientable, then if we choose an isomorphism \( {H}^{n}\left( {{D}_{x}^{n},{S}_{x}^{n - 1};\mathbb{Z}}\right)  \approx  \mathbb{Z} \) for one fiber \( {D}_{x}^{n} \) , this determines such isomorphisms for all fibers by composing with the isomorphisms \( {L}_{y}^{ * } \) , which depend only on the endpoints of \( \gamma \) . Having made such a choice, then if \( \left( *\right) \) is true, we have a preferred isomorphism \( {H}^{n}\left( {E,{E}^{\prime };\mathbb{Z}}\right)  \approx  \mathbb{Z} \) which restricts to the chosen isomorphism \( {H}^{n}\left( {{D}_{x}^{n},{S}_{x}^{n - 1};\mathbb{Z}}\right)  \approx  \mathbb{Z} \) for each fiber. This is because for a path \( \gamma \) from \( x \) to \( y \) , the inclusion \( \left( {{D}_{x}^{n},{S}_{x}^{n - 1}}\right)  \hookrightarrow  \left( {E,{E}^{\prime }}\right) \) is homotopic to the composition of \( {L}_{y} \) with the inclusion \( \left( {{D}_{y}^{n},{S}_{y}^{n - 1}}\right)  \hookrightarrow  \left( {E,{E}^{\prime }}\right) \) . We will use this preferred isomorphism \( {H}^{n}\left( {E,{E}^{\prime };\mathbb{Z}}\right)  \approx  \mathbb{Z} \) in the inductive proof of \( \left( *\right) \) given below. In the case of \( {\mathbb{Z}}_{2} \) coefficients, there can be only one isomorphism of a group with \( {\mathbb{Z}}_{2} \) so no choices are necessary and orientability is irrelevant. We will prove \( \left( *\right) \) in the \( \mathbb{Z} \) coefficient case, leaving it to the reader to replace all \( \mathbb{Z} \) ’s in the proof by \( {\mathbb{Z}}_{2} \) ’s to obtain a proof in the \( {\mathbb{Z}}_{2} \) case.

Suppose first that the CW complex \( B \) has finite dimension \( k \) . Let \( U \subset  B \) be the subspace obtained by deleting one point from the interior of each \( k \) -cell of \( B \) , and let \( V \subset  B \) be the union of the open \( k \) -cells. Thus \( B = U \cup  V \) . For a subspace \( A \subset  B \) let \( {E}_{A} \rightarrow  A \) and \( {E}_{A}^{\prime } \rightarrow  A \) be the disk and sphere bundles obtained by taking the subspaces of \( E \) and \( {E}^{\prime } \) projecting to \( A \) . Consider the following portion of a Mayer-Vietoris sequence, with \( \mathbb{Z} \) coefficients implicit from now on:

\[
{H}^{n}\left( {E,{E}^{\prime }}\right)  \rightarrow  {H}^{n}\left( {{E}_{U},{E}_{U}^{\prime }}\right) \bigoplus {H}^{n}\left( {{E}_{V},{E}_{V}^{\prime }}\right) \xrightarrow[]{\Psi }{H}^{n}\left( {{E}_{U \cap  V},{E}_{U \cap  V}^{\prime }}\right)
\]

The first map is injective since the preceding term in the sequence is zero by induction on \( k \) , since \( U \cap  V \) deformation retracts onto a disjoint union of \( \left( {k - 1}\right) \) -spheres and we can apply Lemma 4D.2 to replace \( {E}_{U \cap  V} \) by the part of \( E \) over this union of \( \left( {k - 1}\right) \) -spheres. By exactness we then have an isomorphism \( {H}^{n}\left( {E,{E}^{\prime }}\right)  \approx  \operatorname{Ker}\Psi \) . Similarly, by Lemma 4D.2 and induction each of the terms \( {H}^{n}\left( {{E}_{U},{E}_{U}^{\prime }}\right) ,{H}^{n}\left( {{E}_{V},{E}_{V}^{\prime }}\right) \) , and \( {H}^{n}\left( {{E}_{U \cap  V},{E}_{U \cap  V}^{\prime }}\right) \) is a product of \( \mathbb{Z} \) ’s, with one \( \mathbb{Z} \) factor for each component of the spaces involved, projection onto the \( \mathbb{Z} \) factor being given by restriction to any fiber in the component. Elements of Ker \( \Psi \) are pairs \( \left( {\alpha ,\beta }\right)  \in  {H}^{n}\left( {{E}_{U},{E}_{U}^{\prime }}\right) \bigoplus {H}^{n}\left( {{E}_{V},{E}_{V}^{\prime }}\right) \) having the same restriction to \( {H}^{n}\left( {{E}_{U \cap  V},{E}_{U \cap  V}^{\prime }}\right) \) . Since \( B \) is connected, this means that all the \( \mathbb{Z} \) coordinates of \( \alpha \) and \( \beta \) in the previous direct product decompositions must be equal, since between any two components of \( U \) or \( V \) one can interpolate a finite sequence of components of \( U \) and \( V \) alternately, each component in the sequence having nontrivial intersection with its neighbors. Thus \( \operatorname{Ker}\Psi \) is a copy of \( \mathbb{Z} \) , with restriction to a fiber being the isomorphism \( {H}^{n}\left( {E,{E}^{\prime }}\right)  \approx  \mathbb{Z} \) .

To finish proving \( \left( *\right) \) for finite-dimensional \( B \) it remains to see that \( {H}^{i}\left( {E,{E}^{\prime }}\right)  = 0 \) for \( i < n \) , but this follows immediately by looking at an earlier stage of the Mayer-Vietoris sequence, where the two terms adjacent to \( {H}^{i}\left( {E,{E}^{\prime }}\right) \) vanish by induction.

Proving (*) for an infinite-dimensional CW complex \( B \) reduces to the finite-dimensional case as in the Leray-Hirsch theorem since we are only interested in cohomology in a finite range of dimensions.

(4) Now we can derive the Gysin sequence for a sphere bundle \( {S}^{n - 1} \rightarrow  E\overset{p}{ \rightarrow  }B \) . Consider the mapping cylinder \( {M}_{p} \) , which is a disk bundle \( {D}^{n} \rightarrow  {M}_{p}\overset{p}{ \rightarrow  }B \) with \( E \) as its boundary sphere bundle. Assuming that a Thom class \( c \in  {H}^{n}\left( {{M}_{p}, E;R}\right) \) exists, as is the case if \( E \) is orientable or if \( R = {\mathbb{Z}}_{2} \) , then the long exact sequence of cohomology groups for the pair \( \left( {{M}_{p}, E}\right) \) gives the first row of the following commutative diagram, with \( R \) coefficients implicit:

\[
\cdots  \rightarrow  {H}^{i}\left( {{M}_{p}, E}\right) \overset{{j}^{ * }}{ \rightarrow  }{H}^{i}\left( {M}_{p}\right)  \rightarrow  {H}^{i}\left( E\right)  \rightarrow  {H}^{i + 1}\left( {{M}_{p}, E}\right)  \rightarrow  \cdots
\]

\[
\approx   \uparrow  \Phi \; \approx   \uparrow  \theta \; \approx   \uparrow  {p}^{ * }
\]

\[
\cdots  \rightarrow  {H}^{i - n}\left( B\right) \overset{ \smile  e}{ \rightarrow  }{H}^{i}\left( B\right) \overset{{p}^{ * }}{ \rightarrow  }{H}^{i}\left( E\right)  \rightarrow  {H}^{i - n + 1}\left( B\right)  \rightarrow  \cdots
\]

The maps \( \Phi \) are the Thom isomorphism, and the vertical map \( {p}^{ * } \) is an isomorphism since \( {M}_{p} \) deformation retracts onto \( B \) . The Euler class \( e \in  {H}^{n}\left( {B;R}\right) \) is defined to be \( {\left( {p}^{ * }\right) }^{-1}{j}^{ * }\left( c\right) , c \) being a Thom class. The square containing the map \( \smile  e \) commutes since for \( b \in  {H}^{i - n}\left( {B;R}\right) \) we have \( {j}^{ * }\Phi \left( b\right)  = {j}^{ * }\left( {{p}^{ * }\left( b\right)  - c}\right)  = {p}^{ * }\left( b\right)  \smile  {j}^{ * }\left( c\right) \) , which equals \( {p}^{ * }\left( {b \smile  e}\right)  = {p}^{ * }\left( b\right)  \smile  {p}^{ * }\left( e\right) \) since \( {p}^{ * }\left( e\right)  = {j}^{ * }\left( c\right) \) . Another way of defining \( e \) is as the class corresponding to \( c \smile  c \) under the Thom isomorphism, since \( \Phi \left( e\right)  = \; {p}^{ * }\left( e\right)  \smile  c = {j}^{ * }\left( c\right)  \smile  c = c \smile  c. \)

Finally, the lower row of the diagram is by definition the Gysin sequence.

To conclude this section we will use the following rather specialized application of the Gysin sequence to compute a few more examples of spaces with polynomial cohomology.

Proposition 4D.11. Suppose that \( {S}^{{2k} - 1} \rightarrow  E\overset{p}{ \rightarrow  }B \) is an orientable sphere bundle such that \( {H}^{ * }\left( {E;R}\right) \) is a polynomial ring \( R\left\lbrack  {{x}_{1},\cdots ,{x}_{\ell }}\right\rbrack \) on even-dimensional generators \( {x}_{i} \) . Then \( {H}^{ * }\left( {B;R}\right)  = R\left\lbrack  {{y}_{1},\cdots ,{y}_{\ell }, e}\right\rbrack \) where \( e \) is the Euler class of the bundle and \( {p}^{ * }\left( {y}_{i}\right)  = {x}_{i} \) for each \( i \) .

Proof: Consider the three terms \( {H}^{i}\left( {B;R}\right) \overset{ \smile  e}{ \rightarrow  }{H}^{i + {2k}}\left( {B;R}\right)  \rightarrow  {H}^{i + {2k}}\left( {E;R}\right) \) of the Gysin sequence. If \( i \) is odd, the third term is zero since \( E \) has no odd-dimensional cohomology. Hence the map \( \smile  e \) is surjective, and by induction on dimension this implies that \( {H}^{ * }\left( {B;R}\right) \) is zero in odd dimensions. This means the Gysin sequence reduces to short exact sequences

\[
0 \rightarrow  {H}^{2i}\left( {B;R}\right) \overset{ \smile  e}{ \rightarrow  }{H}^{{2i} + {2k}}\left( {B;R}\right) \overset{{p}^{ * }}{ \rightarrow  }{H}^{{2i} + {2k}}\left( {E;R}\right)  \rightarrow  0
\]

Since \( {p}^{ * } \) is surjective, we can choose elements \( {y}_{j} \in  {H}^{ * }\left( {B;R}\right) \) with \( {p}^{ * }\left( {y}_{j}\right)  = {x}_{j} \) . It remains to check that \( {H}^{ * }\left( {B;R}\right)  = R\left\lbrack  {{y}_{1},\cdots ,{y}_{\ell }, e}\right\rbrack \) , which is elementary algebra: Given \( b \in  {H}^{ * }\left( {B;R}\right) ,{p}^{ * }\left( b\right) \) must be a polynomial \( f\left( {{x}_{1},\cdots ,{x}_{\ell }}\right) \) , so \( b - f\left( {{y}_{1},\cdots ,{y}_{\ell }}\right) \) is in the kernel of \( {p}^{ * } \) and exactness gives an equation \( b - f\left( {{y}_{1},\cdots ,{y}_{\ell }}\right)  = {b}^{\prime } \smile  e \) for some \( {b}^{\prime } \in  {H}^{ * }\left( {B;R}\right) \) . Since \( {b}^{\prime } \) has lower dimension than \( b \) , we may assume by induction that \( {b}^{\prime } \) is a polynomial in \( {y}_{1},\cdots ,{y}_{\ell }, e \) . Hence \( b = f\left( {{y}_{1},\cdots ,{y}_{\ell }}\right)  + {b}^{\prime } \smile  e \) is also a polynomial in \( {y}_{1},\cdots ,{y}_{\ell }, e \) . Thus the natural map \( R\left\lbrack  {{y}_{1},\cdots ,{y}_{\ell }, e}\right\rbrack   \rightarrow  {H}^{ * }\left( {B;R}\right) \) is surjective. To see that it is injective, suppose there is a polynomial relation \( f\left( {{y}_{1},\cdots ,{y}_{\ell }, e}\right)  = 0 \) in \( {H}^{ * }\left( {B;R}\right) \) . Applying \( {p}^{ * } \) , we get \( f\left( {{x}_{1},\cdots ,{x}_{\ell },0}\right)  = 0 \) since \( {p}^{ * }\left( {y}_{i}\right)  = {x}_{i} \) and \( {p}^{ * }\left( e\right)  = 0 \) from the short exact sequence. The relation \( f\left( {{x}_{1},\cdots ,{x}_{\ell },0}\right)  = 0 \) takes place in the polynomial ring \( R\left\lbrack  {{x}_{1},\cdots ,{x}_{\ell }}\right\rbrack \) , so \( f\left( {{y}_{1},\cdots ,{y}_{\ell },0}\right)  = 0 \) in \( R\left\lbrack  {{y}_{1},\cdots ,{y}_{\ell }, e}\right\rbrack \) , hence \( f\left( {{y}_{1},\cdots ,{y}_{\ell }, e}\right) \) must be divisible by \( e \) , say \( f = {ge} \) for some polynomial \( g \) . The relation \( f\left( {{y}_{1},\cdots ,{y}_{\ell }, e}\right)  = 0 \) in \( {H}^{ * }\left( {B;R}\right) \) then has the form \( g\left( {{y}_{1},\cdots ,{y}_{\ell }, e}\right)  \smile  e = 0 \) . Since \( \smile  e \) is injective, this gives a polynomial relation \( g\left( {{y}_{1},\cdots ,{y}_{\ell }, e}\right)  = 0 \) with \( g \) having lower degree than \( f \) . By induction we deduce that \( g \) must be the zero polynomial, hence also \( f \) .

Example 4D.12. Let us apply this to give another proof that \( {H}^{ * }\left( {{G}_{n}\left( {\mathbb{C}}^{\infty }\right) ;\mathbb{Z}}\right) \) is a polynomial ring \( \mathbb{Z}\left\lbrack  {{c}_{1},\cdots ,{c}_{n}}\right\rbrack \) with \( \left| {c}_{i}\right|  = {2i} \) . We use two fiber bundles:

\[
{S}^{{2n} - 1} \rightarrow  E \rightarrow  {G}_{n}\left( {\mathbb{C}}^{\infty }\right) \;{S}^{\infty } \rightarrow  E \rightarrow  {G}_{n - 1}\left( {\mathbb{C}}^{\infty }\right)
\]

The total space \( E \) in both cases is the space of pairs \( \left( {P, v}\right) \) where \( P \) is an \( n \) -plane in \( {\mathbb{C}}^{\infty } \) and \( v \) is a unit vector in \( P \) . In the first bundle the map \( E \rightarrow  {G}_{n}\left( {\mathbb{C}}^{\infty }\right) \) is \( \left( {P, v}\right)  \mapsto  P \) , with fiber \( {S}^{{2n} - 1} \) , and for the second bundle the map \( E \rightarrow  {G}_{n - 1}\left( {\mathbb{C}}^{\infty }\right) \) sends \( \left( {P, v}\right) \) to the \( \left( {n - 1}\right) \) -plane in \( P \) orthogonal to \( v \) , with fiber \( {S}^{\infty } \) consisting of all the unit vectors in \( {\mathbb{C}}^{\infty } \) orthogonal to a given \( \left( {n - 1}\right) \) -plane. Local triviality for the two bundles is verified in the usual way. Since \( {S}^{\infty } \) is contractible, the map \( E \rightarrow  {G}_{n - 1}\left( {\mathbb{C}}^{\infty }\right) \) induces isomorphisms on homotopy groups, hence also on cohomology. By induction on \( n \) we then have \( {H}^{ * }\left( {E;\mathbb{Z}}\right)  \approx  \mathbb{Z}\left\lbrack  {{c}_{1},\cdots ,{c}_{n - 1}}\right\rbrack \) . The first bundle is orientable since \( {G}_{n}\left( {\mathbb{C}}^{\infty }\right) \) is simply-connected, so the proposition gives \( {H}^{ * }\left( {{G}_{n}\left( {\mathbb{C}}^{\infty }\right) ;\mathbb{Z}}\right)  \approx  \mathbb{Z}\left\lbrack  {{c}_{1},\cdots ,{c}_{n}}\right\rbrack \) for \( {c}_{n} = e \) .

The same argument works in the quaternionic case. For a version of this argument in the real case see §3.3 of [VBKT].

Before giving our next example, let us observe that the Gysin sequence with a fixed coefficient ring \( R \) is valid for any orientable fiber bundle \( F \rightarrow  E\overset{p - }{ \rightarrow  }B \) whose fiber is a CW complex \( F \) with \( {H}^{ * }\left( {F;R}\right)  \approx  {H}^{ * }\left( {{S}^{n - 1};R}\right) \) . Orientability is defined just as before in terms of induced maps \( {L}_{y}^{ * } : {H}^{n - 1}\left( {F;R}\right)  \rightarrow  {H}^{n - 1}\left( {F;R}\right) \) . No changes are needed in the derivation of the Gysin sequence to get this more general case, if the associated ’disk’ bundle is again taken to be the mapping cylinder \( {CF} \rightarrow  {M}_{p} \rightarrow  B \) .

Example 4D.13. We have computed the cohomology of \( {\widetilde{G}}_{n}\left( {\mathbb{R}}^{\infty }\right) \) with \( {\mathbb{Z}}_{2} \) coefficients, finding it to be a polynomial ring on generators in dimensions 2 through \( n \) , and now we compute the cohomology with \( {\mathbb{Z}}_{p} \) coefficients for \( p \) an odd prime. The answer will again be a polynomial algebra, but this time on even-dimensional generators, depending on the parity of \( n \) . Consider first the case that \( n \) is odd, say \( n = {2k} + 1 \) . There are two fiber bundles

\[
{V}_{2}\left( {\mathbb{R}}^{{2k} + 1}\right)  \rightarrow  E \rightarrow  {\widetilde{G}}_{{2k} + 1}\left( {\mathbb{R}}^{\infty }\right) \;{V}_{2}\left( {\mathbb{R}}^{\infty }\right)  \rightarrow  E \rightarrow  {\widetilde{G}}_{{2k} - 1}\left( {\mathbb{R}}^{\infty }\right)
\]

where \( E \) is the space of triples \( \left( {P,{v}_{1},{v}_{2}}\right) \) with \( P \) an oriented \( \left( {{2k} + 1}\right) \) -plane in \( {\mathbb{R}}^{\infty } \) and \( {v}_{1} \) and \( {v}_{2} \) two orthogonal unit vectors in \( P \) . The projection map in the first bundle is \( \left( {P,{v}_{1},{v}_{2}}\right)  \mapsto  P \) , and for the second bundle the projection sends \( \left( {P,{v}_{1},{v}_{2}}\right) \) to the oriented \( \left( {{2k} - 1}\right) \) -plane in \( P \) orthogonal to \( {v}_{1} \) and \( {v}_{2} \) , with the orientation specified by saying for example that \( {v}_{1},{v}_{2} \) followed by a positively oriented basis for the orthogonal \( \left( {{2k} - 1}\right) \) -plane is a positively oriented basis for \( P \) . Both bundles are orientable since their base spaces \( {\widetilde{G}}_{n}\left( {\mathbb{R}}^{\infty }\right) \) are simply-connected, from the bundle \( {SO}\left( n\right)  \rightarrow  {V}_{n}\left( {\mathbb{R}}^{\infty }\right)  \rightarrow  {\widetilde{G}}_{n}\left( {\mathbb{R}}^{\infty }\right) . \)

The fiber \( {V}_{2}\left( {\mathbb{R}}^{\infty }\right) \) of the second bundle is contractible, so \( E \) has the same cohomology as \( {\widetilde{G}}_{{2k} - 1}\left( {\mathbb{R}}^{\infty }\right) \) . The fiber of the first bundle has the same \( {\mathbb{Z}}_{p} \) cohomology as \( {S}^{{4k} - 1} \) if \( p \) is odd, by the calculation at the end of §3.D. So if we assume inductively that \( {H}^{ * }\left( {{\widetilde{G}}_{{2k} - 1}\left( {\mathbb{R}}^{\infty }\right) ;{\mathbb{Z}}_{p}}\right)  \approx  {\mathbb{Z}}_{p}\left\lbrack  {{p}_{1},\cdots ,{p}_{k - 1}}\right\rbrack \) with \( \left| {p}_{i}\right|  = {4i} \) , then Proposition 4D.11 above implies that \( {H}^{ * }\left( {{\widetilde{G}}_{{2k} + 1}\left( {\mathbb{R}}^{\infty }\right) ;{\mathbb{Z}}_{p}}\right)  \approx  {\mathbb{Z}}_{p}\left\lbrack  {{p}_{1},\cdots ,{p}_{k}}\right\rbrack \) where \( {p}_{k} = e \) has dimension \( {4k} \) . The induction can start with \( {\widetilde{G}}_{1}\left( {\mathbb{R}}^{\infty }\right) \) which is just \( {S}^{\infty } \) since an oriented line in \( {\mathbb{R}}^{\infty } \) contains a unique unit vector in the positive direction.

To handle the case of \( {\widetilde{G}}_{n}\left( {\mathbb{R}}^{\infty }\right) \) with \( n = {2k} \) even, we proceed just as in Example 4D.12, considering the bundles

\[
{S}^{{2k} - 1} \rightarrow  E \rightarrow  {\widetilde{G}}_{2k}\left( {\mathbb{R}}^{\infty }\right) \;{S}^{\infty } \rightarrow  E \rightarrow  {\widetilde{G}}_{{2k} - 1}\left( {\mathbb{R}}^{\infty }\right)
\]

By the case \( n \) odd we have \( {H}^{ * }\left( {{\widetilde{G}}_{{2k} - 1}\left( {\mathbb{R}}^{\infty }\right) ;{\mathbb{Z}}_{p}}\right)  \approx  {\mathbb{Z}}_{p}\left\lbrack  {{p}_{1},\cdots ,{p}_{k - 1}}\right\rbrack \) with \( \left| {p}_{i}\right|  = {4i} \) , so the corollary implies that \( {H}^{ * }\left( {{\widetilde{G}}_{2k}\left( {\mathbb{R}}^{\infty }\right) ;{\mathbb{Z}}_{p}}\right) \) is a polynomial ring on these generators and also a generator in dimension \( {2k} \) .

Summarizing, for \( p \) an odd prime we have shown:

\[
{H}^{ * }\left( {{\widetilde{G}}_{{2k} + 1}\left( {\mathbb{R}}^{\infty }\right) ;{\mathbb{Z}}_{p}}\right)  \approx  {\mathbb{Z}}_{p}\left\lbrack  {{p}_{1},\cdots ,{p}_{k}}\right\rbrack  ,\;\left| {p}_{i}\right|  = {4i}
\]

\[
{H}^{ * }\left( {{\widetilde{G}}_{2k}\left( {\mathbb{R}}^{\infty }\right) ;{\mathbb{Z}}_{p}}\right)  \approx  {\mathbb{Z}}_{p}\left\lbrack  {{p}_{1},\cdots ,{p}_{k - 1}, e}\right\rbrack  ,\;\left| {p}_{i}\right|  = {4i},\;\left| e\right|  = {2k}
\]

The same result holds also with \( \mathbb{Q} \) coefficients. In fact, our proof applies for any coefficient ring in which 2 has a multiplicative inverse, since all that is needed is that \( {H}^{ * }\left( {{V}_{2}\left( {\mathbb{R}}^{{2k} + 1}\right) ;R}\right)  \approx  {H}^{ * }\left( {{S}^{{4k} - 1};R}\right) \) . For a calculation of the cohomology of \( {\widetilde{G}}_{n}\left( {\mathbb{R}}^{\infty }\right) \) with \( \mathbb{Z} \) coefficients, see [VBKT]. It turns out that all torsion elements have order 2, and modulo this torsion the integral cohomology is again a polynomial ring on the generators \( {p}_{i} \) and \( e \) . Similar results hold also for the cohomology of the unoriented Grassmann manifold \( {G}_{n}\left( {\mathbb{R}}^{\infty }\right) \) , but with the generator \( e \) replaced by \( {p}_{k} \) when \( n = {2k} \) .
