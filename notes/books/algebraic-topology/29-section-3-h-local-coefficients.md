# 3.H Local Coefficients

Homology and cohomology with local coefficients are fancier versions of ordinary homology and cohomology that can be defined for nonsimply-connected spaces. In various situations these more refined homology and cohomology theories arise naturally and inevitably. For example, the only way to extend Poincaré duality with \( \mathbb{Z} \) coefficients to nonorientable manifolds is to use local coefficients. In the overall scheme of algebraic topology, however, the role played by local coefficients is fairly small. Local coefficients bring an extra level of complication that one tries to avoid whenever possible. With this in mind, the goal of this section will not be to give a full exposition but rather just to sketch the main ideas, leaving the technical details for the interested reader to fill in.

The plan for this section is first to give the quick algebraic definition of homology and cohomology with local coefficients, and then to reinterpret this definition more geometrically in a way that looks more like ordinary homology and cohomology. The reinterpretation also allows the familiar properties of homology and cohomology to be extended to the local coefficient case with very little effort.

## Local Coefficients via Modules

Let \( X \) be a path-connected space having a universal cover \( \widetilde{X} \) and fundamental group \( \pi \) , so that \( X \) is the quotient of \( \widetilde{X} \) by the action of \( \pi \) by deck transformations \( \widetilde{x} \mapsto  \gamma  \cdot  \widetilde{x} \) for \( \gamma  \in  \pi \) and \( \widetilde{x} \in  \widetilde{X} \) . The action of \( \pi \) on \( \widetilde{X} \) induces an action of \( \pi \) on the group \( {C}_{n}\left( \widetilde{X}\right) \) of singular \( n \) -chains in \( \widetilde{X} \) , by sending a singular \( n \) -simplex \( \sigma  : {\Delta }^{n} \rightarrow  \widetilde{X} \) to the composition \( {\Delta }^{n}\overset{\sigma }{ \rightarrow  }\widetilde{X}\overset{y}{ \rightarrow  }\widetilde{X} \) . The action of \( \pi \) on \( {C}_{n}\left( \widetilde{X}\right) \) makes \( {C}_{n}\left( \widetilde{X}\right) \) a module over the group ring \( \mathbb{Z}\left\lbrack  \pi \right\rbrack \) , which consists of the finite formal sums \( \mathop{\sum }\limits_{i}{m}_{i}{\gamma }_{i} \) with \( {m}_{i} \in  \mathbb{Z} \) and \( {\gamma }_{i} \in  \pi \) , with the natural addition \( \mathop{\sum }\limits_{i}{m}_{i}{\gamma }_{i} + \mathop{\sum }\limits_{i}{n}_{i}{\gamma }_{i} = \; \mathop{\sum }\limits_{i}\left( {{m}_{i} + {n}_{i}}\right) {\gamma }_{i} \) and multiplication \( \left( {\mathop{\sum }\limits_{i}{m}_{i}{\gamma }_{i}}\right) \left( {\mathop{\sum }\limits_{j}{n}_{j}{\gamma }_{j}}\right)  = \mathop{\sum }\limits_{{i, j}}{m}_{i}{n}_{j}{\gamma }_{i}{\gamma }_{j} \) . The boundary maps \( \partial  : {C}_{n}\left( \widetilde{X}\right)  \rightarrow  {C}_{n - 1}\left( \widetilde{X}\right) \) are \( \mathbb{Z}\left\lbrack  \pi \right\rbrack \) -module homomorphisms since the action of \( \pi \) on these groups comes from an action on \( \widetilde{X} \) .

If \( M \) is an arbitrary module over \( \mathbb{Z}\left\lbrack  \pi \right\rbrack \) , we would like to define \( {C}_{n}\left( {X;M}\right) \) to be \( {C}_{n}\left( \widetilde{X}\right) { \otimes  }_{\mathbb{Z}\left\lbrack  \pi \right\rbrack  }M \) , but for tensor products over a noncommutative ring one has to be a little careful with left and right module structures. In general, if \( R \) is a ring, possibly noncommutative, one defines the tensor product \( A{ \otimes  }_{R}B \) of a right \( R \) -module \( A \) and a left \( R \) -module \( B \) to be the abelian group with generators \( a \otimes  b \) for \( a \in  A \) and \( b \in  B \) , subject to distributivity and associativity relations:

(i) \( \left( {{a}_{1} + {a}_{2}}\right)  \otimes  b = {a}_{1} \otimes  b + {a}_{2} \otimes  b \) and \( a \otimes  \left( {{b}_{1} + {b}_{2}}\right)  = a \otimes  {b}_{1} + a \otimes  {b}_{2} \) .

(ii) \( {ar} \otimes  b = a \otimes  {rb} \) .

In case \( R = \mathbb{Z}\left\lbrack  \pi \right\rbrack \) , a left \( \mathbb{Z}\left\lbrack  \pi \right\rbrack \) -module \( A \) can be regarded as a right \( \mathbb{Z}\left\lbrack  \pi \right\rbrack \) -module by setting \( {a\gamma } = {\gamma }^{-1}a \) for \( \gamma  \in  \pi \) . So the tensor product of two left \( \mathbb{Z}\left\lbrack  \pi \right\rbrack \) -modules \( A \) and \( B \) is defined, and the relation \( {a\gamma } \otimes  b = a \otimes  {\gamma b} \) becomes \( {\gamma }^{-1}a \otimes  b = a \otimes  {\gamma b} \) , or equivalently \( {a}^{\prime } \otimes  b = \gamma {a}^{\prime } \otimes  {\gamma b} \) where \( {a}^{\prime } = {\gamma }^{-1}a \) . Thus tensoring over \( \mathbb{Z}\left\lbrack  \pi \right\rbrack \) has the effect of factoring out the action of \( \pi \) . To simplify notation we shall write \( A{ \otimes  }_{\mathbb{Z}\left\lbrack  \pi \right\rbrack  }B \) as \( A{ \otimes  }_{\pi }B \) , emphasizing the fact that the essential part of a \( \mathbb{Z}\left\lbrack  \pi \right\rbrack \) -module structure is the action of \( \pi \) .

In particular, \( {C}_{n}\left( \widetilde{X}\right) { \otimes  }_{\pi }M \) is defined if \( M \) is a left \( \mathbb{Z}\left\lbrack  \pi \right\rbrack \) -module. These chain groups \( {C}_{n}\left( {X;M}\right)  = {C}_{n}\left( \widetilde{X}\right) { \otimes  }_{\pi }M \) form a chain complex with the boundary maps \( \partial  \otimes  \mathbb{1} \) . The homology groups \( {H}_{n}\left( {X;M}\right) \) of this chain complex are by definition homology groups with local coefficients.

For cohomology one can set \( {C}^{n}\left( {X;M}\right)  = {\operatorname{Hom}}_{\mathbb{Z}\left\lbrack  \pi \right\rbrack  }\left( {{C}_{n}\left( \widetilde{X}\right) , M}\right) \) , the \( \mathbb{Z}\left\lbrack  \pi \right\rbrack \) -module homomorphisms \( {C}_{n}\left( \widetilde{X}\right)  \rightarrow  M \) . These groups \( {C}^{n}\left( {X;M}\right) \) form a cochain complex whose cohomology groups \( {H}^{n}\left( {X;M}\right) \) are cohomology groups with local coefficients.

Example 3H.1. Let us check that when \( M \) is a trivial \( \mathbb{Z}\left\lbrack  \pi \right\rbrack \) -module, with \( {ym} = m \) for all \( y \in  \pi \) and \( m \in  M \) , then \( {H}_{n}\left( {X;M}\right) \) is just ordinary homology with coefficients in the abelian group \( M \) . For a singular \( n \) -simplex \( \sigma  : {\Delta }^{n} \rightarrow  X \) , the various lifts \( \widetilde{\sigma } : {\Delta }^{n} \rightarrow  \widetilde{X} \) form an orbit of the action of \( \pi \) on \( {C}_{n}\left( \widetilde{X}\right) \) . In \( {C}_{n}\left( \widetilde{X}\right) { \otimes  }_{\pi }M \) all these lifts are identified via the relation \( \widetilde{\sigma } \otimes  m = \gamma \widetilde{\sigma } \otimes  {\gamma m} = \gamma \widetilde{\sigma } \otimes  m \) . Thus we can identify \( {C}_{n}\left( \widetilde{X}\right) { \otimes  }_{\pi }M \) with \( {C}_{n}\left( X\right)  \otimes  M \) , the chain group denoted \( {C}_{n}\left( {X;M}\right) \) in ordinary homology theory, so \( {H}_{n}\left( {X;M}\right) \) reduces to ordinary homology with coefficients in \( M \) . The analogous statement for cohomology is also true since elements of \( {\operatorname{Hom}}_{\mathbb{Z}\left\lbrack  \pi \right\rbrack  }\left( {{C}_{n}\left( \widetilde{X}\right) , M}\right) \) are functions from singular \( n \) -simplices \( \widetilde{\sigma } : {\Delta }^{n} \rightarrow  \widetilde{X} \) to \( M \) taking the same value on all elements of a \( \pi \) -orbit since the action of \( \pi \) on \( M \) is trivial, so \( {\operatorname{Hom}}_{\mathbb{Z}\left\lbrack  \pi \right\rbrack  }\left( {{C}_{n}\left( \widetilde{X}\right) , M}\right) \) is identifiable with \( \operatorname{Hom}\left( {{C}_{n}\left( X\right) , M}\right) \) , ordinary cochains with coefficients in \( M \) .

Example 3H.2. Suppose we take \( M = \mathbb{Z}\left\lbrack  \pi \right\rbrack \) , viewed as a module over itself via its ring structure. For a ring \( R \) with identity element, \( A{ \otimes  }_{R}R \) is naturally isomorphic to \( A \) via the correspondence \( a \otimes  r \mapsto  {ar} \) . So we have a natural identification of \( {C}_{n}\left( \widetilde{X}\right) { \otimes  }_{\pi }\mathbb{Z}\left\lbrack  \pi \right\rbrack \) with \( {C}_{n}\left( \widetilde{X}\right) \) , and hence an isomorphism \( {H}_{n}\left( {X;\mathbb{Z}\left\lbrack  \pi \right\rbrack  }\right)  \approx  {H}_{n}\left( \widetilde{X}\right) \) . Generalizing this, let \( {X}^{\prime } \rightarrow  X \) be the cover corresponding to a subgroup \( {\pi }^{\prime } \subset  \pi \) . Then the free abelian group \( \mathbb{Z}\left\lbrack  {\pi /{\pi }^{\prime }}\right\rbrack \) with basis the cosets \( y{\pi }^{\prime } \) is a \( \mathbb{Z}\left\lbrack  \pi \right\rbrack \) -module and \( {C}_{n}\left( \widetilde{X}\right) { \otimes  }_{\mathbb{Z}\left\lbrack  \pi \right\rbrack  }\mathbb{Z}\left\lbrack  {\pi /{\pi }^{\prime }}\right\rbrack   \approx  {C}_{n}\left( {X}^{\prime }\right) \) , so \( {H}_{n}\left( {X;\mathbb{Z}\left\lbrack  {\pi /{\pi }^{\prime }}\right\rbrack  }\right)  \approx  {H}_{n}\left( {X}^{\prime }\right) \) . More generally, if \( A \) is an abelian group then \( A\left\lbrack  {\pi /{\pi }^{\prime }}\right\rbrack \) is a \( \mathbb{Z}\left\lbrack  \pi \right\rbrack \) -module and \( {H}_{n}\left( {X;A\left\lbrack  {\pi /{\pi }^{\prime }}\right\rbrack  }\right)  \approx  {H}_{n}\left( {{X}^{\prime };A}\right) \) . So homology of covering spaces is a special case of homology with local coefficients. The corresponding assertions for cohomology are not true, however, as we shall see later in the section.

For a \( \mathbb{Z}\left\lbrack  \pi \right\rbrack \) -module \( M \) , let \( {\pi }^{\prime } \) be the kernel of the homomorphism \( \rho  : \pi  \rightarrow  \operatorname{Aut}\left( M\right) \) defining the module structure, given by \( \rho \left( \gamma \right) \left( m\right)  = {ym} \) , where \( \operatorname{Aut}\left( M\right) \) is the group of automorphisms of the abelian group \( M \) . If \( {X}^{\prime } \rightarrow  X \) is the cover corresponding to the normal subgroup \( {\pi }^{\prime } \) of \( \pi \) , then \( {C}_{n}\left( \widetilde{X}\right) { \otimes  }_{\pi }M \approx  {C}_{n}\left( {X}^{\prime }\right) { \otimes  }_{\pi }M \approx  {C}_{n}\left( {X}^{\prime }\right) { \otimes  }_{\mathbb{Z}\left\lbrack  {\pi /{\pi }^{\prime }}\right\rbrack  }M \) . This gives a more efficient description of \( {H}_{n}\left( {X;M}\right) \) .

Example 3H.3. As a special case, suppose that we take \( M = \mathbb{Z} \) , so \( \operatorname{Aut}\left( \mathbb{Z}\right)  \approx  {\mathbb{Z}}_{2} = \{  \pm  1\} \) . For a nontrivial \( \mathbb{Z}\left\lbrack  \pi \right\rbrack \) -module structure on \( M,{\pi }^{\prime } \) is a subgroup of index 2 and \( {X}^{\prime } \rightarrow  X \) is a 2-sheeted covering space. If \( \tau \) is the nontrivial deck transformation of \( {X}^{\prime } \) , let \( {C}_{n}^{ + }\left( {X}^{\prime }\right)  = \left\{  {\alpha  \in  {C}_{n}\left( {X}^{\prime }\right)  \mid  {\tau }_{\sharp }\left( \alpha \right)  = \alpha }\right\} \) and \( {C}_{n}^{ - }\left( {X}^{\prime }\right)  = \left\{  {\alpha  \in  {C}_{n}\left( {X}^{\prime }\right)  \mid  {\tau }_{\sharp }\left( \alpha \right)  =  - \alpha }\right\} \) . It follows easily that \( {C}_{n}^{ \pm  }\left( {X}^{\prime }\right) \) has basis the chains \( \sigma  \pm  {\tau \sigma } \) for \( \sigma  : {\Delta }^{n} \rightarrow  {X}^{\prime } \) , and we have short exact sequences

\[
0 \rightarrow  {C}_{n}^{ - }\left( {X}^{\prime }\right)  \hookrightarrow  {C}_{n}\left( {X}^{\prime }\right) \overset{\sum }{ \rightarrow  }{C}_{n}^{ + }\left( {X}^{\prime }\right)  \rightarrow  0
\]

\[
0 \rightarrow  {C}_{n}^{ + }\left( {X}^{\prime }\right)  \hookrightarrow  {C}_{n}\left( {X}^{\prime }\right) \overset{\Delta }{ \rightarrow  }{C}_{n}^{ - }\left( {X}^{\prime }\right)  \rightarrow  0
\]

where \( \sum \left( \alpha \right)  = \alpha  + {\tau }_{\sharp }\left( \alpha \right) \) and \( \Delta \left( \alpha \right)  = \alpha  - {\tau }_{\sharp }\left( \alpha \right) \) . The homomorphism \( {C}_{n}\left( X\right)  \rightarrow  {C}_{n}^{ + }\left( {X}^{\prime }\right) \) sending a singular simplex in \( X \) to the sum of its two lifts to \( {X}^{\prime } \) is an isomorphism. The quotient map \( {C}_{n}\left( {X}^{\prime }\right)  \rightarrow  {C}_{n}\left( {X}^{\prime }\right) { \otimes  }_{\pi }\mathbb{Z} \) has kernel \( {C}_{n}^{ + }\left( {X}^{\prime }\right) \) , so the second short exact sequence gives an isomorphism \( {C}_{n}^{ - }\left( {X}^{\prime }\right)  \approx  {C}_{n}\left( {X}^{\prime }\right) { \otimes  }_{\pi }\mathbb{Z} \) . These isomorphisms are isomorphisms of chain complexes and the short exact sequences are short exact sequence of chain complexes, so from the first short exact sequence we get a long exact sequence of homology groups

\[
\cdots  \rightarrow  {H}_{n}\left( {X;\widetilde{\mathbb{Z}}}\right)  \rightarrow  {H}_{n}\left( {X}^{\prime }\right) \overset{{p}_{ * }}{ \rightarrow  }{H}_{n}\left( X\right)  \rightarrow  {H}_{n - 1}\left( {X;\widetilde{\mathbb{Z}}}\right)  \rightarrow  \cdots
\]

where the symbol \( \widetilde{\mathbb{Z}} \) indicates local coefficients in the module \( \mathbb{Z} \) and \( {p}_{ * } \) is induced by the covering projection \( p : {X}^{\prime } \rightarrow  X \) .

Let us apply this exact sequence when \( X \) is a nonorientable \( n \) -manifold \( M \) which is closed and connected. We shall use terminology and notation from §3.3. We can view \( \mathbb{Z} \) as a \( \mathbb{Z}\left\lbrack  {{\pi }_{1}M}\right\rbrack \) -module by letting a loop \( \gamma \) in \( M \) act on \( \mathbb{Z} \) by multiplication by +1 or -1 according to whether \( \gamma \) preserves or reverses local orientations of \( M \) . The double cover \( {X}^{\prime } \rightarrow  X \) is then the 2-sheeted cover \( \widetilde{M} \rightarrow  M \) with \( \widetilde{M} \) orientable. The nonorientability of \( M \) implies that \( {H}_{n}\left( M\right)  = 0 \) . Since \( {H}_{n + 1}\left( M\right)  = 0 \) , the exact sequence above then gives \( {H}_{n}\left( {M;\widetilde{\mathbb{Z}}}\right)  \approx  {H}_{n}\left( \widetilde{M}\right)  \approx  \mathbb{Z} \) . This can be interpreted as saying that by taking homology with local coefficients we obtain a fundamental class for a nonorientable manifold.

## Local Coefficients via Bundles of Groups

Now we wish to reinterpret homology and cohomology with local coefficients in more geometric terms, making it look more like ordinary homology and cohomology.

Let us first define a special kind of covering space with extra algebraic structure. A bundle of groups is a map \( p : E \rightarrow  X \) together with a group structure on each subset \( {p}^{-1}\left( x\right) \) , such that all these groups \( {p}^{-1}\left( x\right) \) are isomorphic to a fixed group \( G \) in the following special way: Each point of \( X \) has a neighborhood \( U \) for which there exists a homeomorphism \( {h}_{U} : {p}^{-1}\left( U\right)  \rightarrow  U \times  G \) taking each \( {p}^{-1}\left( x\right) \) to \( \{ x\}  \times  G \) by a group isomorphism. Since \( G \) is given the discrete topology, the projection \( p \) is a covering space. Borrowing terminology from the theory of fiber bundles, the subsets \( {p}^{-1}\left( x\right) \) are called the fibers of \( p : E \rightarrow  X \) , and one speaks of \( E \) as a bundle of groups with fiber \( G \) . It may be worth remarking that if we modify the definition by replacing the word 'group' with 'vector space' throughout, then we obtain the much more common notion of a vector bundle; see [VBKT].

Trivial examples are provided by products \( E = X \times  G \) . Nontrivial examples we have considered are the covering spaces \( {M}_{\mathbb{Z}} \rightarrow  M \) of nonorientable manifolds \( M \) defined in §3.3. Here the group \( G \) is the homology coefficient group \( \mathbb{Z} \) , though one could equally well define a bundle of groups \( {M}_{G} \rightarrow  M \) for any abelian coefficient group \( G \) .

Homology groups of \( X \) with coefficients in a bundle \( E \) of abelian groups may be defined as follows. Consider finite sums \( \mathop{\sum }\limits_{i}{n}_{i}{\sigma }_{i} \) where each \( {\sigma }_{i} : {\Delta }^{n} \rightarrow  X \) is a singular \( n \) -simplex in \( X \) and \( {n}_{i} : {\Delta }^{n} \rightarrow  E \) is a lifting of \( {\sigma }_{i} \) . The sum of two lifts \( {n}_{i} \) and \( {m}_{i} \) of the same \( {\sigma }_{i} \) is defined by \( \left( {{n}_{i} + {m}_{i}}\right) \left( s\right)  = {n}_{i}\left( s\right)  + {m}_{i}\left( s\right) \) , and is also a lift of \( {\sigma }_{i} \) . In this way the finite sums \( \mathop{\sum }\limits_{i}{n}_{i}{\sigma }_{i} \) form an abelian group \( {C}_{n}\left( {X;E}\right) \) , provided we allow the deletion of terms \( {n}_{i}{\sigma }_{i} \) when \( {n}_{i} \) is the zero-valued lift. A boundary homomorphism \( \partial  : {C}_{n}\left( {X;E}\right)  \rightarrow  {C}_{n - 1}\left( {X;E}\right) \) is defined by the formula \( \partial \left( {\mathop{\sum }\limits_{i}{n}_{i}{\sigma }_{i}}\right)  = \; \mathop{\sum }\limits_{{i, j}}{\left( -1\right) }^{j}{n}_{i}{\sigma }_{i} \mid  \left\lbrack  {{v}_{0},\cdots ,{\widehat{v}}_{j},\cdots ,{v}_{n}}\right\rbrack \) where ’ \( {n}_{i} \) ’ in the right side of the equation means the restricted lifting \( {n}_{i} \mid  \left\lbrack  {{v}_{0},\cdots ,{\widehat{v}}_{j},\cdots ,{v}_{n}}\right\rbrack \) . The proof that the usual boundary homomorphism \( \partial \) satisfies \( {\partial }^{2} = 0 \) still works in the present context, so the groups \( {C}_{n}\left( {X;E}\right) \) form a chain complex. We denote the homology groups of this chain complex by \( {H}_{n}\left( {X;E}\right) \) .

In case \( E \) is the product bundle \( X \times  G \) , lifts \( {n}_{i} \) are simply elements of \( G \) , so \( {H}_{n}\left( {X;E}\right)  = {H}_{n}\left( {X;G}\right) \) , ordinary homology. In the general case, lifts \( {n}_{i} : {\Delta }^{n} \rightarrow  E \) are uniquely determined by their value at one point \( s \in  {\Delta }^{n} \) , and these values can be specified arbitrarily since \( {\Delta }^{n} \) is simply-connected, so the \( {n}_{i} \) ’s can be thought of as elements of \( {p}^{-1}\left( {{\sigma }_{i}\left( s\right) }\right) \) , a group isomorphic to \( G \) . However if \( E \) is not a product, there is no canonical isomorphism between different fibers \( {p}^{-1}\left( x\right) \) , so one cannot identify \( {H}_{n}\left( {X;E}\right) \) with ordinary homology.

An alternative approach would be to take the coefficients \( {n}_{i} \) to be elements of the fiber group over a specific point of \( {\sigma }_{i}\left( {\Delta }^{n}\right) \) , say \( {\sigma }_{i}\left( {v}_{0}\right) \) . However, with such a definition the formula for the boundary operator \( \partial \) becomes more complicated since there is no point of \( {\Delta }^{n} \) that lies in all the faces.

Our task now is to relate the homology groups \( {H}_{n}\left( {X;E}\right) \) to homology groups with coefficients in a module, as defined earlier. In \( \text{ § }{1.3} \) we described how covering spaces of \( X \) with a given fiber \( F \) can be classified in terms of actions of \( {\pi }_{1}\left( X\right) \) on \( F \) , assuming \( X \) is path-connected and has the local properties guaranteeing the existence of a universal cover. It is easy to check that covering spaces that are bundles of groups with fiber a group \( G \) are equivalent to actions of \( {\pi }_{1}\left( X\right) \) on \( G \) by automorphisms of \( G \) , that is, homomorphisms from \( {\pi }_{1}\left( X\right) \) to \( \operatorname{Aut}\left( G\right) \) .

For example, for the bundle \( {M}_{\mathbb{Z}} \rightarrow  M \) the action of a loop \( \gamma \) on the fiber \( \mathbb{Z} \) is multiplication by \( \pm  1 \) according to whether \( \gamma \) preserves or reverses orientation in \( M \) , that is, whether \( \gamma \) lifts to a closed loop in the orientable double cover \( \widetilde{M} \rightarrow  M \) or not. As another example, the action of \( {\pi }_{1}\left( X\right) \) on itself by inner automorphisms corresponds to a bundle of groups \( p : E \rightarrow  X \) with fibers \( {p}^{-1}\left( x\right)  = {\pi }_{1}\left( {X, x}\right) \) . This example is rather similar in spirit to the examples \( {M}_{\mathbb{Z}} \rightarrow  M \) . In both cases one has a functor associating a group to each point of a space, and all the groups at different points are isomorphic, but not canonically so. Different choices of isomorphisms are obtained by choosing different paths between two points, and loops give rise to an action of \( {\pi }_{1} \) on the fibers.

In the case of bundles of groups \( p : E \rightarrow  X \) whose fiber \( G \) is abelian, an action of \( {\pi }_{1}\left( X\right) \) on \( G \) by automorphisms is the same as a \( \mathbb{Z}\left\lbrack  {{\pi }_{1}X}\right\rbrack \) -module structure on \( G \) .

Proposition 3H.4. If \( X \) is a path-connected space having a universal covering space, then the groups \( {H}_{n}\left( {X;E}\right) \) are naturally isomorphic to the homology groups \( {H}_{n}\left( {X;G}\right) \) with local coefficients in the \( \mathbb{Z}\left\lbrack  \pi \right\rbrack \) -module \( G \) associated to \( E \) , where \( \pi  = {\pi }_{1}\left( X\right) \) .

Proof: As noted earlier, a bundle of groups \( E \rightarrow  X \) with fiber \( G \) is equivalent to an action of \( \pi \) on \( G \) . In more explicit terms this means that if \( \widetilde{X} \) is the universal cover of \( X \) , then \( E \) is identifiable with the quotient of \( \widetilde{X} \times  G \) by the diagonal action of \( \pi ,\gamma \left( {\widetilde{x}, g}\right)  = \left( {\gamma \widetilde{x},{\gamma g}}\right) \) where the action in the first coordinate is by deck transformations of \( \widetilde{X} \) . For a chain \( \mathop{\sum }\limits_{i}{n}_{i}{\sigma }_{i} \in  {C}_{n}\left( {X;E}\right) \) , the coefficient \( {n}_{i} \) gives a lift of \( {\sigma }_{i} \) to \( E \) , and \( {n}_{i} \) in turn has various lifts to \( \widetilde{X} \times  G \) . Thus we have natural surjec-tions \( {C}_{n}\left( {\widetilde{X} \times  G}\right)  \rightarrow  {C}_{n}\left( E\right)  \rightarrow  {C}_{n}\left( {X;E}\right) \) expressing each of these groups as a quotient of the preceding one. More precisely, identifying \( {C}_{n}\left( {\widetilde{X} \times  G}\right) \) with \( {C}_{n}\left( \widetilde{X}\right)  \otimes  \mathbb{Z}\left\lbrack  G\right\rbrack \) in the obvious way, then \( {C}_{n}\left( E\right) \) is the quotient of \( {C}_{n}\left( \widetilde{X}\right)  \otimes  \mathbb{Z}\left\lbrack  G\right\rbrack \) under the identifications \( \widetilde{\sigma } \otimes  g \sim  \gamma  \cdot  \widetilde{\sigma } \otimes  \gamma  \cdot  g \) . This quotient is the tensor product \( {C}_{n}\left( \widetilde{X}\right) { \otimes  }_{\pi }\mathbb{Z}\left\lbrack  G\right\rbrack \) . To pass to the quotient \( {C}_{n}\left( {X;E}\right) \) of \( {C}_{n}\left( E\right)  = {C}_{n}\left( \widetilde{X}\right) { \otimes  }_{\pi }\mathbb{Z}\left\lbrack  G\right\rbrack \) we need to take into account the sum operation in \( {C}_{n}\left( {X;E}\right) \) , addition of lifts \( {n}_{i} : {\Delta }^{n} \rightarrow  E \) . This means that in sums \( \widetilde{\sigma } \otimes  {g}_{1} + \widetilde{\sigma } \otimes  {g}_{2} = \widetilde{\sigma } \otimes  \left( {{g}_{1} + {g}_{2}}\right) \) , the term \( {g}_{1} + {g}_{2} \) should be interpreted not in \( \mathbb{Z}\left\lbrack  G\right\rbrack \) but in the natural quotient \( G \) of \( \mathbb{Z}\left\lbrack  G\right\rbrack \) . Hence \( {C}_{n}\left( {X;E}\right) \) is identified with the quotient \( {C}_{n}\left( \widetilde{X}\right) { \otimes  }_{\pi }G \) of \( {C}_{n}\left( \widetilde{X}\right) { \otimes  }_{\pi }\mathbb{Z}\left\lbrack  G\right\rbrack \) . This natural identification commutes with the boundary homomorphisms, so the homology groups are also identified.

More generally, if \( X \) has a number of path-components \( {X}_{\alpha } \) with universal covers \( {\widetilde{X}}_{\alpha } \) , then \( {C}_{n}\left( {X;E}\right)  = {\bigoplus }_{\alpha }\left( {{C}_{n}\left( {\widetilde{X}}_{\alpha }\right) { \otimes  }_{\mathbb{Z}\left\lbrack  {{\pi }_{1}\left( {X}_{\alpha }\right) }\right\rbrack  }G}\right) \) , so \( {H}_{n}\left( {X;E}\right) \) splits accordingly as a direct sum of the local coefficient homology groups for the path-components \( {X}_{\alpha } \) .

We turn now to the question of whether homology with local coefficients satisfies axioms similar to those for ordinary homology. The main novelty is with the behavior of induced homomorphisms. In order for a map \( f : X \rightarrow  {X}^{\prime } \) to induce a map on homology with local coefficients we must have bundles of groups \( E \rightarrow  X \) and \( {E}^{\prime } \rightarrow  {X}^{\prime } \) that are related in some way. The natural assumption to make is that there is a commutative diagram as at the right, such that \( \widetilde{f} \) restricts to a homomorphism in each fiber. With this hypothesis there is then a chain homomorphism \( {f}_{\sharp } : {C}_{n}\left( {X;E}\right)  \rightarrow  {C}_{n}\left( {{X}^{\prime };{E}^{\prime }}\right) \) obtained by composing singular simplices with \( f \) and their lifts with \( \widetilde{f} \) , hence there is an induced homomorphism \( {f}_{ * } : {H}_{n}\left( {X;E}\right)  \rightarrow  {H}_{n}\left( {{X}^{\prime };{E}^{\prime }}\right) \) . The fibers of \( E \) and \( {E}^{\prime } \) need not be isomorphic groups, so change-of-coefficient homomorphisms \( {H}_{n}\left( {X;{G}_{1}}\right)  \rightarrow  {H}_{n}\left( {X;{G}_{2}}\right) \) for ordinary homology are a special case. To avoid this extra complication we shall consider only the case that \( \widetilde{f} \) restricts to an isomorphism on each fiber. With this condition, a commutative diagram as above will be called a bundle map.

![bo_d7dtv0s91nqc73eq8nv0_340_1343_1454_247_185_0.jpg](images/bo_d7dtv0s91nqc73eq8nv0_340_1343_1454_247_185_0.jpg)

Here is a method for constructing bundle maps. Starting with a map \( f : X \rightarrow  {X}^{\prime } \) and a bundle of groups \( {p}^{\prime } : {E}^{\prime } \rightarrow  {X}^{\prime } \) , let

\[
E = \left\{  {\left( {x,{e}^{\prime }}\right)  \in  X \times  {E}^{\prime } \mid  f\left( x\right)  = {p}^{\prime }\left( {e}^{\prime }\right) }\right\}  .
\]

This fits into a commutative diagram as above if we define \( p\left( {x,{e}^{\prime }}\right)  = x \) and \( \widetilde{f}\left( {x,{e}^{\prime }}\right)  = \; {e}^{\prime } \) . In particular, the fiber \( {p}^{-1}\left( x\right) \) consists of pairs \( \left( {x,{e}^{\prime }}\right) \) with \( {p}^{\prime }\left( {e}^{\prime }\right)  = f\left( x\right) \) , so \( \widetilde{f} \) is a bijection of this fiber with the fiber of \( {E}^{\prime } \rightarrow  {X}^{\prime } \) over \( f\left( x\right) \) . We use this bijection to give \( {p}^{-1}\left( x\right) \) a group structure. To check that \( p : E \rightarrow  X \) is a bundle of groups, let \( {h}^{\prime } : {\left( {p}^{\prime }\right) }^{-1}\left( {U}^{\prime }\right)  \rightarrow  {U}^{\prime } \times  G \) be an isomorphism as in the definition of a bundle of groups. Define \( h : {p}^{-1}\left( U\right)  \rightarrow  U \times  G \) over \( U = {f}^{-1}\left( {U}^{\prime }\right) \) by \( h\left( {x,{e}^{\prime }}\right)  = \left( {x,{h}_{2}^{\prime }\left( {e}^{\prime }\right) }\right) \) where \( {h}_{2}^{\prime } \) is the second coordinate of \( {h}^{\prime } \) . An inverse for \( h \) is \( \left( {x, g}\right)  \in  \left( {x,{\left( {h}^{\prime }\right) }^{-1}\left( {f\left( x\right) , g}\right) }\right) \) , and \( h \) is clearly an isomorphism on each fiber. Thus \( p : E \rightarrow  X \) is a bundle of groups, called the pullback of \( {E}^{\prime } \rightarrow  {X}^{\prime } \) via \( f \) , or the induced bundle. The notation \( {f}^{ * }\left( {E}^{\prime }\right) \) is often used for the pullback bundle.

Given any bundle map \( E \rightarrow  {E}^{\prime } \) as in the diagram above, it is routine to check that the map \( E \rightarrow  {f}^{ * }\left( {E}^{\prime }\right) , e \mapsto  \left( {p\left( e\right) ,\widetilde{f}\left( e\right) }\right) \) , is an isomorphism of bundles over \( X \) , so the pullback construction produces all bundle maps. Thus we see one reason why homology with local coefficients is somewhat complicated: \( {H}_{n}\left( {X;E}\right) \) is really a functor of two variables, covariant in \( X \) and contravariant in \( E \) .

Viewing bundles of abelian groups over \( X \) as \( \mathbb{Z}\left\lbrack  {{\pi }_{1}X}\right\rbrack \) -modules, the pullback construction corresponds to making a \( \mathbb{Z}\left\lbrack  {{\pi }_{1}{X}^{\prime }}\right\rbrack \) -module into a \( \mathbb{Z}\left\lbrack  {{\pi }_{1}X}\right\rbrack \) -module by defining \( {\gamma g} = {f}_{ * }\left( \gamma \right) g \) for \( {f}_{ * } : {\pi }_{1}\left( X\right)  \rightarrow  {\pi }_{1}\left( {X}^{\prime }\right) \) . This follows easily from the definitions. In particular, this implies that homotopic maps \( {f}_{0},{f}_{1} : X \rightarrow  {X}^{\prime } \) induce isomorphic pullback bundles \( {f}_{0}^{ * }\left( {E}^{\prime }\right) ,{f}_{1}^{ * }\left( {E}^{\prime }\right) \) . Hence the map \( {f}_{ * } : {H}_{n}\left( {X;E}\right)  \rightarrow  {H}_{n}\left( {{X}^{\prime };{E}^{\prime }}\right) \) induced by a bundle map depends only on the homotopy class of \( f \) .

Generalizing the definition of \( {H}_{n}\left( {X;E}\right) \) to pairs \( \left( {X, A}\right) \) is straightforward, starting with the definition of \( {H}_{n}\left( {X, A;E}\right) \) as the \( {n}^{th} \) homology group of the chain complex of quotients \( {C}_{n}\left( {X;E}\right) /{C}_{n}\left( {A;E}\right) \) where \( p : E \rightarrow  X \) becomes a bundle of groups over \( A \) by restriction to \( {p}^{-1}\left( A\right) \) . Associated to the pair \( \left( {X, A}\right) \) there is then a long exact sequence of homology groups with local coefficients in the bundle \( E \) . The excision property is proved just as for ordinary homology, via iterated barycentric subdivision. The final axiom for homology, involving disjoint unions, extends trivially to homology with local coefficients. Simplicial and cellular homology also extend without difficulty to the case of local coefficients, as do the proofs that these forms of homology agree with singular homology for \( \Delta \) -complexes and CW complexes, respectively. We leave the verifications of all these statements to the energetic reader.

Now we turn to cohomology. One might try defining \( {H}^{n}\left( {X;E}\right) \) by simply dualizing, taking \( \operatorname{Hom}\left( {{C}_{n}\left( X\right) , E}\right) \) , but this makes no sense since \( E \) is not a group. Instead, the cochain group \( {C}^{n}\left( {X;E}\right) \) is defined to consist of all functions \( \varphi \) assigning to each singular simplex \( \sigma  : {\Delta }^{n} \rightarrow  X \) a lift \( \varphi \left( \sigma \right)  : {\Delta }^{n} \rightarrow  E \) . In case \( E \) is the product \( X \times  G \) , this amounts to assigning an element of \( G \) to each \( \sigma \) , so this definition generalizes ordinary cohomology. Coboundary maps \( \delta  : {C}^{n}\left( {X;E}\right)  \rightarrow  {C}^{n + 1}\left( {X;E}\right) \) are defined just as with ordinary cohomology, and satisfy \( {\delta }^{2} = 0 \) , so we have cohomology groups \( {H}^{n}\left( {X;E}\right) \) , and in the relative case, \( {H}^{n}\left( {X, A;E}\right) \) , defined via relative cochains \( {C}^{n}\left( {X, A;E}\right)  = \operatorname{Ker}\left( {{C}^{n}\left( {X;E}\right)  \rightarrow  {C}^{n}\left( {A;E}\right) }\right) . \)

For a path-connected space \( X \) with universal cover \( \widetilde{X} \) and fundamental group \( \pi \) , we can identify \( {H}^{n}\left( {X;E}\right) \) with \( {H}^{n}\left( {X;G}\right) \) , cohomology with local coefficients in the \( \mathbb{Z}\left\lbrack  \pi \right\rbrack \) -module \( G \) corresponding to \( E \) , by identifying \( {C}^{n}\left( {X;E}\right) \) with \( {\operatorname{Hom}}_{\mathbb{Z}\left\lbrack  \pi \right\rbrack  }\left( {{C}_{n}\left( \widetilde{X}\right) , G}\right) \) in the following way. An element \( \varphi  \in  {C}^{n}\left( {X;E}\right) \) assigns to each \( \sigma  : {\Delta }^{n} \rightarrow  X \) a lift to \( E \) . Regarding \( E \) as the quotient of \( \widetilde{X} \times  G \) under the diagonal action of \( \pi \) , a lift of \( \sigma \) to \( E \) is the same as an orbit of a lift to \( \widetilde{X} \times  G \) . Such an orbit is a function \( f \) assigning to each lift \( \widetilde{\sigma } : {\Delta }^{n} \rightarrow  \widetilde{X} \) an element \( f\left( \widetilde{\sigma }\right)  \in  G \) such that \( f\left( {\gamma \widetilde{\sigma }}\right)  = {\gamma f}\left( \widetilde{\sigma }\right) \) for all \( \gamma  \in  \pi \) , that is, an element of \( {\operatorname{Hom}}_{\mathbb{Z}\left\lbrack  \pi \right\rbrack  }\left( {{C}_{n}\left( \widetilde{X}\right) , G}\right) \) .

The basic properties of ordinary cohomology in \( \text{ § }{3.1} \) extend without great difficulty to cohomology groups with local coefficients. In order to define the map \( {f}^{ * } : {H}^{n}\left( {{X}^{\prime };{E}^{\prime }}\right)  \rightarrow  {H}^{n}\left( {X;E}\right) \) induced by a bundle map as before, it suffices to observe that a singular simplex \( \sigma  : {\Delta }^{n} \rightarrow  X \) and a lift \( {\widetilde{\sigma }}^{\prime } : {\Delta }^{n} \rightarrow  {E}^{\prime } \) of \( {f\sigma } \) define a lift \( \widetilde{\sigma } = \; \left( {\sigma ,{\widetilde{\sigma }}^{\prime }}\right)  : {\Delta }^{n} \rightarrow  {f}^{ * }\left( E\right) \) of \( \sigma \) . To show that \( f \simeq  g \) implies \( {f}^{ * } = {g}^{ * } \) requires some modification of the proof of the corresponding result for ordinary cohomology in §3.1, which proceeded by dualizing the proof for homology. In the local coefficient case one constructs a chain homotopy \( {P}^{ * } \) satisfying \( {g}^{\sharp } - {f}^{\sharp } = {P}^{ * }\delta  + \delta {P}^{ * } \) directly from the subdivision of \( {\Delta }^{n} \times  I \) used in the proof of the homology result. Similar remarks apply to proving excision and Mayer-Vietoris sequences for cohomology with local coefficients. To prove the equivalence of simplicial and cellular cohomology with singular cohomology in the local coefficient context, one should use the telescope argument from the proof of Lemma 2.34 to show that \( {H}^{n}\left( {{X}^{k};E}\right)  \approx  {H}^{n}\left( {X;E}\right) \) for \( k > n \) . Once again details will be left to the reader.

The difference between homology with local coefficients and cohomology with local coefficients is illuminated by comparing the following proposition with our earlier identification of \( {H}_{ * }\left( {X;\mathbb{Z}\left\lbrack  {{\pi }_{1}X}\right\rbrack  }\right) \) with the ordinary homology of the universal cover of \( X \) .

Proposition 3H.5. If \( X \) is a finite CW complex with universal cover \( \widetilde{X} \) and fundamental group \( \pi \) , then for all \( n,{H}^{n}\left( {X;\mathbb{Z}\left\lbrack  \pi \right\rbrack  }\right) \) is isomorphic to \( {H}_{c}^{n}\left( {\widetilde{X};\mathbb{Z}}\right) \) , cohomology of \( \widetilde{X} \) with compact supports and ordinary integer coefficients.

For example, consider the \( n \) -dimensional torus \( {T}^{n} \) , the product of \( n \) circles, with fundamental group \( \pi  = {\mathbb{Z}}^{n} \) and universal cover \( {\mathbb{R}}^{n} \) . We have \( {H}_{i}\left( {{T}^{n};\mathbb{Z}\left\lbrack  \pi \right\rbrack  }\right)  \approx  {H}_{i}\left( {\mathbb{R}}^{n}\right) \) , which is zero except for a \( \mathbb{Z} \) in dimension 0, but \( {H}^{i}\left( {{T}^{n};\mathbb{Z}\left\lbrack  \pi \right\rbrack  }\right)  \approx  {H}_{c}^{i}\left( {\mathbb{R}}^{n}\right) \) vanishes except for a \( \mathbb{Z} \) in dimension \( n \) , as we saw in Example 3.34.

To prove the proposition we shall use a few general facts about cohomology with compact supports. One significant difference between ordinary cohomology and cohomology with compact supports is in induced maps. A map \( f : X \rightarrow  Y \) induces \( {f}^{\sharp } : {C}_{c}^{n}\left( {Y;G}\right)  \rightarrow  {C}_{c}^{n}\left( {X;G}\right) \) and hence \( {f}^{ * } : {H}_{c}^{n}\left( {Y;G}\right)  \rightarrow  {H}_{c}^{n}\left( {X;G}\right) \) provided that \( f \) is proper: The preimage \( {f}^{-1}\left( K\right) \) of each compact set \( K \) in \( Y \) is compact in \( X \) . Thus if \( \varphi  \in  {C}^{n}\left( {Y;G}\right) \) vanishes on chains in \( Y - K \) then \( {f}^{\sharp }\left( \varphi \right)  \in  {C}^{n}\left( {X;G}\right) \) vanishes on chains in \( X - {f}^{-1}\left( K\right) \) . Further, to guarantee that \( f \simeq  g \) implies \( {f}^{ * } = {g}^{ * } \) we should restrict attention to homotopies that are proper as maps \( X \times  I \rightarrow  Y \) . Relative groups \( {H}_{c}^{n}\left( {X, A;G}\right) \) are defined when \( A \) is a closed subset of \( X \) , which guarantees that the inclusion \( A \hookrightarrow  X \) is a proper map. With these constraints the basic theory of \( \text{ § }{3.1} \) translates without difficulty to cohomology with compact supports.

In particular, for a locally compact CW complex \( X \) one can compute \( {H}_{c}^{ * }\left( {X;G}\right) \) using finite cellular cochains, the cellular cochains vanishing on all but finitely many cells. Namely, to compute \( {H}_{c}^{n}\left( {{X}^{n},{X}^{n - 1};G}\right) \) using excision one first has to identify this group with \( {H}_{c}^{n}\left( {{X}^{n}, N\left( {X}^{n - 1}\right) ;G}\right) \) where \( N\left( {X}^{n - 1}\right) \) is a closed neighborhood of \( {X}^{n - 1} \) in \( {X}^{n} \) obtained by deleting an open \( n \) -disk from the interior of each \( n \) -cell. If \( X \) is locally compact, the obvious deformation retraction of \( N\left( {X}^{n - 1}\right) \) onto \( {X}^{n - 1} \) is a proper homotopy equivalence. Hence via long exact sequences and the five-lemma we obtain isomorphisms \( {H}_{c}^{n}\left( {{X}^{n},{X}^{n - 1};G}\right)  \approx  {H}_{c}^{n}\left( {{X}^{n}, N\left( {X}^{n - 1}\right) ;G}\right) \) , and by excision the latter group can be identified with the finite cochains.

Proof of 3H.5: As noted above, we can compute \( {H}_{c}^{ * }\left( {\widetilde{X};\mathbb{Z}}\right) \) using the groups \( {C}_{f}^{n}\left( {\widetilde{X};\mathbb{Z}}\right) \) of finite cellular cochains \( \varphi  : {C}_{n} \rightarrow  \mathbb{Z} \) , where \( {C}_{n} = {H}_{n}\left( {{\widetilde{X}}^{n},{\widetilde{X}}^{n - 1}}\right) \) . Giving \( \widetilde{X} \) the CW structure lifting the CW structure on \( X \) , then since \( X \) is compact, finite cellular cochains are exactly homomorphisms \( \varphi  : {C}_{n} \rightarrow  \mathbb{Z} \) such that for each cell \( {e}^{n} \) of \( \widetilde{X} \) , \( \varphi \left( {\gamma {e}^{n}}\right) \) is nonzero for only finitely many covering transformations \( \gamma  \in  \pi \) . Such a \( \varphi \) determines a map \( \widehat{\varphi } : {C}_{n} \rightarrow  \mathbb{Z}\left\lbrack  \pi \right\rbrack \) by setting \( \widehat{\varphi }\left( {e}^{n}\right)  = \mathop{\sum }\limits_{y}\varphi \left( {{\gamma }^{-1}{e}^{n}}\right) \gamma \) . The map \( \widehat{\varphi } \) is a \( \mathbb{Z}\left\lbrack  \pi \right\rbrack \) -homomorphism since if we replace the summation index \( \gamma \) in the right side of \( \varphi \left( {\eta {e}^{n}}\right)  = \mathop{\sum }\limits_{y}\varphi \left( {{\gamma }^{-1}\eta {e}^{n}}\right) \gamma \) by \( {\eta \gamma } \) , we get \( \mathop{\sum }\limits_{y}\varphi \left( {{\gamma }^{-1}{e}^{n}}\right) {\eta \gamma } \) . The function \( \varphi  \mapsto  \widehat{\varphi } \) defines a homomorphism \( {C}_{f}^{n}\left( {\widetilde{X};\mathbb{Z}}\right)  \rightarrow  {\operatorname{Hom}}_{\mathbb{Z}\left\lbrack  \pi \right\rbrack  }\left( {{C}_{n},\mathbb{Z}\left\lbrack  \pi \right\rbrack  }\right) \) which is injective since \( \varphi \) is recoverable from \( \widehat{\varphi } \) as the coefficient of \( \gamma  = 1 \) . Furthermore, this homomorphism is surjective since a \( \mathbb{Z}\left\lbrack  \pi \right\rbrack \) -homomorphism \( \psi  : M \rightarrow  \mathbb{Z}\left\lbrack  \pi \right\rbrack \) has the form \( \psi \left( x\right)  = \mathop{\sum }\limits_{y}{\psi }_{y}\left( x\right) \gamma \) with \( {\psi }_{\gamma } \in  {\operatorname{Hom}}_{\mathbb{Z}}\left( {M,\mathbb{Z}}\right) \) satisfying \( {\psi }_{\gamma }\left( x\right)  = {\psi }_{1}\left( {{\gamma }^{-1}x}\right) \) , so \( {\psi }_{1} \) determines \( \psi \) . The isomorphisms \( {C}_{f}^{n}\left( {\widetilde{X};\mathbb{Z}}\right)  \approx  {\operatorname{Hom}}_{\mathbb{Z}\left\lbrack  \pi \right\rbrack  }\left( {{C}_{n},\mathbb{Z}\left\lbrack  \pi \right\rbrack  }\right) \) are isomorphisms of cochain complexes, so the respective cohomology groups \( {H}_{c}^{n}\left( {\widetilde{X};\mathbb{Z}}\right) \) and \( {H}^{n}\left( {X;\mathbb{Z}\left\lbrack  \pi \right\rbrack  }\right) \) are isomorphic.

Cup and cap product work easily with local coefficients in a bundle of rings, the latter concept being defined in the obvious way. The cap product can be used to give a version of Poincaré duality for a closed \( n \) -manifold \( M \) using coefficients in a bundle of rings \( E \) under the same assumption as with ordinary coefficients that there exists a fundamental class \( \left\lbrack  M\right\rbrack   \in  {H}_{n}\left( {M;E}\right) \) restricting to a generator of \( {H}_{n}\left( {M, M-\{ x\} ;E}\right) \) for all \( x \in  M \) . By excision the latter group is isomorphic to the fiber ring \( R \) of \( E \) . The same proof as for ordinary coefficients then shows that \( \left\lbrack  M\right\rbrack   \frown   : {H}^{k}\left( {M;E}\right)  \rightarrow  {H}_{n - k}\left( {M;E}\right) \) is an isomorphism for all \( k \) .

Taking \( R \) to be one of the standard rings \( \mathbb{Z},\mathbb{Q} \) , or \( {\mathbb{Z}}_{p} \) does not give anything new since the only ring automorphism these rings have is the identity, so the bundle of rings \( E \) must be the product \( M \times  R \) . To get something more interesting, suppose we take \( R \) to be the ring \( \mathbb{Z}\left\lbrack  i\right\rbrack \) of Gaussian integers, the complex numbers \( a + {bi} \) with \( a, b \in  \mathbb{Z} \) . This has complex conjugation \( a + {bi} \mapsto  a - {bi} \) as a ring isomorphism. If \( M \) is nonorientable and connected we can use the homomorphism \( \omega  : {\pi }_{1}\left( M\right)  \rightarrow  \{  \pm  1\} \) that defines the bundle of groups \( {M}_{\mathbb{Z}} \) to build a bundle of rings \( E \) corresponding to the action of \( {\pi }_{1}\left( M\right) \) on \( \mathbb{Z}\left\lbrack  i\right\rbrack \) given by \( y\left( {a + {bi}}\right)  = a + \omega \left( y\right) {bi} \) . The homology and cohomology groups of \( M \) with coefficients in \( E \) depend only on the additive structure of \( \mathbb{Z}\left\lbrack  i\right\rbrack \) so they split as the direct sum of their real and imaginary parts, which are just the homology or cohomology groups with ordinary coefficients \( \mathbb{Z} \) and twisted coefficients \( \widetilde{\mathbb{Z}} \) , respectively. The fundamental class in \( {H}_{n}\left( {M;\widetilde{\mathbb{Z}}}\right) \) constructed in Example 3H.3 can be viewed as a pure imaginary fundamental class \( \left\lbrack  M\right\rbrack   \in  {H}_{n}\left( {M;E}\right) \) . Since cap product with \( \left\lbrack  M\right\rbrack \) interchanges real and imaginary parts, we obtain:

Theorem 3H.6. If \( M \) is a nonorientable closed connected \( n \) -manifold then cap product with the pure imaginary fundamental class \( \left\lbrack  M\right\rbrack \) gives isomorphisms \( {H}^{k}\left( {M;\mathbb{Z}}\right)  \approx \; {H}_{n - k}\left( {M;\widetilde{\mathbb{Z}}}\right) \) and \( {H}^{k}\left( {M;\widetilde{\mathbb{Z}}}\right)  \approx  {H}_{n - k}\left( {M;\mathbb{Z}}\right) \) .

More generally this holds with \( \mathbb{Z} \) replaced by other rings such as \( \mathbb{Q} \) or \( {\mathbb{Z}}_{p} \) . There is also a version for noncompact manifolds using cohomology with compact supports.
