# 14.3 Complex Grassmann Manifolds

Still continuing our complex analogue of real vector bundle theory, we define the complex Grassmann manifold \( {\operatorname{Gr}}_{n}\left( {\mathbb{C}}^{n + k}\right) \) to be the set of all complex \( n \) -planes through the origin in the complex vector space \( {\mathbb{C}}^{n + k} \) . Just as in the real case, this set has a natural structure as smooth manifold. In fact \( {\mathrm{{Gr}}}_{n}\left( {\mathbb{C}}^{n + k}\right) \) has a natural structure as complex analytic manifold of complex dimension \( {nk} \) . Furthermore there is a canonical complex \( n \) -plane bundle which we denote by \( {\gamma }^{n} = {\gamma }^{n}\left( {\mathbb{C}}^{n + k}\right) \) over \( {\operatorname{Gr}}_{n}\left( {\mathbb{C}}^{n + k}\right) \) . By definition, the total space of \( {\gamma }^{n} \) consists of all pairs \( \left( {X, v}\right) \) where \( X \) is a complex \( n \) -plane through the origin in \( {\mathbb{C}}^{n + k} \) and \( v \) is a vector in \( X \) .

As an example, let us study the special case \( n = 1 \) . The Grassmann manifold \( {\operatorname{Gr}}_{1}\left( {\mathbb{C}}^{k + 1}\right) \) is also known as the complex projective space \( {\mathbb{P}}^{k}\left( \mathbb{C}\right) \) . We will investigate the cohomology ring \( {\mathrm{H}}^{ * }\left( {{\mathbb{P}}^{k}\left( \mathbb{C}\right) ;\mathbb{Z}}\right) \) . (Compare Problem 12-C)

Applying the Gysin sequence to the canonical line bundle \( {\gamma }^{1} = {\gamma }^{1}\left( {\mathbb{C}}^{k + 1}\right) \) over \( {\mathbb{P}}^{k}\left( \mathbb{C}\right) \) , and using the fact that \( {\mathrm{c}}_{1}\left( {\gamma }^{1}\right)  = \mathrm{e}\left( {\gamma }_{\mathbb{R}}^{1}\right) \) , we have

\[
\ldots  \rightarrow  {\mathrm{H}}^{i + 1}\left( {E}_{0}\right)  \rightarrow  {\mathrm{H}}^{i}\left( {{\mathbb{P}}^{k}\left( \mathbb{C}\right) }\right) \overset{ \sim  {\mathrm{c}}_{1}}{ \rightarrow  }{\mathrm{H}}^{i + 2}\left( {{\mathbb{P}}^{k}\left( \mathbb{C}\right) }\right) \overset{{\pi }_{0}^{ * }}{ \rightarrow  }{\mathrm{H}}^{i + 2}\left( {E}_{0}\right)  \rightarrow  \ldots
\]

using integer coefficients. The space \( {E}_{0} = {E}_{0}\left( {{\gamma }^{1}\left( {\mathbb{C}}^{k + 1}\right) }\right) \) is the set of all pairs

(line through the origin in \( {\mathbb{C}}^{k + 1} \) , non-zero vector in that line)

This can be identified with \( {\mathbb{C}}^{k + 1} \smallsetminus  \{ 0\} \) , and hence has the same homotopy type as the unit sphere \( {S}^{{2k} + 1} \) . Thus our Gysin sequence reduces to

\[
0 \rightarrow  {\mathrm{H}}^{i}\left( {{\mathbb{P}}^{k}\left( \mathbb{C}\right) }\right) \xrightarrow[]{ \smile  {\mathrm{c}}_{1}}{\mathrm{H}}^{i + 2}\left( {{\mathbb{P}}^{k}\left( \mathbb{C}\right) }\right)  \rightarrow  0
\]

for \( 0 \leq  i \leq  {2k} - 2 \) . Hence

\[
{\mathrm{H}}^{0}\left( {{\mathbb{P}}^{k}\left( \mathbb{C}\right) }\right)  \cong  {\mathrm{H}}^{2}\left( {{\mathbb{P}}^{k}\left( \mathbb{C}\right) }\right)  \cong  \ldots  \cong  {\mathrm{H}}^{2k}\left( {{\mathbb{P}}^{k}\left( \mathbb{C}\right) }\right) .
\]

Since \( {\mathbb{P}}^{k}\left( \mathbb{C}\right) \) is clearly connected, it follows that each \( {\mathrm{H}}^{2i}\left( {{\mathbb{P}}^{k}\left( \mathbb{C}\right) }\right) \) is infinite cyclic generated \( {\mathrm{c}}_{1}{\left( {\gamma }^{1}\right) }^{i} \) for \( i \leq  k \) . Similarly

\[
{\mathrm{H}}^{1}\left( {{\mathbb{P}}^{k}\left( \mathbb{C}\right) }\right)  \cong  {\mathrm{H}}^{3}\left( {{\mathbb{P}}^{k}\left( \mathbb{C}\right) }\right)  \cong  \ldots  \cong  {\mathrm{H}}^{{2k} - 1}\left( {{\mathbb{P}}^{k}\left( \mathbb{C}\right) }\right)
\]

and using the portion

\[
\cdots  \rightarrow  {\mathrm{H}}^{-1}\left( {{\mathbb{P}}^{k}\left( \mathbb{C}\right) }\right)  \rightarrow  {\mathrm{H}}^{1}\left( {{\mathbb{P}}^{k}\left( \mathbb{C}\right) }\right)  \rightarrow  {\mathrm{H}}^{1}\left( {E}_{0}\right)  \rightarrow  \ldots
\]

of the Gysin sequence, we see that these odd-dimensional groups are all zero. That is:

Theorem 14.4. The cohomology ring \( {\mathrm{H}}^{ * }\left( {{\mathbb{P}}^{k}\left( \mathbb{C}\right) ;\mathbb{Z}}\right) \) is a truncated polynomial ring terminating in dimension \( {2k} \) , and generated by the Chern class \( {\mathrm{c}}_{1}\left( {{\gamma }^{1}\left( {\mathbb{C}}^{k + 1}\right) }\right) \) .

Now let us let \( k \) tend to infinity. The canonical \( n \) -plane bundle \( {\gamma }^{n}\left( {\mathbb{C}}^{\infty }\right) \) over \( {\mathrm{{Gr}}}_{n}\left( {\mathbb{C}}^{\infty }\right) \) will be denoted briefly by \( {\gamma }^{n} \) . Using 14.4, it follows that \( {\mathrm{H}}^{ * }\left( {{\mathrm{{Gr}}}_{1}\left( {\mathbb{C}}^{\infty }\right) }\right) \) is the polynomial ring generated by \( {\mathrm{c}}_{1}\left( {\gamma }^{1}\right) \) . More generally we will show the following.

Theorem 14.5. The cohomology ring \( {\mathrm{H}}^{ * }\left( {{\mathrm{{Gr}}}_{n}\left( {\mathbb{C}}^{\infty }\right) ;\mathbb{Z}}\right) \) is the polynomial ring over \( \mathbb{Z} \) generated by the Chern classes \( {\mathrm{c}}_{1}\left( {\gamma }^{n}\right) ,\ldots ,{\mathrm{c}}_{n}\left( {\gamma }^{n}\right) \) . There are no polynomial relations between these \( n \) generators.

Proof by induction on \( n \) . We may assume that \( n \geq  2 \) , since the Theorem has already been established for \( n = 1 \) . Consider the Gysin sequence

\[
\cdots  \rightarrow  {\mathrm{H}}^{i}\left( {\mathrm{{Gr}}}_{n}\right) \xrightarrow[]{ \sim  {\mathrm{c}}_{n}}{\mathrm{H}}^{i + {2n}}\left( {\mathrm{{Gr}}}_{n}\right) \xrightarrow[]{{\pi }_{0}^{ * }}{\mathrm{H}}^{i + {2n}}\left( {E}_{0}\right)  \rightarrow  {\mathrm{H}}^{i + 1}\left( {\mathrm{{Gr}}}_{n}\right)  \rightarrow  \ldots
\]

associated with the bundle \( {\gamma }^{n} \) , using integer coefficients.

We will first show that the cohomology ring \( {\mathrm{H}}^{ * }\left( {E}_{0}\right) \) can be identified with \( {\mathrm{H}}^{ * }\left( {\mathrm{{Gr}}}_{n - 1}\right) \) . In fact a canonical map \( f : {E}_{0} \rightarrow  {\mathrm{{Gr}}}_{n - 1} \) is constructed as follows. By definition, a point \( \left( {X, v}\right) \) in \( {E}_{0} \) consists of an \( n \) -plane \( X \) in \( {\mathbb{C}}^{\infty } \) together with a non-zero vector \( v \) in \( X \) . Let \( f\left( {X, v}\right)  = X \cap  {v}^{ \bot  } \) be the orthogonal complement of \( v \) in \( X \) , using the standard Hermitian metric

\[
\left\langle  {\left( {{v}_{1},{v}_{2},\ldots }\right) ,\left( {{w}_{1},{w}_{2},\ldots }\right) }\right\rangle   = \sum {v}_{j}{\bar{w}}_{j}
\]

on \( {\mathbb{C}}^{\infty } \) . Then \( f\left( {X, v}\right) \) is a well defined \( \left( {n - 1}\right) \) -plane in \( {\mathbb{C}}^{\infty } \) .

In order to show that \( f \) induces cohomology isomorphisms, it is convenient to pass to the sub-bundle \( {\gamma }^{n}\left( {\mathbb{C}}^{N}\right)  \subset  {\gamma }^{n} \) , consisting of complex n-planes in \( N \) - space where \( N \) is large but finite. Let \( {f}_{N} : {E}_{0}\left( {{\gamma }^{n}\left( {\mathbb{C}}^{N}\right) }\right)  \rightarrow  {\mathrm{{Gr}}}_{n - 1}\left( {\mathbb{C}}^{N}\right) \) be the corresponding restriction of \( f \) . For any \( \left( {n - 1}\right) \) -plane \( Y \) in \( {\operatorname{Gr}}_{n - 1}\left( {\mathbb{C}}^{N}\right) \) it is evident that the inverse image

\[
{f}_{N}^{-1}\left( Y\right)  \subset  {E}_{0}\left( {{\gamma }^{n}\left( {\mathbb{C}}^{N}\right) }\right)
\]

consists of all pairs \( \left( {X, v}\right) \) where \( v \in  {\mathbb{C}}^{N} \) is a non-zero vector perpendicular to \( Y \) , and where \( X = Y + \mathbb{C}v \) is determined by \( v \) and \( Y \) . Thus \( {f}_{N} \) can be identified with the projection map

\[
{E}_{0}\left( {\omega }^{N - n + 1}\right)  \rightarrow  {\mathrm{{Gr}}}_{n - 1}\left( {\mathbb{C}}^{N}\right)
\]

where \( {\omega }^{N - n + 1} \) is the complex vector bundle whose fiber, over \( Y \in \; {\operatorname{Gr}}_{n - 1}\left( {\mathbb{C}}^{N}\right) \) , is the orthogonal complement of \( Y \) in \( {\mathbb{C}}^{N} \) .

Using the Gysin sequence of this new vector bundle, it follows that \( {f}_{N} \) induces cohomology isomorphisms in dimensions \( \leq  2\left( {N - n}\right) \) . Therefore, taking the direct limit as \( N \) tends to infinity, \( f \) induces cohomology isomorphisms in all dimensions.

Thus we can insert \( {\mathrm{{Gr}}}_{n - 1} \) in place of \( {E}_{0} \) in the Gysin sequence, obtaining a new exact sequence of the form

\[
\cdots  \rightarrow  {\mathrm{H}}^{i}\left( {\mathrm{{Gr}}}_{n}\right)  \rightarrow  {\mathrm{H}}^{i + {2n}}\left( {\mathrm{{Gr}}}_{n}\right) \overset{\lambda }{ \rightarrow  }{\mathrm{H}}^{i + {2n}}\left( {\mathrm{{Gr}}}_{n - 1}\right)  \rightarrow  {\mathrm{H}}^{i + 1}\left( {\mathrm{{Gr}}}_{n}\right)  \rightarrow  \ldots
\]

with \( \lambda  = {f}^{* - 1}{\pi }_{0}^{ * } \) .

We must show that this homomorphism \( \lambda  = {f}^{* - 1}{\pi }_{0}^{ * } \) maps the Chern class \( {\mathrm{c}}_{i}\left( {\gamma }^{n}\right) \) to \( {\mathrm{c}}_{i}\left( {\gamma }^{n - 1}\right) \) . This statement is clear for \( i = n \) , so we may assume that \( i < n \) . By the definition of Chern classes, the image \( {\pi }_{0}^{ * }{\mathrm{c}}_{i}\left( {\gamma }^{n}\right) \) is equal to \( {\mathrm{c}}_{i}\left( {\gamma }_{0}^{n}\right) \) . But \( f : {E}_{0} \rightarrow  {\mathrm{{Gr}}}_{n - 1} \) is clearly covered by a bundle map \( {\gamma }_{0}^{n} \mapsto  {\gamma }^{n - 1} \) . Therefore \( {f}^{ * }{\mathrm{c}}_{i}\left( {\gamma }^{n - 1}\right)  = {\mathrm{c}}_{i}\left( {\gamma }_{0}^{n}\right) \) by 14.2, and it follows that

\[
\lambda {\mathrm{c}}_{i}\left( {\gamma }^{n}\right)  = {f}^{* - 1}{\pi }_{0}^{ * }{\mathrm{c}}_{i}\left( {\gamma }^{n}\right)
\]

is equal to \( {\mathrm{c}}_{i}\left( {\gamma }^{n - 1}\right) \) as asserted.

Now let us apply the induction hypothesis. Since \( {\mathrm{H}}^{ * }\left( {\mathrm{{Gr}}}_{n - 1}\right) \) is generated by the Chern classes \( {\mathrm{c}}_{1}\left( {\gamma }^{n - 1}\right) ,\ldots ,{\mathrm{c}}_{n - 1}\left( {\gamma }^{n - 1}\right) \) , it follows that the homomorphism \( \lambda \) is surjective, so our sequence reduces to

\[
0 \rightarrow  {\mathrm{H}}^{i}\left( {\mathrm{{Gr}}}_{n}\right) \xrightarrow[]{ \sim  {\mathrm{c}}_{n}}{\mathrm{H}}^{i + {2n}}\left( {\mathrm{{Gr}}}_{n}\right) \overset{\lambda }{ \rightarrow  }{\mathrm{H}}^{i + {2n}}\left( {\mathrm{{Gr}}}_{n - 1}\right)  \rightarrow  0.
\]

Using this sequence, we will prove, by a subsidiary induction on \( i \) , that every element \( x \) of \( {\mathrm{H}}^{i + {2n}}\left( {\mathrm{{Gr}}}_{n}\right) \) can be expressed uniquely as a polynomial in the Chern classes \( {\mathrm{c}}_{1}\left( {\gamma }^{n}\right) ,\ldots ,{\mathrm{c}}_{n}\left( {\gamma }^{n}\right) \) . Certainly the image \( \lambda \left( x\right) \) can be expressed uniquely as a polynomial \( p\left( {{\mathrm{c}}_{1}\left( {\gamma }^{n - 1}\right) ,\ldots ,{\mathrm{c}}_{n - 1}\left( {\gamma }^{n - 1}\right) }\right) \) by our main induction hypothesis. Therefore the element \( x - p\left( {{\mathrm{c}}_{1}\left( {\gamma }^{n}\right) ,\ldots ,{\mathrm{c}}_{n - 1}\left( {\gamma }^{n}\right) }\right) \) belongs to the kernel of \( \lambda \) , and hence can be expressed as a product \( y{\mathrm{c}}_{n}\left( {\gamma }^{n}\right) \) for some uniquely determined \( y \in  {\mathrm{H}}^{i}\left( {\mathrm{{Gr}}}_{n}\right) \) . Now \( y \) can be expressed uniquely as a polynomial \( q\left( {{\mathrm{c}}_{1}\left( {\gamma }^{n}\right) ,\ldots ,{\mathrm{c}}_{n}\left( {\gamma }^{n}\right) }\right) \) by our subsidiary induction hypothesis, hence

\[
x = p\left( {{\mathrm{c}}_{1}\left( {\gamma }^{n}\right) ,\ldots ,{\mathrm{c}}_{n - 1}\left( {\gamma }^{n}\right) }\right)  + {\mathrm{c}}_{n}\left( {\gamma }^{n}\right) q\left( {{\mathrm{c}}_{1}\left( {\gamma }^{n}\right) ,\ldots ,{\mathrm{c}}_{n}\left( {\gamma }^{n}\right) }\right) .
\]

The polynomials on the right are unique, since if \( x \) were also equal to

\[
{p}^{\prime }\left( {{\mathrm{c}}_{1}\left( {\gamma }^{n}\right) ,\ldots ,{\mathrm{c}}_{n - 1}\left( {\gamma }^{n}\right) }\right)  + {\mathrm{c}}_{n}\left( {\gamma }^{n}\right) {q}^{\prime }\left( {{\mathrm{c}}_{1}\left( {\gamma }^{n}\right) ,\ldots ,{\mathrm{c}}_{n}\left( {\gamma }^{n}\right) }\right)
\]

then applying \( \lambda \) we would see that \( p = {p}^{\prime } \) , and dividing the difference by \( {\mathrm{c}}_{n}\left( {\gamma }^{n}\right) \) we would see that \( q = {q}^{\prime } \) .

Just as for real \( n \) -plane bundles (Theorem 5.6), we can prove:

Theorem 14.6. Every complex \( n \) -plane bundle over a paracompact base space possesses a bundle map into the canonical complex \( n \) -plane bundle \( {\gamma }^{n} = {\gamma }^{n}\left( {\mathbb{C}}^{\infty }\right) \) over \( {\mathrm{{Gr}}}_{n} = {\mathrm{{Gr}}}_{n}\left( {\mathbb{C}}^{\infty }\right) \) .

In other words every complex \( n \) -plane bundle over the paracompact base \( B \) is isomorphic to the induced bundle \( {f}^{ * }\left( {\gamma }^{n}\right) \) for some \( f : B \rightarrow  {\mathrm{{Gr}}}_{n} \) . In fact, just as in the real case, one can actually prove the sharper statement that two induced bundles \( {f}^{ * }\left( {\gamma }^{n}\right) \) and \( {g}^{ * }\left( {\gamma }^{n}\right) \) are isomorphic if and only if \( f \) is homotopic to \( g \) . For this reason the bundle \( {\gamma }^{n} = {\gamma }^{n}\left( {\mathbb{C}}^{\infty }\right) \) is called the universal complex \( n \) -plane bundle, and its base space \( {\mathrm{{Gr}}}_{n}\left( {\mathbb{C}}^{\infty }\right) \) is called the classifying space for complex \( n \) -plane bundles. [The notation \( \mathrm{{BU}}\left( n\right) \) is often used in the literature for this classifying space.]
